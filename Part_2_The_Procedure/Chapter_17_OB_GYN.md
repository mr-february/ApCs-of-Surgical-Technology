# Chapter 17: Obstetrics and Gynecology

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Apply the ApC framework to cesarean section and common gynecologic procedures.
2. Describe the specific draping, incision, and retractors for OB/GYN cases.
3. Identify the anatomical hazards unique to pelvic surgery.
4. Explain the scrub technologist's role in obstetric emergencies.

## 17.1 Reproductive Anatomy Review

**Uterus:** Fundus (top), body, cervix. Layers: endometrium, myometrium, serosa.

**Adnexa:** Fallopian tubes (fimbriated ends capture oocytes) + ovaries (bilateral; produce hormones and ova).

**Critical relationship:** The **ureter passes under the uterine artery** ("water under the bridge") at the lateral cervix. Ureteral injury is the most feared complication of hysterectomy — it happens when the ureter is not identified before the uterine vessels are clamped.

## 17.2 Cesarean Section (C-Section)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine with **left lateral tilt** (a wedge or folded blanket placed under the right hip tilts the uterus off the IVC; prevents supine hypotensive syndrome in the third-trimester patient).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol or betadine (betadine preferred by some for the perineal and vaginal area if visible); prep from the xiphoid to the upper thighs, full width; include the pubic area.

**Draping:**
- Ether screen (a vertical drape bar) placed across the patient's chest to separate the operative field from the anesthesia provider and the patient's view
- Four draping towels framing the lower abdominal incision (Pfannenstiel)
- Fenestrated laparotomy sheet placed over the patient
- A separate sterile "receiving" towel or warm blanket is placed on the lower end of the sterile field to receive the infant — **the infant is NOT a sterile item**; it should be wrapped and passed to the neonatal team immediately

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:**
- **Skin incision:** #10 blade on a #3 handle; **Pfannenstiel (low transverse)** incision approximately 2 cm above the pubic symphysis, extending 10–12 cm. The incision curves slightly upward at the lateral ends (a smile incision).
- **Fascial incision:** same #10 blade used to score the anterior rectus fascia transversely in the midline; the fascia is then extended laterally with Mayo scissors or electrocautery.
- **Uterine incision:** after the bladder is retracted downward, a **#10 blade** makes the initial stab incision into the lower uterine segment; **bandage scissors** are used to extend the uterine incision laterally in a curved (upward) direction. A controlled, deliberate incision avoids fetal injury.

**Retractors:**
- **Doyen retractor (bladder blade):** a large, curved-blade retractor that retracts the bladder inferiorly to expose the lower uterine segment; this is the key retractor of the C-section approach
- An assistant holds the Doyen retractor downward throughout the bladder flap development and uterine incision
- Additional Army-Navy retractors for skin and fascial retraction when needed

---

### The Procedure

1. Skin incision; divide subcutaneous fat; open anterior rectus fascia
2. Separate rectus muscles vertically (bluntly with fingers)
3. Open peritoneum sharply; extend opening vertically toward the bladder reflection
4. **Bladder flap:** incise and dissect the peritoneum overlying the lower uterine segment; retract bladder inferiorly with the Doyen retractor — expose the smooth lower uterine segment
5. **Uterine incision:** stab incision; extend with bandage scissors
6. **Delivery of the infant:** surgeon's gloved hand enters and guides the fetal presenting part (head or breech) into the uterine incision; the circulator applies fundal pressure (Kristeller maneuver) to assist delivery; deliver head → clear the mouth and nose → deliver body
7. **Cord clamped and cut:** two clamps placed; divided between; infant passed to neonatal team
8. **Placenta:** spontaneously delivered or manually extracted after oxytocin is given (circulator notifies anesthesia to give oxytocin at cord clamping); examine for completeness
9. Close the uterine incision: 0 or #1 Vicryl continuous, one or two layers

---

### The Closure

**Uterine incision:** #1 Vicryl running (two-layer closure: inner continuous lock + outer imbricating Lembert stitch, or single running depending on surgeon preference).

**Peritoneum:** May or may not be closed; if closed, 3-0 Vicryl running.

**Rectus fascia:** 0 Vicryl running (looped PDS or two strands from each end, meeting in the middle).

**Subcutaneous fat:** 3-0 Vicryl if ≥2 cm depth (reduces seroma risk).

**Skin:** Staples; or 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular + Steri-strips.

**Dressing:** Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad; secured with tape (or transparent film dressing).

## 17.3 Total Abdominal Hysterectomy (TAH)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; Foley catheter inserted. Low lithotomy may be added if vaginal access is needed (combined approach).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep from umbilicus to mid-thigh, including the perineum and vagina (betadine for vaginal prep).

**Draping:**
- Four towels framing the lower abdominal incision (Pfannenstiel or midline)
- Laparotomy sheet
- Stirrups and perineal drape if lithotomy position is used

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:**
- **Pfannenstiel approach:** same as C-section — low transverse skin incision, transverse fascial incision
- **Midline approach:** #10 blade; midline incision from umbilicus to pubis (for large uteri, malignancy, or limited pelvic access)

**Retractors:**
- **O'Sullivan-O'Connor retractor** (ringed self-retaining retractor with interchangeable blades): the standard retractor for abdominal pelvic surgery; provides lateral, inferior (bladder blade), and superior exposure; placed after peritoneal entry
- **Deaver retractors** (hand-held) used to expose specific pelvic structures as needed

---

### The Procedure

1. Enter peritoneum; systematic pelvic exploration
2. Round ligaments: clamped, cut, ligated bilaterally (Heaney clamp, Mayo scissors, 0 Vicryl tie)
3. Broad ligament opened; ureter identified and protected
4. Infundibulopelvic (IP) ligaments (if BSO is performed): clamped, cut, ligated; or if ovaries preserved, utero-ovarian ligaments clamped/cut/ligated
5. Uterine vessels: bladder reflected downward; uterine vessels clamped at the cervix bilaterally (three Heaney clamps — two on patient's side, one on specimen); cut; suture-ligated
6. Cardinal and uterosacral ligaments: clamped, cut, ligated sequentially
7. Incise the vagina circumferentially around the cervix; remove uterus and cervix
8. Vaginal cuff closure: running 0 Vicryl locking stitch; or interrupted figure-of-eight sutures

**Scrub tech:** Heaney clamps are passed repeatedly — keep several loaded and ready. Alert the surgeon if the uterine clamps slip — this indicates a risk of ureteral injury.

---

### The Closure

**Peritoneum:** May be left open; or 3-0 Vicryl running.

**Fascia:** 0 Vicryl or PDS running.

**Subcutaneous:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** Staples or 4-0 Monocryl.

**Drain:** Rarely needed; JP drain placed in the pelvis if significant dissection or hemostasis concern.

**Dressing:** Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad.

## 17.4 Laparoscopic Hysterectomy (TLH)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine → modified lithotomy (legs in Allen stirrups at low lithotomy) after abdominal access is established; steep Trendelenburg (gravity displaces bowel out of the pelvis).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol abdomen; betadine vaginal prep. A uterine manipulator is placed vaginally before the abdominal approach begins.

**Draping:**
- Laparoscopic draping of the abdomen (fenestrated laparoscopic sheet over trocar sites)
- Perineal drape around the lithotomy setup with vaginal access incorporated

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #11 blade; umbilical port incision (10–12 mm); three additional 5 mm port incisions in the lower quadrants and suprapubic area.

**Retractors:** Trocars + pneumoperitoneum + Trendelenburg position = the "retractor" system. The uterine manipulator (e.g., RUMI system) allows the surgeon to antiflex/retroflex the uterus from below, providing dynamic retraction of the uterus to expose each vascular pedicle.

---

### The Procedure

Mirrors TAH steps, executed laparoscopically with energy devices (LigaSure, EnSeal, or bipolar + scissors) instead of clamp-cut-tie technique. The vaginal cuff is cut with laparoscopic scissors at the end; the uterus is delivered vaginally.

---

### The Closure

**Port sites ≥10 mm:** Fascial closure with 0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular.

**Vaginal cuff:** Sutured vaginally or laparoscopically with 0 Vicryl.

**Dressing:** Small adhesive dressings over port sites.

## 17.5 Dilation and Curettage (D&C)

### The Approach

**Position:** Lithotomy (Allen stirrups).

**Prep:** Betadine vaginal prep.

**Draping:** Lithotomy/perineal drape — splits around both legs; exposes the perineum and vagina.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** No formal incision — the approach is through the **natural orifice** (vagina/cervix). The cervix is accessed with a speculum and dilated with instruments.

**Retractors:**
- **Weighted speculum** (placed posteriorly in the vagina; its weight provides posterior retraction of the vaginal wall)
- **Anterior retractor** (thin Sims retractor placed anteriorly to retract the anterior vaginal wall)
- Together, these two retractors expose the cervix completely

---

### The Procedure

1. Bimanual examination under anesthesia; determine uterine size and position
2. Grasp the cervix with a **single-tooth tenaculum** (placed on the anterior lip)
3. Sound the uterus with a uterine sound; note the depth (normally 6–8 cm)
4. Dilate the cervix: **Hegar dilators** (graduated sizes, from 3 mm up to desired size); or Pratt dilators (tapered)
5. Curettage: pass the **sharp curette** into the uterine cavity; scrape the endometrial lining in a systematic pattern; collect tissue with the curette
6. Suction curettage (if indicated): electric or manual vacuum aspiration cannula passed into the cavity; suction applied; contents removed

---

### The Closure

No formal wound closure — the uterine cervix self-closes as the tenaculum is removed. No sutures. No dressing.

**Remove:** weighted speculum, retractors, tenaculum. Ensure hemostasis (cervical bleeding from the tenaculum site with pressure or silver nitrate if needed).

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "In pelvic surgery, your Approach determines whether the ureter is safe. Your retractors determine whether the surgeon can see it."

- In a C-section, the Doyen retractor is your most important tool. If it slips, the bladder rises into the field and risks being cut by the bandage scissors. Hold it steady.
- In hysterectomy, know when the uterine vessels have been secured — after that, the case moves quickly toward the vaginal cuff. Have your 0 Vicryl ready.
- For a D&C, the dilators are handed in sequence from small to large. Pre-arrange them in order on your Mayo stand before the case begins.
