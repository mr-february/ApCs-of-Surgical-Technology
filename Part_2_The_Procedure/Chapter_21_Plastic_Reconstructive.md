# Chapter 21: Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery

---

## The Reconstructive Ladder

Every wound management decision in plastic surgery starts at the simplest effective option and escalates as needed:

1. Primary closure → 2. Secondary intention → 3. Delayed primary → 4. Skin graft → 5. Local flap → 6. Regional flap → 7. Free flap

The rung selected determines the Approach. A skin graft case is a completely different setup from a free flap case. Know which rung you're on before you scrub.

**Why it matters at the back table:** Plastic surgery lives or dies on technique precision and sterility. A contaminated graft bed, a pedicle that gets crushed by a heavy retractor, or an implant that touched skin — these failures begin before the Procedure, in the Approach. Your setup is part of the outcome.

## Split-Thickness Skin Grafting (STSG)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I–II depending on recipient wound · Position: Varies with wound location · Avg time: 30–90 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Two sites: donor site + recipient site

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Dermatome: confirm type (Zimmer electric or equivalent); correct blade guard depth set per surgeon's specification (0.010"–0.030"); mineral oil for the donor site on the field
> - [ ] Mesh carrier (if meshing): confirm expansion ratio requested (1:1.5, 1:2, 1:3); assembled and ready before graft is harvested
> - [ ] Damp saline gauze on the back table: the harvested graft goes here dermis-down immediately; keep it moist at all times
> - [ ] Recipient bed preparation supplies: curette, electrocautery, and scissors for debridement
> - [ ] Graft fixation: staple gun loaded; or 4-0 chromic/plain gut on a small needle for edge suturing
> - [ ] Bolster or NPWT dressing: confirm which type the surgeon wants; have it prepared before the graft goes down
> - [ ] Donor site dressing: Adaptic or Xeroform + absorptive gauze + elastic bandage
> - [ ] Change outer gloves between harvesting from the donor site and handling the graft at the recipient site

**Position:** Determined by the wound location. Supine for lower extremity, trunk, face. Lateral or prone for back or posterior wounds.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the recipient wound and the donor site. These may be the same field (thigh wound + thigh donor) or separate fields.

**Draping:** Recipient wound and donor site within the same drape field if possible. Donor site most commonly the anterolateral thigh.

**The Knife (Donor Site):** The dermatome is the cutting tool — it shaves a thin layer of skin at a preset depth. Set the blade guard before the case starts per the surgeon's specification. The thigh is stretched taut and lubricated with mineral oil before the dermatome is applied.

**Retractors (Donor Site):** No conventional retractors. The assistant and scrub tech use flat boards or their palms to stretch the skin taut on both sides of the advancing dermatome. A tongue depressor or skin grafting board pressed against the leading edge of the dermatome creates a flat, even surface for a uniform graft.

### The Procedure

1. Mineral oil applied to the donor site; skin stretched taut
2. Dermatome applied firmly; powered on; harvested graft rolls onto the guard
3. Graft removed; placed dermis-down on a damp saline gauze
4. **Meshing (if indicated):** graft threaded through the mesh carrier; perforated pattern allows expansion, drainage, and conformation to irregular surfaces
5. Recipient wound bed prepared: excise non-viable tissue; ensure vascularity; control hemostasis completely
6. Apply graft dermis-down onto the recipient bed; trim to fit; secure with staples, suture, or combination
7. Complete immobile contact between graft and bed is required — hematoma, seroma, or shear movement = graft failure

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Graft desiccation:** if the harvested graft dries out before application, it will not take; keep it on a moist saline gauze and do not let it sit uncovered
> - **Dermatome depth error:** if the guard is set too deep, the donor site will not re-epithelialize on its own and will require a graft itself; confirm the depth setting with the surgeon before the first pass
> - **Hematoma under the graft:** even a small hematoma between the graft and the bed lifts the graft off the blood supply and kills it; achieve complete hemostasis in the recipient bed before the graft goes down; do not rush this step
> - **Staple misalignment:** staples that tent the graft edges upward lift the graft away from the bed at the margins; apply staples flat against the skin surface

### The Closure

**Donor site:** Non-adherent dressing (Adaptic, Xeroform, or Mepitel) + absorptive gauze + elastic bandage. The donor site heals by re-epithelialization — it is not sutured.

**Graft (recipient site):** Secured edges + bolster dressing (tie-over or NPWT) to maintain constant contact between the graft and the recipient bed.

## Z-Plasty (Local Flap)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Varies with scar location · Avg time: 30–60 min · Team: surgeon only or + 1 · Used for: scar contracture release, scar reorientation

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Surgical marking pen: surgeon will draw the Z design on the skin before incising — confirm the marker is on the field
> - [ ] #15 blade × 2: fine, precise cuts required; have a fresh blade available
> - [ ] Metzenbaum scissors (small, curved): for flap elevation in the subcutaneous plane
> - [ ] Fine skin hooks: for flap tip retraction without crushing
> - [ ] 5-0 nylon (face) or 4-0 nylon (body) for final skin closure; 4-0 Monocryl for buried subcutaneous sutures

**Position:** Determined by the scar location. Supine or sitting for facial and neck Z-plasties. Extremity positioning as appropriate.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol for extremities; betadine for face.

**Draping:** Small towel aperture drape for facial Z-plasties; extremity drapes for limb contractures.

**The Knife:** #15 blade. The Z design is drawn on the skin before incision: central limb along the scar; two diagonal limbs at 60° angles from each end in opposite directions. Three incisions define two triangular flaps.

**Retractors:** Fine skin hooks or Senn double-ended retractors to elevate and retract the triangular flap tips during dissection.

### The Procedure

1. Incise all three limbs of the Z with the #15 blade
2. Elevate both triangular flaps in the subcutaneous plane with Metzenbaum scissors
3. Transpose the flaps: each triangle is rotated 90° and sutured into the opposite limb's position
4. Result: scar reoriented 90° from its original direction; contracture tension released; total scar length increases

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Flap tip ischemia:** if the subcutaneous plane is elevated too thin (dermis only), the flap tip loses perfusion; maintain adequate subcutaneous depth under each flap
> - **Geometric distortion:** the 60° limb angles are precise; if the incisions are not drawn correctly before cutting, the transposition will not work; confirm the design is marked before handing the blade

### The Closure

| Layer | Suture | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Subcutaneous (dead space) | 4-0 Monocryl buried interrupted | Reduces skin tension |
| Skin | 5-0 nylon or Prolene (face) / 4-0 nylon (body) | Interrupted |

**Dressing:** Non-adherent + light gauze; or Steri-strips reinforcing the skin edges.

## Free Flap (Microvascular)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Varies by flap and defect · Avg time: 4–10 hrs · Team: Two simultaneous scrubbed teams (donor site + recipient site) · Microscope: required for anastomosis · Most complex procedure in plastic surgery

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Two complete instrument setups: one for flap harvest (donor team); one for recipient site and anastomosis (recipient team)
> - [ ] Microvascular instruments: Castroviejo needle drivers × 2; jewelers forceps × 2; micro scissors; vessel dilators; background material (blue or yellow) for anastomosis; vessel loops (Silastic)
> - [ ] Anastomosis sutures: 9-0 or 10-0 nylon on a BV-1 needle × multiple; keep them moist
> - [ ] Venous coupler system (if used): confirm the correct sizes; assembler on the field
> - [ ] Doppler probe: sterile for intraoperative confirmation of flap perfusion after anastomosis
> - [ ] Operating microscope sterile drape: drape before the anastomosis step
> - [ ] Flap-specific instruments: for DIEP flap — abdominal retractors, perforator dissection instruments; for fibula — bone saw, plates and screws for jaw reconstruction
> - [ ] Communication between teams: confirm with both surgeons that the recipient vessels are ready before the pedicle is divided at the donor site

**Position:** Varies by flap and defect. DIEP flap (abdominal to breast): supine, arms extended. Fibula free flap (jaw reconstruction): lateral, leg extended. Two simultaneous surgical fields.

**Prep:** Both donor and recipient sites prepped simultaneously by two teams.

**Draping:** Two separate sterile fields, or one large combined field encompassing both sites.

**The Knife:** #15 or #10 blade for recipient site incisions and vessel exposure. #15 blade for flap harvest at the donor site.

**Retractors:** Recipient vessels: fine vein retractors; Silastic vessel loops around the recipient artery and vein. Operating microscope replaces conventional retractors during the anastomosis — vessel loops hold the vessels in position; the microscope provides the access.

### The Procedure

1. **Donor site:** flap harvested with its pedicle (artery + vein dissected to maximum length); flap is NOT divided until recipient vessels are confirmed ready
2. **Recipient site:** defect prepared; recipient vessels identified, dissected free, and controlled with vessel loops
3. **Pedicle division:** when both teams are ready, the flap pedicle is divided; the flap is transferred to the recipient site; ischemia time begins
4. **Microvascular anastomosis:**
   - Arterial anastomosis first: 9-0 or 10-0 nylon interrupted; 6–10 sutures per anastomosis; under ×10–×25 magnification; Castroviejo needle drivers; jewelers forceps
   - Venous anastomosis: same technique; or venous coupler for faster, reliable vein-to-vein anastomosis
5. Vessel clamps removed; flow confirmed by Doppler; capillary refill and flap bleeding checked

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Ischemia time:** from pedicle division to anastomosis completion, every minute counts; the microvascular instruments must be immediately accessible; no searching during the anastomosis
> - **9-0/10-0 suture management:** at this scale, sutures are nearly invisible; keep them moist; never let them dry, kink, or tangle; keep the microscope field clean
> - **Anastomosis tension:** if the pedicle is too short and the vessels are under tension, the anastomosis will fail; alert the surgeon before dividing the pedicle if the distance looks marginal
> - **Compression on the flap:** after the anastomosis, no compressive dressing or retractor pressure on the flap; compression occludes the anastomosis and the flap dies; communicate this to the circulator when they are applying the dressing

### The Closure

**Donor site:** Primary closure (layered closure for DIEP; fibula leg closed in layers) or skin graft if primary closure is not possible.

**Recipient site:** Flap sutured to wound edges; drains placed under the flap; layered closure of remaining tissue planes.

**Dressing:** Non-compressive over the free flap. Window dressing (gauze with a clear window over the flap surface) allows regular monitoring of flap perfusion post-op.

## Mastectomy with Immediate Implant Reconstruction

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, operative arm on arm board · Avg time: 2–4 hrs · Team: surgeon + 1–2 assistants · Implants: tissue expander or direct-to-implant (DTI) with ADM · No-touch technique required

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Implant or tissue expander: confirm type and size with the surgeon before opening; keep it sealed until the pocket is ready
> - [ ] Triple antibiotic solution (gentamicin + bacitracin + polymyxin B in saline): prepared by the circulator; used to irrigate the implant pocket before implant placement
> - [ ] Change outer gloves before handling the implant — no exceptions; the no-touch technique prevents biofilm seeding
> - [ ] ADM (AlloDerm or equivalent): confirm size ordered; keep it moist in saline per manufacturer instructions until it is needed
> - [ ] JP drains × 2: one for the mastectomy flap space; one for the axilla; have 2-0 nylon for securing
> - [ ] Lighted retractor or fiberoptic headlight: essential for skin flap elevation; the thin flap plane is difficult to see with standard retractors alone

**Position:** Supine; operative arm extended on an arm board.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; entire chest from chin to umbilicus, including the axilla.

**Draping:** Four towels framing the chest → fenestrated mastectomy drape. Arm stockinette if the arm will be draped free; secured if not.

**The Knife:** #10 blade. Skin-sparing mastectomy: elliptical incision around the nipple-areola complex only; skin envelope preserved. Simple mastectomy: larger elliptical incision incorporating the entire nipple-areola complex.

**Retractors:** Army-Navy retractors initially. Skin flap elevation: Senn retractors, sharp Hohmann, or a lighted retractor to illuminate the subcutaneous plane. Axillary dissection (if performed): Richardson retractors and vein retractors to protect the axillary vein.

### The Procedure

1. Elliptical skin incision; elevate skin flaps in the subcutaneous plane (thin flaps preserve the skin envelope)
2. Divide the breast at the pectoralis major fascia; free the breast from the muscle; remove the specimen
3. **Sentinel lymph node biopsy** (if indicated): blue dye and/or gamma probe-guided identification; remove and send for frozen section
4. **Axillary node dissection** (if indicated): remove Level I/II axillary nodes
5. **Reconstruction:**
   - **Tissue expander:** deflated silicone shell placed under pectoralis major and serratus anterior; filled with saline intraoperatively; spacer until permanent exchange
   - **DTI (direct-to-implant):** permanent silicone gel implant placed; ADM sewn to the inferior pectoralis border to form the lower pole pocket
6. Irrigate the pocket with triple antibiotic solution; change outer gloves; introduce the implant with minimal handling

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Skin flap thinness:** if the flap elevation is too thin, the overlying skin loses its blood supply and will necrose over the implant — a catastrophic wound healing failure; the surgeon controls flap thickness, but do not create tension on the flap with retractors
> - **Implant contact with skin:** if the implant touches the skin before being placed in the irrigated pocket, change gloves and re-irrigate; biofilm seeding increases capsular contracture rates dramatically
> - **ADM handling:** ADM must be kept moist; dried ADM becomes stiff and is difficult to suture; keep it in saline until the moment it is needed
> - **Drain count:** two JP drains are standard; document their placement sites and confirm the count before closing

### The Closure

| Layer | Suture | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Pectoralis / ADM to chest wall | 2-0 or 3-0 Vicryl | Securing the inferior pocket |
| Subcutaneous | 3-0 Vicryl | |
| Skin | 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular + Steri-strips | |
| JP drains × 2 | 2-0 nylon | One in the mastectomy space; one in the axilla |

**Dressing:** Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad; surgical bra or binder for compression.

## Clinical Pearls

> "In plastic surgery, your Approach must protect the thing that heals. A contaminated graft bed, a crushed flap pedicle, or an implant that touched skin are failures that begin before the Procedure."

- Skin grafts fail without contact. Achieve complete hemostasis in the recipient bed before the graft goes down. A single hematoma pocket is enough to kill a graft.
- In free flap cases, change your gloves before handling any anastomosis suture. Contamination at the anastomosis leads to thrombosis and flap loss.
- The no-touch technique for implants is non-negotiable. Change gloves, irrigate the pocket, and minimize handling time. This is not about ritual — it is about biofilm.
- In microvascular anastomosis, silence and stillness at the field when the surgeon is suturing. The sutures are nearly invisible. Every distraction costs a suture.
