## 21.1 The Reconstructive Ladder

Every wound management decision in plastic surgery begins with the simplest effective option and escalates as needed:

1. Primary closure → 2. Secondary intention → 3. Delayed primary → 4. Skin graft → 5. Local flap → 6. Regional flap → 7. Free flap

The rung selected determines the **Approach** for that case. A skin graft Approach is entirely different from a free flap Approach. The framework is the same; the execution changes with the complexity.

---

## 21.2 Split-Thickness Skin Grafting (STSG)

### The Approach — Recipient Site

**Position:** Determined by the recipient wound location; varies widely. Supine for lower extremity, trunk, and face; lateral or prone for back or posterior wounds.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the wound and a generous surrounding area. Prep the donor site separately.

**Draping:** The recipient wound and the donor site may be on the same field (e.g., thigh wound + thigh donor) or separated by draping if on different body regions.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; change outer gloves between harvesting from the donor site and handling the graft.

**The Knife (Donor Site):**
- The **dermatome** is the "knife" for STSG harvest — it shaves a thin layer of skin at a preset depth (0.010"–0.020" for thin; 0.015"–0.020" for medium; 0.020"–0.035" for thicker grafts)
- Powered (Zimmer or Padgett electric dermatome) or manual (Watson or Humby knife): the surgeon selects the width and depth; the scrub tech sets the blade guard to the correct depth measurement
- Donor site most commonly the **anterolateral thigh**; the thigh is stretched taut and lubricated with mineral oil before the dermatome is applied

**Retractors (Donor Site):**
- No conventional retractors — the assistant stretches the skin taut using flat boards or their palms placed on each side of the advancing dermatome
- A **tongue depressor** or **skin grafting board** pressed firmly against the leading edge of the dermatome creates a flat, even surface for the blade to glide across

---

### The Procedure

1. Mineral oil applied to the donor site; assistant stretches skin taut
2. Dermatome set to correct depth and width; powered on; applied firmly to donor skin; harvested graft rolls onto the dermatome guard
3. Graft removed from the dermatome; placed dermis-down on a damp saline gauze; kept moist
4. **Meshing (if indicated):** graft threaded through a mesh carrier at the desired expansion ratio (1:1.5, 1:2, 1:3); perforated pattern allows the graft to expand and conform to irregular surfaces; allows drainage
5. Recipient wound bed prepared: excise non-viable tissue, ensure vascularity, control hemostasis
6. Apply graft dermis-down onto the recipient bed; trim to fit; secure with staples, suture, or staple + suture combination
7. Graft must have complete, immobile contact with the bed — any hematoma, seroma, or shear movement = graft failure

---

### The Closure

**Donor site:** covered with a non-adherent dressing (Adaptic, Xeroform, Mepitel) + absorptive gauze + elastic bandage. The donor site heals by re-epithelialization from the residual skin appendages; it is NOT sutured.

**Graft (recipient site):** No formal closure — the graft IS the closure. Secured edges + a bolster dressing (tie-over or negative pressure dressing) to hold the graft against the bed.

**Bolster dressing:** A mound of moist cotton or foam is placed over the graft and sutured (tie-over bolster) or an NPWT dressing is applied at negative pressure to keep constant contact between graft and bed.

## 21.3 Local Flap — Z-Plasty

### The Approach

**Position:** Determined by the location of the scar or contracture; most commonly supine or sitting for facial/neck Z-plasties.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol for extremities; betadine-based or CHG for the face.

**Draping:** Appropriate to the region; a small towel aperture drape for facial Z-plasties; extremity drapes for limb contractures.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #15 blade on a #3 handle; the Z-plasty design is drawn on the skin with a surgical marking pen before the incision:
- Central limb along the scar
- Two diagonal limbs extending from each end of the central limb at 60° angles in opposite directions
- The resulting three incisions define two triangular flaps

**Retractors:** Fine-tooth skin hooks or Senn retractors (double-ended) to elevate and retract the triangular flap tips during dissection.

---

### The Procedure

1. Incise all three limbs of the Z with the #15 blade
2. Elevate both triangular flaps in the subcutaneous plane using Metzenbaum scissors
3. Transpose the flaps: each triangle is rotated 90° and sutured into the opposite limb's position — the result is a scar reoriented 90° from its original direction and lengthened along the axis of contracture
4. **Net effect:** scar contracture is relieved by reorienting the tension vector; the total scar length increases

---

### The Closure

**Deep / subcutaneous:** 4-0 Monocryl buried interrupted (to eliminate dead space and reduce skin tension).

**Skin:** 5-0 nylon or Prolene interrupted (face); or 4-0 nylon elsewhere.

**Dressing:** Non-adherent + light gauze; or wound closure strips (Steri-strips) reinforcing the skin edges.

---

## 21.4 Free Flap (Microvascular)

### The Approach

This is the most complex rung of the reconstructive ladder. The Approach involves two simultaneous fields: the **donor site** (where the flap is harvested) and the **recipient site** (where the defect exists and the vessels for anastomosis are dissected).

**Position:** Varies widely by flap and defect. For a DIEP flap (abdominal free flap to breast): supine; arms extended. For a fibula free flap (for jaw reconstruction): lateral, with the leg extended.

**Prep:** Both donor and recipient sites are prepped simultaneously (two teams).

**Draping:** Two separate sterile fields, or one large combined field encompassing both sites.

**Gown and Gloves:** Two scrubbed teams typically work simultaneously — one harvesting the flap, one preparing the recipient site.

**The Knife:**
- **Recipient site:** #15 or #10 blade for the defect/resection incision and the vessel exposure incision
- **Donor site:** #15 blade for flap harvest; the flap is elevated with electrocautery, scissors, and the operating microscope or loupes

**Retractors:**
- **Recipient vessels:** fine vein retractors and vessel loops (Silastic rubber loops around the recipient artery and vein) control and retract the recipient vessels during anastomosis
- **Operating microscope:** used during the microvascular anastomosis; the surgeon looks through the microscope; both the artery and the vein are anastomosed under high magnification
- The microscope replaces conventional retractors — the vessel loops hold the vessels in place; the microscope provides the access

---

### The Procedure

1. **Donor site:** flap is harvested with its pedicle (artery + vein identified and dissected to maximum length); flap is divided only when the recipient vessels are confirmed ready
2. **Recipient site:** prepare the defect; identify recipient vessels (internal mammary artery/vein for breast; facial artery for head and neck; anterior tibial artery for lower extremity)
3. **Microvascular anastomosis:**
   - Arterial anastomosis first: **9-0 or 10-0 nylon** interrupted sutures (6–10 per anastomosis); magnification ×10–×25; each suture placed with jewelers forceps and Castroviejo needle drivers; vessel clamps removed; flow confirmed by Doppler
   - Venous anastomosis: same technique; or a **venous coupler** (mechanical coupling device) used for vein-to-vein anastomosis (faster, reliable)
4. Confirm flap perfusion: visible bleeding at cut edges; Doppler signal; capillary refill

---

### The Closure

**Donor site:** primary closure of the donor wound (Pfannenstiel closure for DIEP; fibula leg closed in layers) or skin graft coverage of the donor.

**Recipient site:** Flap sutured to wound edges; drains placed under the flap; layered closure of any remaining tissue planes.

**Dressing:**
- Non-compressive dressing over the free flap — **no pressure** on the flap; compression occludes the anastomosis
- Window dressing (gauze with a clear window over the flap surface) allows regular monitoring of flap perfusion post-op

---

## 21.5 Mastectomy with Immediate Implant Reconstruction

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; arm on the operative side extended on an arm board (allows access to the axilla for sentinel node biopsy or axillary dissection).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the entire chest from the chin to the umbilicus and laterally to include the axilla.

**Draping:**
- Four towels framing the chest; fenestrated sheet (or mastectomy drape)
- The arm is draped with a stockinette and may be draped free or secured
- If immediate reconstruction, the implant must be handled without contact with the skin or breast tissue — **the implant pocket is irrigated with triple antibiotic solution** (gentamicin + bacitracin + polymyxin B in saline) and gloves are changed before any implant handling

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; gloves changed before implant handling (the "no-touch technique").

**The Knife:**
- **Skin-sparing mastectomy:** #10 blade; elliptical incision around the nipple-areola complex; the skin envelope is preserved; the breast tissue is removed through the periareolar opening
- **Simple mastectomy:** #10 blade; larger elliptical incision incorporating the nipple-areola complex

**Retractors:**
- **Initial skin:** army-navy retractors
- **Skin flap elevation:** Senn retractors, sharp Hohmann, or a lighted retractor (to illuminate the plane between the dermis and the breast tissue)
- **Axillary dissection (if performed):** Richardson retractors; vein retractors to protect the axillary vein

---

### The Procedure

1. Elliptical skin incision; elevate skin flaps in the subcutaneous plane (thin flaps preserve the skin envelope)
2. Divide the breast at the pectoralis major fascia; free the breast from the muscle; remove specimen
3. **Sentinel lymph node biopsy** (if indicated): blue dye and/or radiotracer-guided identification of the sentinel node(s) in the axilla; remove and send for frozen section
4. **Axillary lymph node dissection** (if indicated): remove Level I/II axillary nodes
5. **Reconstruction:**
   - **Tissue expander:** a deflated silicone shell placed under the pectoralis major and serratus anterior; filled with saline at the time of surgery; serves as a temporary spacer until permanent implant exchange
   - **Direct-to-implant (DTI):** permanent silicone gel implant placed directly; supported by an acellular dermal matrix (ADM — e.g., AlloDerm) sewn to the inferior pectoralis border, forming a pocket for the lower pole
6. Irrigate pocket with triple antibiotic solution; change gloves; introduce implant with minimal handling

---

### The Closure

**Pectoralis / ADM sutures:** 2-0 or 3-0 Vicryl, securing the ADM to the inferior chest wall and pectoralis edge.

**Subcutaneous:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular + Steri-strips.

**Drain:** Two JP drains through separate stab incisions — one in the mastectomy flap space, one in the axilla.

**Dressing:** Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad; surgical bra or binder applied for compression.

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "In plastic surgery, your Approach must protect the thing that heals. A contaminated graft bed, a crushed flap pedicle, or an implant that touched skin are all failures that begin before the Procedure."

- Skin grafts die without contact. If the bolster is too loose, too early to remove, or improperly placed, the graft fails. Protect your work during the Closure phase.
- In free flap cases, change your gloves before handling any suture used for the anastomosis. A contaminated anastomosis is a thrombosed anastomosis.
- The no-touch technique for implants is not optional. Even a brief contact between an implant and the skin increases the biofilm seeding risk and the rate of capsular contracture.
