# Chapter 20: Ophthalmology

---

## Anatomy You Need at the Table

**External:** Cornea (transparent anterior dome), sclera (white wall), limbus (cornea-sclera junction), conjunctiva (mucous membrane lining the inner lids and sclera).

**Anterior segment:** Anterior chamber (aqueous humor), iris, lens (crystalline; focuses light onto retina).

**Posterior segment:** Vitreous (gel-filled cavity), retina (macula = central vision), choroid (vascular layer beneath the retina), optic nerve (CN II).

**IOP:** Normal intraocular pressure 10–21 mmHg. Maintained by aqueous humor production and drainage.

**Critical fact:** The eye is a closed pressurized sphere. Any breach of the outer coats (cornea, sclera) that is not controlled causes sudden loss of IOP — which can trigger expulsive hemorrhage, vitreous loss, or collapse of the eye. Nothing in ophthalmic surgery is casual.

**Why it matters at the back table:** Ophthalmic surgery is the smallest-scale work in the OR. Every pass happens under a microscope. Instruments must arrive oriented correctly, silently, and precisely. An IOL touched by a glove is destroyed. A wrong solution instilled into the eye is catastrophic.

## Cataract Extraction with IOL Implantation (Phacoemulsification)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, head in foam donut or flat headrest · Avg time: 15–30 min · Team: surgeon only or + 1 scrub · Microscope: required · Implants: intraocular lens (IOL)

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] IOL confirmed: type and power confirmed with the surgeon before the case begins; IOL loaded into the injector cartridge per manufacturer's instructions — do NOT touch the IOL with gloves or instruments
> - [ ] Phacoemulsification machine: footpedal set up; confirmed tubing flushed and primed; BSS bags spiked
> - [ ] OVD (viscoelastic): Healon, DisCoVisc, or equivalent; on the field on the Mayo stand; syringe pre-drawn
> - [ ] Keratome (primary incision knife): correct width for the IOL system; on the Mayo stand
> - [ ] Paracentesis knife: on the Mayo stand; this makes the side-port entry
> - [ ] Cystotome (or capsulorhexis forceps): for the continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis
> - [ ] Eye speculum: size appropriate for the patient; confirm it opens to the correct width
> - [ ] BSS in a 3 mL syringe with a 27-gauge cannula: for hydrodissection and stromal hydration
> - [ ] Do NOT use CHG anywhere near the eye — it is directly toxic to the corneal endothelium; use betadine only
> - [ ] Dilating drops confirmed given 30–60 minutes before the case; confirm the pupil is fully dilated before incision

**Position:** Supine; head in a foam donut or flat headrest; operating microscope directly overhead. Slight reverse Trendelenburg in some setups.

**Prep:** 5% betadine drops instilled into the conjunctival sac immediately before draping — this is the primary antiseptic. External periocular skin prepped with betadine paint. Never use CHG near the eye.

**Draping:** Ophthalmic aperture adhesive drape placed directly over the periorbital skin; lashes are folded away from the operative field by the adhesive flap. The eye speculum is placed after draping. The microscope is draped with a sterile cover before the case.

**The Knife:** Keratome (2.2–2.8 mm, pre-formed angled blade) creates a self-sealing primary corneal incision at the limbus. A smaller paracentesis knife creates a side-port entry 90° away.

**Retractors:** Wire eye speculum holds the eyelids open. Irrigation fluid pressure within the anterior chamber maintains the globe's shape — this is the "retractor" for the anterior segment.

### The Procedure

1. Eye speculum placed; confirm full pupil dilation
2. **OVD injection:** viscoelastic injected through the paracentesis into the anterior chamber (protects the corneal endothelium; maintains chamber depth)
3. **Capsulorhexis:** cystotome or capsulorhexis forceps tears a smooth, continuous circular opening in the anterior lens capsule (CCC — continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis)
4. **Hydrodissection:** BSS injected under the anterior capsule; the lens nucleus rotates freely when complete
5. **Phacoemulsification:** the phaco handpiece is inserted through the main incision; ultrasonic energy emulsifies the nucleus; simultaneous irrigation and aspiration removes the material
6. **Irrigation/Aspiration (I/A):** a separate I/A handpiece removes remaining soft cortical material from within the capsular bag
7. **OVD re-injection:** viscoelastic fills the capsular bag to support IOL deployment
8. **IOL implantation:** foldable IOL loaded in the injector cartridge; inserted through the main incision; deployed and unfolded within the capsular bag; haptics positioned
9. **OVD removal:** I/A handpiece removes all remaining viscoelastic
10. **Stromal hydration:** BSS injected into the corneal stroma at the incision to create a watertight self-sealing closure

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **IOL contamination:** if the IOL is touched by a glove, instrument, or falls off the field, it cannot be used; open a new one; do NOT attempt to place a contaminated lens
> - **Phaco surge:** if the phaco tip becomes occluded and suddenly releases, there is a surge of aspiration that can collapse the anterior chamber; BSS-filled syringe must be immediately accessible to restore the chamber
> - **Posterior capsule rupture:** if the posterior capsule tears during phacoemulsification, the case changes immediately — the surgeon may need different instruments (vitrectomy handpiece, different OVD); watch for the call and be ready
> - **OVD not fully removed:** retained viscoelastic causes a post-op IOP spike; the surgeon will confirm its removal — do not rush the I/A step
> - **Wrong solution on the field:** confirm every solution on the back table is labeled; solutions that look identical (BSS, viscoelastic, Miochol) have completely different effects if instilled in the wrong sequence

### The Closure

No sutures required — the keratome incision is self-sealing by design. If a wound leak is detected (Seidel test with fluorescein), a single 10-0 nylon suture closes the wound.

**Subconjunctival injection:** Antibiotic + steroid at the end of the case.

**Dressing:** Antibiotic + steroid drops; rigid plastic eye shield taped over the orbit (shield only — not a pressure patch).

## Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, head in foam donut · Avg time: 60–180 min depending on pathology · Team: surgeon + 1 scrub · Microscope: required · Implants: none; tamponade agent (gas or silicone oil) at the end

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Vitrectomy machine: confirm gauge (23 vs. 25 gauge); tubing set primed; cutter running at the correct cut rate; infusion BSS primed; footpedal configured
> - [ ] Cannulas (3-port system): infusion, endoilluminator, and working port; matching gauge and length
> - [ ] Wide-angle viewing system (BIOM or AVI): coupling gel ready; confirm the viewing lens is sterile
> - [ ] Endolaser probe (532 nm or diode): in the room; tested for function before the case
> - [ ] Tamponade agent: confirm which agent the surgeon will use (gas type: C₃F₈ or SF₆, or silicone oil); have it ready but do NOT open until called
> - [ ] Silicone oil injection system (if applicable): confirm the injection set matches the oil viscosity ordered
> - [ ] Membrane forceps, pick, and any retinal instruments the surgeon requests for specific pathology
> - [ ] Do NOT use CHG near the eye; betadine only

**Position:** Supine; head in a foam donut; operating microscope directly overhead.

**Prep:** 5% betadine drops; betadine periocular skin prep; no CHG.

**Draping:** Ophthalmic aperture adhesive drape; eye speculum placed.

**The Knife:** Three MVR blade stab incisions at the pars plana (3.5–4 mm posterior to the limbus, over non-functional retina):
- Inferotemporal: infusion cannula (sutured in place)
- Superotemporal: endoilluminator (light pipe)
- Superonasal or second temporal: working instrument port

**Retractors:** Eye speculum. The infusion cannula maintains IOP with continuous BSS infusion — this keeps the eye properly inflated throughout the case. The wide-angle viewing system provides panoramic visualization of the fundus.

### The Procedure

1. Place three cannulas; suture the infusion cannula; confirm its tip is visible in the vitreous cavity before starting infusion
2. Insert the vitrector; core vitrectomy to debulk the vitreous
3. Address the pathology: peel epiretinal membranes, trim traction, drain subretinal fluid, treat retinal breaks with endolaser
4. **Endolaser:** laser probe inserted through the working port; photocoagulation applied around retinal tears
5. **Fluid-air exchange (FAX):** infusion switched to air; retina flattened
6. **Tamponade placement:** air left in place, or gas (C₃F₈, SF₆) or silicone oil injected to maintain retinal tamponade

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Infusion cannula position:** if the infusion cannula tip is in the retina rather than the vitreous cavity, infusion flow will strip the retina; surgeon must confirm the tip position with direct visualization before starting infusion
> - **Gas type and concentration:** if the wrong gas or concentration is used for tamponade, the bubble volume is wrong; confirm the gas type and mixing ratio with the surgeon before injection
> - **Silicone oil emulsification:** if the wrong viscosity of silicone oil is used, it can emulsify and cause cataract or glaucoma post-op; confirm the viscosity specification before opening
> - **Endolaser over normal retina:** the laser must be applied only to the target area; extra passes risk permanent scotomas; pass the probe cautiously and confirm the surgeon sees the target clearly before activating

### The Closure

23/25-gauge incisions are self-sealing — no sutures. 20-gauge incisions require 7-0 Vicryl closure of each sclerotomy. Conjunctiva closed with 7-0 Vicryl at each cannula site, or self-sealing.

**Subconjunctival injection:** Antibiotic + steroid.

**Dressing:** Rigid eye shield taped over the orbit.

**Post-op positioning:** If gas tamponade placed, patient must maintain face-down positioning for 1–2 weeks. Alert the OR team and post-op nursing before the patient leaves the room.

## Strabismus Correction (Extraocular Muscle Surgery)

> **QUICK FACTS**
> Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, head neutral · Avg time: 30–60 min · Team: surgeon + 1 · Microscope or loupes · Implants: none

### The Approach

> **BEFORE YOU SCRUB**
> - [ ] Muscle hooks (Jameson, Green, or squint hooks): multiple sizes; at least 3 on the Mayo stand — two to isolate the muscle, one for backup
> - [ ] 6-0 Vicryl loaded on a spatula needle × 2 for the muscle sutures
> - [ ] Westcott scissors (spring scissors) for the conjunctival peritomy and dissection
> - [ ] 0.12 mm Colibri forceps for conjunctival grasping
> - [ ] Calipers: for measuring the recession or resection distance on the sclera
> - [ ] Muscle clamp (e.g., Von Graefe): for resection cases
> - [ ] Oculocardiac reflex warning: alert anesthesia when the muscle hook goes under the muscle and any time traction is applied — bradycardia is common and expected

**Position:** Supine; head neutral; microscope or surgical loupes used.

**Prep:** 5% betadine drops; betadine periocular skin prep.

**Draping:** Ophthalmic aperture adhesive drape; eye speculum placed.

**The Knife:** Fine Westcott scissors or #15 blade makes a conjunctival peritomy (circumferential incision through the conjunctiva at the limbus) to expose the sclera and muscle insertions.

**Retractors:** Eye speculum holds the lids. Muscle hooks (Jameson, Green) are the primary retractors — hooked instruments slid under the targeted extraocular muscle to isolate it. Fine Colibri forceps grasp the conjunctiva during the peritomy.

### The Procedure

1. Conjunctival peritomy; expose the targeted muscle's scleral insertion
2. Pass the muscle hook under the muscle; place a second hook to ensure the full width is engaged
3. Dissect the intermuscular membrane to free the muscle
4. Place locking sutures (6-0 Vicryl double-arm) through the muscle belly
5. **Recession (weakening):** cut the muscle at its scleral insertion; reattach it more posteriorly using calipers to measure the recession distance; 6-0 Vicryl suture
6. **Resection (strengthening):** clamp the muscle posteriorly; excise the clamped segment; reattach at the original insertion; shorter muscle = stronger

> **⚠ WATCH FOR**
> - **Oculocardiac reflex:** traction on any extraocular muscle triggers vagally-mediated bradycardia; this is not an emergency but it must be anticipated — warn anesthesia each time the hook is placed and each time traction is applied; the reflex resolves when traction is released
> - **Scleral suture depth:** if a suture is passed too deeply through the sclera, it can penetrate the eye and cause retinal hemorrhage; pass the spatula needle parallel to the scleral surface, not perpendicular
> - **Muscle slip:** if the muscle hook slips off the muscle during dissection, the muscle retracts into the orbit — a surgical emergency; have a second hook immediately available at all times
> - **Caliper measurement:** recession and resection amounts are calculated pre-op and are exact; confirm the measurement with the surgeon before tying the suture

### The Closure

| Layer | Suture | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Conjunctiva | 6-0 plain gut or 7-0 Vicryl | Interrupted or running at the peritomy site; or self-healing if opening is small |

**Dressing:** Antibiotic eye ointment; eye pad for 4–6 hours or eye drops without patching (surgeon preference).

## Clinical Pearls

> "In ophthalmology, the Approach is measured in millimeters and the Procedure is measured in microns. The keratome and the speculum must be perfect before the eye is ever touched."

- Never touch an IOL directly. Use only the injector cartridge. If the IOL contacts a glove, an instrument, or the floor, it goes in the trash — not in the eye.
- Label every solution on the back table. BSS, viscoelastic, Miochol, and Miostat all look nearly identical. Instilling the wrong one into the anterior chamber changes the case immediately.
- In strabismus, warn anesthesia every time you apply traction to a muscle. The oculocardiac reflex is predictable and manageable — but only if anesthesia expects it.
- In vitrectomy, confirm the infusion cannula tip is visible in the vitreous cavity before turning on the infusion. This is not optional. Infusing into the retina strips it off the choroid.
