## 20.1 Ocular Anatomy Review

**External:** Cornea (transparent anterior dome), sclera (white wall), limbus (cornea-sclera junction), conjunctiva (mucous membrane lining the inner lids and sclera).

**Anterior segment:** Anterior chamber (aqueous humor between cornea and iris), iris (colored ring with pupil), lens (crystalline; focuses light onto retina).

**Posterior segment:** Vitreous (gel-filled posterior cavity), retina (light-sensing neural tissue; macula = central vision; fovea = peak acuity), choroid (vascular layer beneath retina), optic nerve (CN II).

**IOP:** Normal intraocular pressure 10–21 mmHg. Maintained by balance of aqueous humor production and drainage.

**Critical fact:** The eye is a closed pressurized sphere. Any breach of the outer coats (cornea, sclera) that is not controlled causes loss of IOP, which can result in expulsive hemorrhage, loss of vitreous, or collapse of the eye.

## 20.2 Cataract Extraction with IOL Implantation (Phacoemulsification)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; head in a neutral headrest (foam donut or flat head rest for microscope access); slight reverse Trendelenburg in some setups. The operating microscope is positioned directly over the face.

**Prep:**
- Dilating drops administered 30–60 minutes preoperatively (phenylephrine + tropicamide to fully dilate the pupil)
- **5% betadine drops** instilled into the conjunctival sac immediately before draping — this is the primary antiseptic for ophthalmic prep and is proven to reduce postoperative endophthalmitis
- **Never use CHG near the eye** — it is directly toxic to the corneal endothelium
- External periocular skin: betadine paint applied with a cotton applicator; prep the lids, lashes (lashes may be covered), and periorbital skin

**Draping:**
- **Ophthalmic aperture drape:** a small adhesive drape with a fenestrated opening that adheres directly to the periorbital skin; isolates the eye from surrounding skin and lashes
- The adhesive border sticks to the skin edges around the eye; the lashes are turned away from the operative field (a small adhesive flap folds the lashes outward)
- The microscope is already draped with a sterile cover prior to the case

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; ophthalmic surgery is performed at very small scale — gloves must fit precisely (no bunching).

**The Knife:** A **keratome** (a pre-formed diamond or metal knife with a specific width and angled blade) makes the primary corneal incision at the limbus — typically a 2.2–2.8 mm self-sealing incision. A smaller **paracentesis** knife makes a secondary side-port entry approximately 90° away from the main incision. These are the two entry points for the entire procedure.

**Retractors:**
- **Wire speculum (eye speculum):** placed between the lids before draping is complete; holds the eyelids open throughout the case; comes in multiple sizes and designs (wire spring, Barraquer lid speculum)
- This is the only retractor in cataract surgery — the speculum holds the lids; the eye itself is held open by the irrigating fluid pressure within the anterior chamber

---

### The Procedure

1. Eye speculum placed; confirm pupil is fully dilated
2. **OVD (viscoelastic) injection:** ophthalmic viscosurgical device (e.g., Healon, DisCoVisc) injected into the anterior chamber through the paracentesis to protect the corneal endothelium and maintain chamber depth
3. **Capsulorhexis:** a bent cystotome (or capsulorhexis forceps) tears a smooth, circular opening in the anterior lens capsule (continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis — CCC); the IOL will be placed through this opening into the capsular bag
4. **Hydrodissection:** BSS injected under the anterior capsule to free the lens nucleus from the cortex; the nucleus rotates freely when complete
5. **Phacoemulsification:** the phaco handpiece (ultrasonic) is inserted through the main incision; ultrasonic energy emulsifies the nucleus into small particles; simultaneous irrigation maintains chamber; aspiration removes the emulsified material
6. **Irrigation/Aspiration (I/A):** a separate I/A handpiece removes the remaining soft cortical material from within the capsular bag
7. **OVD re-injection:** viscoelastic fills the capsular bag to support IOL deployment
8. **IOL implantation:** the foldable IOL is loaded into a single-use injector cartridge; the injector is introduced through the main incision; the IOL is deployed and unfolds within the capsular bag; haptics (supporting arms) are positioned in the bag equator
9. **OVD removal:** I/A handpiece removes all remaining viscoelastic from the anterior chamber
10. **Seal incisions:** BSS injected to hydrate the stromal tissue at the incision site, creating a watertight seal; the self-sealing architecture of the wound closes without sutures

---

### The Closure

The primary corneal incision is **self-sealing** by design (the tri-planar, beveled keratome creates a valve that closes under IOP). No sutures are typically required.

**Suture:** If a wound leak is detected (Seidel test — fluorescein strip shows aqueous streaming out), a single 10-0 nylon suture closes the wound.

**Subconjunctival injection:** Antibiotic + steroid (e.g., cefuroxime + dexamethasone) injected subconjunctivally at the end of the case.

**Patch and shield:** A drop of antibiotic + steroid eye drops; a firm plastic **eye shield** taped over the orbit (not a pressure patch — a shield only, to prevent accidental trauma).

**Dressing:** No dressing on the eye itself; only the rigid shield.

## 20.3 Pars Plana Vitrectomy (PPV)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; head in a neutral foam donut; operating microscope overhead.

**Prep:** Same as cataract — 5% betadine drops; betadine periocular skin prep; no CHG.

**Draping:** Same ophthalmic aperture adhesive drape; eye speculum placed.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** Three **microvitreoretinal (MVR) blade** stab incisions at the **pars plana** (3.5–4 mm posterior to the limbus, in the scleral region that overlies non-functional retina):
1. Inferotemporal: infusion cannula
2. Superotemporal: endoilluminator (light pipe)
3. Superonasal (or second temporal): working instrument port (vitrector, forceps, laser probe)

These three incisions (typically 23 or 25 gauge — very small) define the entire intraoperative access to the posterior segment.

**Retractors:**
- The eye speculum holds the lids
- The **infusion cannula** (sutured in place at its port) maintains IOP with BSS infusion — this is the "pressure retractor" keeping the eye properly inflated throughout the case
- The **wide-angle viewing system** (e.g., BIOM or AVI lens placed on the cornea with coupling gel) provides an inverted, panoramic view of the fundus through the microscope

---

### The Procedure

1. Place three cannulas at pars plana; secure infusion cannula with a suture; confirm the tip is visible in the vitreous cavity before starting infusion
2. Insert the **vitrector** (pneumatic guillotine cutter: cuts vitreous at 5,000–10,000 cuts/minute while aspirating); core vitrectomy to debulk the vitreous
3. Address pathology: peel epiretinal membranes with membrane forceps, trim vitreous traction, drain subretinal fluid, treat retinal breaks with endolaser
4. **Endolaser:** a fiber-optic laser probe (usually diode 532 nm) is inserted through the working port; laser photocoagulation seals retinal tears around the detached area
5. **Fluid-air exchange (FAX):** infusion switched to air; subretinal fluid drained; retina flattened
6. **Tamponade agent placement:** air left in place, or gas (C₃F₈, SF₆) or silicone oil injected to maintain retinal tamponade

---

### The Closure

**Cannula sites:** The 23/25-gauge incisions are self-sealing (similar to the keratome incisions in cataract); no sutures required for small-gauge vitrectomy. Larger gauge (20-gauge) requires 7-0 Vicryl closure of each sclerotomy.

**Conjunctiva:** Closed with 7-0 Vicryl at each cannula site (or self-sealing).

**Subconjunctival injection:** Antibiotic + steroid.

**Shield:** Rigid eye shield taped over the orbit.

**Patient positioning post-op:** If a gas tamponade was placed, the patient must maintain face-down positioning for 1–2 weeks to keep the gas bubble over the macula. If silicone oil was placed, the positioning requirement depends on the pathology.

## 20.4 Strabismus Correction (Extraocular Muscle Surgery)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; head neutral; operating microscope or surgical loupes used.

**Prep:** 5% betadine drops; periocular betadine.

**Draping:** Ophthalmic aperture drape; eye speculum.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** A fine **Westcott scissors** (spring scissors) or a #15 blade makes a **conjunctival peritomy** — a circumferential incision through the conjunctiva at the limbus to expose the underlying sclera and the muscle insertions.

**Retractors:**
- **Eye speculum** holds the lids
- **Squint hooks (muscle hooks, Jameson hooks, Green hooks):** hooked instruments slid under the targeted extraocular muscle; when pulled, the hook holds the muscle out of the eye for isolation and manipulation — this is the primary retractor for strabismus surgery
- Fine tooth forceps (0.12 Colibri) grasp the conjunctiva during the peritomy

---

### The Procedure

1. Conjunctival peritomy; expose the targeted muscle's scleral insertion
2. Pass the muscle hook under the muscle; place a second hook to ensure the full width of the muscle is engaged
3. Dissect the intermuscular membrane and capsule to free the muscle
4. Place **locking sutures** (6-0 Vicryl) through the muscle belly; double-arm
5. **Recession (weakening):** clamp and cut the muscle at its scleral insertion; reattach the muscle more posteriorly on the sclera — new insertion point sutured with 6-0 Vicryl; muscle now has less mechanical advantage
6. **Resection (strengthening):** clamp the muscle at a more posterior point; excise the clamped segment; reattach the remaining muscle at its original insertion point — shorter muscle = stronger

---

### The Closure

**Conjunctiva:** 6-0 plain gut or 7-0 Vicryl; interrupted or running at the peritomy site. Or the conjunctiva may be left to heal by itself if the opening is small.

**Dressing:** Antibiotic eye ointment; eye pad (pressure patch) for 4–6 hours post-op; or eye drops without patching.

## Clinical Pearls

> "In ophthalmology, the Approach is measured in millimeters and the Procedure is measured in microns. The keratome and the speculum must be perfect before the eye is ever touched."

- An IOL touched directly with an instrument or glove causes permanent surface damage. Use only the injector cartridge. If the IOL is dropped, it is unusable — open a new one.
- If the phaco handpiece accidentally becomes occluded and then suddenly releases, there is a surge of aspiration that can collapse the anterior chamber. Keep BSS readily available.
- In strabismus surgery, traction on extraocular muscles triggers the **oculocardiac reflex** (vagally-mediated bradycardia). Warn anesthesia when you pass the muscle hook and when it is under tension.
