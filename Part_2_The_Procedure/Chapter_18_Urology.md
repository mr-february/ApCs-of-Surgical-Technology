# Chapter 18: Urology

## 18.1 Urologic Anatomy Review

**Kidneys:** Retroperitoneal; renal pelvis → ureter → bladder. Three narrowings where stones lodge: ureteropelvic junction (UPJ), pelvic brim, ureterovesical junction (UVJ).

**Bladder:** Muscular; trigone (posterior base) contains the two ureteral orifices and the internal urethral orifice.

**Prostate:** Surrounds the prostatic urethra; divided into peripheral zone (most cancers), transitional zone (BPH), central zone.

**Male urethra:** Prostatic → membranous (external sphincter — critical to preserve) → penile/bulbar.

## 18.2 Cystoscopy with Ureteral Stent Placement

### The Approach

**Position:** Lithotomy (Allen or candy-cane stirrups).

**Prep:** Betadine; prep the perineum, external genitalia, and inner thighs. Males: prep the glans and urethral meatus.

**Draping:**
- Lithotomy/perineal drape; or a cystoscopy drape with a collection pouch integrated at the perineum to catch irrigation fluid runoff
- Fenestrated aperture over the urethral meatus (males) or vaginal/urethral region (females)

**Gown and Gloves:** Sterile gown; sterile gloves. Standard setup.

**The Knife:** No skin incision — the approach is through the **natural orifice** (urethra). The "knife" equivalent is the obturator tip of the cystoscope sheath, which passes through the urethra.

**Retractors:**
- No conventional retractors — the **cystoscope sheath** itself holds the urethra open
- **Irrigation fluid** (sterile saline or sterile water) distends the bladder and creates the working space — it functions as both the retractor and the visualization medium

---

### The Procedure

1. Lubricate the urethra with sterile lubricating jelly
2. Introduce the cystoscope sheath with the obturator; advance gently through the urethra into the bladder
3. Remove obturator; attach the bridge + telescope (30° for most bladder inspections; 70° for the lateral walls and anteriorly)
4. Connect irrigation; distend the bladder; systematically inspect all walls, trigone, ureteral orifices, and dome
5. **Ureteral stent placement:** under fluoroscopy (C-arm), pass a guidewire through the cystoscope working channel up the ipsilateral ureter to the renal pelvis; pass the double-J stent over the guidewire using a pusher; withdraw the guidewire; stent coils proximally in the renal pelvis and distally in the bladder

---

### The Closure

No wound closure — the urethra self-closes.

Remove all instruments; drain the bladder; remove the cystoscope.

**Post-procedure:** Foley catheter may be left in place if stent was placed or if the surgeon wishes to monitor urine output.

## 18.3 Transurethral Resection of the Prostate (TURP)

### The Approach

**Position:** Lithotomy (Allen stirrups; legs slightly lower than standard — reduces venous pressure in the prostate and decreases bleeding).

**Prep:** Betadine; perineum and genitalia.

**Draping:** Same as cystoscopy — lithotomy/cystoscopy drape with collection pouch.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** No skin incision. The approach is entirely endoscopic — through the urethra. The "cutting" instrument is the **resectoscope's loop electrode**, which uses electrical current (cut mode) to shave away prostatic tissue.

**Retractors:**
- The **resectoscope sheath** (22–26 Fr) holds the urethra open
- **Irrigation fluid** (non-conductive glycine 1.5% for monopolar TURP; saline for bipolar TURP) continuously distends the bladder and carries resected tissue chips away from the operative site

---

### The Procedure

1. Insert the resectoscope sheath; attach the working element (cutting loop), telescope, and irrigation
2. Inspect the bladder; identify the **verumontanum** (a small mound on the posterior prostatic urethra = distal safety landmark; resection must not go past this point — the external sphincter lies just distal)
3. Systematically resect the obstructing prostatic tissue in chips; work from the bladder neck toward the verumontanum; coagulate bleeding vessels with ball electrode
4. Chips collect in the bladder; evacuate periodically with an **Ellik evacuator** (a glass bulb irrigator that creates a vortex pulling chips into the collection chamber)
5. Final hemostasis; ensure no active bleeding vessels before withdrawing the scope

**Scrub tech: track irrigation fluid balance — record fluid in and fluid out; a large discrepancy (fluid absorbed into circulation) may signal TURP syndrome.**

---

### The Closure

No wound closure.

**Post-procedure:** A **three-way Foley catheter** (22–24 Fr) with a 30 mL balloon is placed into the bladder; the balloon is positioned at the bladder neck; continuous bladder irrigation (CBI) through the third channel prevents clot formation. Traction on the catheter may be applied briefly to tamponade the prostatic fossa bleeding.

## 18.4 Radical Nephrectomy (Laparoscopic)

### The Approach

**Position:** Lateral decubitus (operative side up); bean bag positioner for stability; kidney rest elevated under the flank; table slightly "broken" (flexed at the waist) to open the flank space.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the entire flank circumferentially from the lower rib margin to the iliac crest and from the midline anteriorly to the spine posteriorly.

**Draping:**
- Split sheet under the patient
- Lap sheet equivalent over the upper body and lower extremities
- The flank is exposed as the operative field; an antimicrobial incise drape (Ioban) may seal the drape edges to the prep site
- The draping must allow free access from the 12th rib to the iliac crest

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #11 blade for all port incisions:
- 10–12 mm camera port at the umbilicus or slightly lateral (in line with the flank)
- Two or three 5 mm working ports in the flank
- A small hand-assist port (7–8 cm Pfannenstiel or lower quadrant incision) if hand-assisted technique is used

**Retractors:**
- No conventional retractors — the pneumoperitoneum (flank/retroperitoneal approach) provides working space
- **Laparoscopic instruments** (fan retractor, grasper) retract the colon medially to expose the kidney
- In the hand-assisted technique, the surgeon's non-dominant hand inside the hand-assist port provides direct tactile retraction

---

### The Procedure

1. Access the peritoneal cavity; place trocars under direct vision
2. Reflect the colon medially along the white line of Toldt; expose the retroperitoneum and kidney
3. Identify the renal hilum; dissect the **renal artery first** (clips + divided or Endo-GIA vascular load); then the **renal vein** (Endo-GIA or clips)
4. Free the kidney from surrounding fat and Gerota's fascia; preserve the adrenal gland (if not involved by tumor)
5. Divide the ureter distally with clips; specimen freed
6. Place specimen in an extraction bag; extend one port incision to extract the kidney

---

### The Closure

**Fascial closure at extraction site:** 0 Vicryl interrupted or running (Pfannenstiel fascia closed securely).

**Port sites ≥10 mm:** 0 Vicryl fascial closure with Carter-Thomason or direct suture.

**Skin:** 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular at all sites.

**Dressing:** Small adhesive dressings.

## 18.5 Robotic-Assisted Radical Prostatectomy (RARP)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; steep Trendelenburg (gravity displaces bowel out of the pelvis and toward the diaphragm); arms tucked; padded shoulder supports to prevent sliding.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep from umbilicus to mid-thigh, full width; include the perineum.

**Draping:**
- Laparoscopic draping of the lower abdomen
- Robot draping: the robotic arms are sterile-draped (dedicated robotic arm drapes); the scrub tech assists with draping the robot before it is docked
- The patient cart (robot) is docked over the patient's lower body after port placement

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard. The scrub tech stands at the robot-side patient cart throughout the case to exchange instruments at the robotic arms.

**The Knife:** #11 blade for all port incisions:
- 12 mm camera port just superior to the umbilicus (or infraumbilical)
- Three to four 8 mm robotic arm ports in a fan configuration across the lower abdomen
- One 10–12 mm assistant port for the bedside scrub tech to introduce sutures, clips, and irrigate

**Retractors:**
- **Pneumoperitoneum + steep Trendelenburg** is the "retractor" — the bowel falls cephalad by gravity, exposing the pelvis
- Robotic instruments (ProGrasp forceps, needle drivers, scissors) provide all intraoperative retraction through robotic arms under the console surgeon's control
- The **bedside assistant** (scrub tech or PA) may provide additional retraction through the assistant port with a laparoscopic grasper

---

### The Procedure

1. Ports placed under direct vision; robot docked to the ports
2. Enter the space of Retzius (retropubic space) by incising the peritoneum; the bladder falls posteriorly
3. Divide the endopelvic fascia; control the dorsal venous complex (DVC) of Santorini with a suture ligature (2-0 Vicryl on a large needle — passed through the assistant port by the scrub tech)
4. Dissect the bladder neck; identify and divide the urethra anteriorly
5. Dissect the posterior prostate off the rectum; divide the posterior urethra and seminal vesicles
6. Nerve-sparing dissection (if indicated): preserve the neurovascular bundles along the posterolateral prostate using cold scissors (no energy)
7. Divide the urethra at the apex; remove the specimen through the assistant port in a bag
8. **Vesicourethral anastomosis:** the bladder neck is sewn to the urethral stump with a running 3-0 Monocryl suture (Van Velthoven technique — two sutures from each side meeting posteriorly); a Foley catheter placed before tying

---

### The Closure

**Robot undocked** before closure.

**Peritoneum:** 3-0 Vicryl running; close the pelvic peritoneum over the anastomosis.

**Fascial closure at 12 mm ports:** 0 Vicryl.

**Skin (all sites):** 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular.

**Drain:** JP drain placed near the vesicourethral anastomosis through an assistant port site; 2-0 nylon to skin.

**Dressing:** Small adhesive dressings; the Foley catheter remains for 5–7 days.

## Clinical Pearls

> "In urology, the Approach is often through a natural orifice. The irrigation fluid is your medium, your light, and your retractor all at once — manage it carefully."

- In a TURP, keep track of the irrigation running in vs. running out. If the patient absorbs a liter or more of glycine, the electrolyte shift is an emergency — not a lab result to deal with later.
- In RARP, the scrub tech's assistant-port role is demanding. Pre-load sutures in a needle holder before they are called for; the console surgeon cannot stop and wait.
- For nephrectomy, the renal artery is always divided before the vein. Dividing the vein first causes the kidney to engorge with blood — significantly increasing blood loss during the remainder of the dissection.
