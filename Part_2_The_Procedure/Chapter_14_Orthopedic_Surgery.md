# Chapter 14: Orthopedic Surgery

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Apply the ApC framework to common orthopedic procedures.
2. Describe the specific draping, incision, and retractor setup for each orthopedic case.
3. Explain the scrub technologist's role in total joint arthroplasty, fracture fixation, and arthroscopy.
4. Describe tourniquet use, cement handling, and implant documentation.

---

## 14.1 Musculoskeletal Anatomy Overview

**Bone:** Cortical (dense outer) and cancellous/trabecular (spongy inner). Long bones contain the medullary canal. Bone heals by callus formation and remodeling over 6–8 weeks (cortical) to 3–4 weeks (cancellous).

**Joints:** Articular cartilage covers the ends of bones within joints; stabilized by ligaments (bone-to-bone) and tendons (muscle-to-bone). Synovial fluid lubricates the joint surface.

## 14.2 Total Hip Arthroplasty (THA)

### The Approach

**Position:** Lateral decubitus (posterior approach — most common); or supine (anterior approach). For lateral: patient secured with a beanbag positioner and lateral post system; dependent leg padded at the knee and ankle; axillary roll placed.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the entire lower extremity circumferentially from the iliac crest to the foot; include the perineum (betadine) if visible.

**Draping:**
- Impervious stockinette placed over the foot and lower leg
- Hip drape system (split sheet below the hip + large lap-sheet equivalent); OR
- **Extremity draping:** three-quarter sheet placed under the hip; transparent adhesive incise drape (Ioban) over the hip to seal the drape edge to the prep site; extremity drape (split sheet) wrapping the leg
- The leg must be free-draping — able to be internally/externally rotated throughout the case

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; double-glove is strongly recommended for joint arthroplasty (implant sterility protection, increased glove perforation rates in longer cases).

**The Knife:** #10 blade on a #3 handle. Posterior approach: the incision runs from just posterior to the greater trochanter, curving proximally along the posterior aspect of the femur, approximately 10–15 cm total length. The skin incision must extend through Camper's fascia to the iliotibial band.

**Retractors:**
- Initial soft tissue: Cobra retractors and sharp Hohmann retractors placed around the acetabulum once the hip capsule is opened
- Femoral: offset Hohmann retractor under the femoral neck; bone hook around the proximal femur during femoral preparation
- The combination of sharp Hohmann retractors (anterior, posterior, inferior rim) creates 360° acetabular exposure

---

### The Procedure

1. Incise iliotibial band and gluteus maximus fascia; split gluteus maximus bluntly
2. Identify and tag the short external rotators (piriformis, obturator internus); divide near femoral insertion for capsular access and later repair
3. Open and excise the posterior hip capsule; dislocate the femoral head anteriorly
4. Resect the femoral head with an oscillating saw at the predetermined neck cut angle (templated preoperatively)
5. **Acetabular preparation:** place Hohmann retractors for exposure; ream the acetabulum concentrically in increasing sizes until subchondral bone is uniformly exposed ("bleeding bone"); place definitive acetabular shell with impactor (press fit ± 1–2 screws); insert liner
6. **Femoral preparation:** sequentially broach the femoral canal in increasing sizes until cortical contact is achieved; trial stem + head; reduce hip; assess stability and range of motion
7. Place definitive stem (cementless press-fit or cemented); attach definitive femoral head; reduce hip; final stability check

---

### The Closure

**External rotators:** Reattach piriformis and obturator internus to the femur with #1 Ethibond through drill holes or suture anchors.

**Fascia lata / IT band:** #1 Vicryl interrupted or figure-of-eight.

**Subcutaneous:** 2-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** Staples or 3-0 Monocryl subcuticular.

**Drain:** Hemovac drain through a separate stab incision; sutured with 2-0 nylon.

**Dressing:** Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad + elastic tape (Elastikon) or waterproof dressing.

## 14.3 Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; ipsilateral hip slightly flexed and externally rotated with a lateral post or triangular positioning pillow under the thigh. Tourniquet applied to the proximal thigh over cast padding.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; elevate the extremity; prep circumferentially from tourniquet to toes. Apply tourniquet padding first, then prep below it.

**Draping:**
- Impervious stockinette over the foot
- Lay the limb down; split sheet below the knee; transparent adhesive incise drape over the knee
- Extremity drape wrapped around the thigh below the tourniquet; the leg must flex to 90°+ during the procedure — draping must not restrict this movement
- A leggings-type knee drape system is commonly used

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; double-glove for implant sterility.

**The Knife:** #10 blade on a #3 handle. Anterior midline skin incision from 5 cm above the patella to the tibial tubercle; approximately 12–15 cm. The incision is made with the knee in flexion (90°) to reduce skin tension.

**Retractors:**
- Medial parapatellar arthrotomy (incision through the quadriceps tendon, around the medial patella, to the patellar tendon): retractors are inserted into the knee joint itself
- **Hohmann retractors** placed medially, laterally, and posteriorly within the joint for bony exposure
- **Knee retractor (bent Hohmann or specific TKA retractor)** placed posteriorly to protect the posterior capsule during tibial cut
- The patella is everted or subluxed laterally to expose the distal femur

---

### The Procedure

1. Medial parapatellar arthrotomy; enter knee joint; evert/sublux patella
2. Remove anterior fat pad (partial); identify landmarks
3. **Distal femoral cut:** distal cutting guide pinned to femur; oscillating saw makes the distal cut
4. **AP, posterior, and chamfer cuts:** anterior/posterior/chamfer cutting guide pinned; oscillating saw completes the four femoral cuts; femoral box cut made for posterior stabilized component if used
5. **Tibial cut:** extramedullary or intramedullary tibial guide; oscillating saw makes the proximal tibial cut perpendicular to the tibial shaft axis
6. **Size and trial:** trial femur + tibial tray + poly insert in place; flex/extend knee; assess balance — flexion gap must equal extension gap; medial/lateral balance checked
7. **Patellar resurfacing (if performed):** saw guide; oscillating saw; patellar drill holes; trial patellar button; cement definitive button
8. **Cement and final components:** cement or press-fit femoral component; cement tibial tray; insert poly; cement patella; hold all components in place until cement cures (fully pressurize each)
9. Deflate tourniquet; identify and control bleeding vessels with electrocautery before closing
10. Final range-of-motion check

---

### The Closure

**Arthrotomy (quadriceps/medial parapatellar):** #1 Vicryl interrupted figure-of-eight.

**Subcutaneous:** 2-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** Staples or 3-0 Monocryl.

**Drain:** Hemovac drain through a lateral stab incision (some surgeons; practice is evolving).

**Dressing:** Bulky soft dressing — saline-moistened Kerlix wrapped around knee, then cast padding, then elastic bandage (ACE wrap) from foot to mid-thigh.

## 14.4 Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) — Distal Radius Fracture

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; arm extended on a hand table (radiolucent for fluoroscopy). Tourniquet on proximal forearm.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the entire arm from tourniquet to fingertips circumferentially.

**Draping:**
- Impervious stockinette to the fingers
- Split sheet under the arm; additional extremity drape
- Arm board covered with sterile drape; C-arm sterile drape placed over the imaging detector

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #15 blade on a #3 handle (smaller incision, finer control). Volar (palmar) approach (Henry approach): longitudinal incision along the volar radial wrist, approximately 5–7 cm.

**Retractors:** Small Army-Navy retractors initially; then small Weitlaner placed in the subcutaneous layer; later, small Richardson retractors to protect the flexor tendons and neurovascular structures as the fracture is exposed.

---

### The Procedure

1. Incise through skin, subcutaneous; identify the palmaris longus (if present) and flexor carpi radialis tendons; retract FCR radially and FPL ulnarly
2. Incise the floor of the FCR sheath; expose the pronator quadratus (PQ) muscle
3. Incise PQ off the radial border; elevate subperiosteally to expose the distal radius fracture
4. Reduce fracture fragments; hold with K-wires or reduction forceps; confirm with fluoroscopy
5. Apply volar locking plate (VLP) to the volar surface of the radius; confirm positioning with fluoroscopy
6. Drill; measure; place locking screws in the distal fragment and cortical screws in the shaft
7. Final fluoroscopic images in multiple planes; confirm alignment and hardware position

---

### The Closure

**Pronator quadratus:** Re-approximate with 3-0 Vicryl; provides a soft tissue layer over the plate.

**FCR sheath floor:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Subcutaneous:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular or interrupted nylon; Steri-strips.

**Splint:** Volar plaster or fiberglass splint applied by the surgeon or cast tech in the OR.

**Dressing:** Webril cotton padding under the splint; ace wrap.

## 14.5 Knee Arthroscopy with Partial Meniscectomy

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; leg hanging free off the table break in a leg holder (circumferential leg post); or leg holder device on the table rail. Tourniquet on the proximal thigh (inflated only if needed for visibility).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep the entire lower leg and knee circumferentially from tourniquet to ankle.

**Draping:**
- Impervious stockinette to the ankle
- Extremity drape; split sheet below the tourniquet; arthroscopy fluid collection pouch drape placed under the knee to catch irrigation runoff

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #11 blade on a #3 handle (stab incisions only — 5 mm or smaller). Anterolateral portal: 1 cm incision at the lateral soft-spot just lateral to the patellar tendon at the joint line. Anteromedial portal: mirror image medially (for instrumentation).

**Retractors:** None — the distended joint (maintained by arthroscopic fluid pump pressure) provides its own working space. The arthroscope and instruments provide access through portals.

---

### The Procedure

1. Inject local anesthetic into portal sites; make stab incisions with #11 blade
2. Insert arthroscope trocar-cannula; enter the knee joint; remove trocar; attach camera; begin irrigation
3. Systematic inspection: suprapatellar pouch → medial gutter → medial compartment (medial meniscus, MCL) → intercondylar notch (ACL, PCL) → lateral compartment (lateral meniscus, LCL) → lateral gutter → patellofemoral joint
4. Identify the meniscal tear; classify (type, location, zone)
5. Insert probe through the anteromedial portal; probe the tear to assess extent and stability
6. Switch: arthroscope to anteromedial; biter or shaver to anterolateral
7. Resect the unstable meniscal fragment with arthroscopic biter (Basket forceps) or motorized shaver; contour the remaining stable rim smoothly
8. Irrigate the joint; remove loose fragments; inspect final result

---

### The Closure

**Portal sites:** 4-0 nylon horizontal mattress or simple interrupted × 1 per portal; or skin glue.

**Dressing:** 4×4 gauze over portals; cotton roll or Kerlix wrapped around knee; ace wrap.

**No deep layer closure required** — portals are too small.

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "In orthopedics, the Approach is also mechanical. Retractors that aren't held steady, or an incise drape that peels mid-case, compromises the entire sterile field around a permanent implant."

- Arthroscopy runs wet. Irrigation fluid runs everywhere. Protect your back table with a waterproof barrier drape if the field is prone to flooding.
- Cement has a working window. Know the temperature of the room — warm rooms shorten cement working time. Communicate timing to the surgeon.
- Double-glove for every joint arthroplasty. Studies show glove perforation rates of 20–30% in TJA cases; the inner glove is the patient's last line of protection.
