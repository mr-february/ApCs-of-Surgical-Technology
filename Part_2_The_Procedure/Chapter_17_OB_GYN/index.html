    Chapter 17: Obstetrics and Gynecology¶

    Anatomy You Need at the Table¶

    Uterus: Fundus (top), body, cervix. Layers: endometrium, myometrium, serosa.

    Adnexa: Fallopian tubes (fimbriated ends capture ova) + ovaries (bilateral; produce hormones).

    Critical relationship: The ureter passes under the uterine artery ("water under the bridge") at the lateral cervix. Ureteral injury is the most feared complication of hysterectomy. It happens when the ureter is not identified before the uterine vessels are clamped.

    Why it matters at the back table: OB/GYN cases often escalate quickly (especially C-sections and postpartum hemorrhage). Know the case flow; anticipate the turn from routine to emergent.

    Cesarean Section (C-Section)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Supine with left lateral tilt · Avg time: 30–60 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant + neonatal team at delivery · Blood products: type and screen; keep blood available

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Neonatal team notified and present in the room before incision - [ ] Warm blanket or receiving towel on the lower end of the sterile field for infant handoff — the infant is NOT sterile - [ ] Doyen (bladder blade) retractor on the Mayo stand; ready at the uterine incision step - [ ] Bandage scissors (for uterine incision extension): on the Mayo stand, NOT in the general scissor pile - [ ] Oxytocin: circulator has it drawn and ready to give at cord clamping — confirm with anesthesia the moment the cord is clamped - [ ] Two cord clamps immediately accessible (not buried in a pile) - [ ] 0 or #1 Vicryl × 2 loaded on needle holders for uterine closure before the infant is out - [ ] Specimen cup for placenta

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    #10 blade on #3 handle × 2 One for skin incision, one for fascial scoring and uterine stab incision
    Bandage scissors Extends the uterine incision laterally — NOT standard scissors; keep on Mayo stand separately
    Mayo scissors, curved Fascial extension and general cutting
    Mayo scissors, straight General cutting
    Metzenbaum scissors Tissue dissection
    DeBakey tissue forceps Atraumatic tissue handling
    Adson forceps (1×2 teeth) Skin and fascial edges
    Russian tissue forceps General tissue grasping
    Kelly hemostats × 6 Hemostasis and clamping
    Kocher clamps × 4 Fascial and tissue clamping
    Allis clamps × 2 Fascial edge retraction
    Right-angle clamps × 2 Dissection and vessel isolation
    Cord clamps × 2 Umbilical cord clamping at delivery — keep immediately accessible
    Mayo-Hegar needle drivers × 2 Suturing throughout
    Doyen retractor / bladder blade Most critical retractor — retracts bladder to expose lower uterine segment
    Army-Navy retractors × 2 Skin and subcutaneous retraction
    Richardson retractors × 2 Fascial and soft tissue retraction
    Balfour or O'Sullivan-O'Connor retractor For cases with difficult abdominal access
    Yankauer suction × 2 One for amniotic fluid at delivery, one for abdomen
    Bulb syringe Infant airway clearance at delivery
    ESU pencil Electrocautery throughout
    Warm receiving towel / sterile drape For infant handoff — infant is NOT sterile
    Specimen cup For placenta

    Position: Supine with left lateral tilt (wedge or folded blanket under the right hip to displace the uterus off the IVC — prevents supine hypotensive syndrome).

    Prep: CHG/alcohol or betadine; xiphoid to upper thighs, full width including the pubic area.

    Draping: Ether screen (vertical drape bar) across the patient's chest → four towels framing the Pfannenstiel incision → fenestrated laparotomy sheet. A warm receiving blanket or sterile towel on the lower sterile field is ready to receive the infant.

    The Knife: #10 blade on #3 handle. Pfannenstiel (low transverse) skin incision 2 cm above the pubic symphysis, 10–12 cm. Same blade scores the anterior rectus fascia transversely; fascia then extended laterally with Mayo scissors. Uterine incision: #10 blade stab incision into the lower uterine segment; bandage scissors extend it laterally in a curved direction.

    Retractors: Doyen retractor (bladder blade) retracts the bladder inferiorly to expose the lower uterine segment. An assistant holds it downward throughout the bladder flap and uterine incision. Army-Navy retractors for skin and fascial retraction.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Skin incision; divide subcutaneous fat; open anterior rectus fascia transversely; separate rectus muscles bluntly
    2. Open peritoneum sharply; extend opening
    3. Bladder flap: incise peritoneum overlying the lower uterine segment; retract bladder down with Doyen retractor; expose smooth lower uterine segment
    4. Uterine incision: #10 blade stab; extend with bandage scissors laterally
    5. Infant delivery: surgeon's hand enters to guide the presenting part; circulator applies fundal pressure; deliver head → clear airway → deliver body
    6. Cord clamped and cut: two clamps placed; divided between them; infant passed immediately to neonatal team
    7. Circulator gives oxytocin to anesthesia at cord clamping; placenta delivered spontaneously or manually extracted; examine for completeness
    8. Close uterine incision with 0 or #1 Vicryl

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Uterine atony after delivery: if the uterus is boggy and bleeding doesn't stop after oxytocin, additional uterotonics (Methergine, Hemabate, misoprostol) will be called for — know they exist and alert the circulator to have them available - Doyen retractor slipping: if the bladder rises into the field, the bandage scissors can enter the bladder — hold the retractor steady; if it slips, call it out before the surgeon extends the incision - Fetal malpresentation: if the presenting part is not easily delivered, the surgeon may need forceps (Simpson or Kielland) — confirm they are in the room before the case - Cord clamping timing: in some cases (planned delayed cord clamping), the surgeon will hold the infant at the level of the introitus for 30–60 seconds before clamping — do not rush the clamps - Specimen placenta: the placenta is sent to pathology in many institutions; have a labeled specimen cup ready

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Uterine incision #1 Vicryl running One or two layers; inner continuous lock + outer imbricating
    Peritoneum 3-0 Vicryl running Surgeon may choose to leave open
    Rectus fascia 0 Vicryl running Looped PDS or two strands meeting in the middle
    Subcutaneous fat 3-0 Vicryl If ≥2 cm depth
    Skin Staples or 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular + Steri-strips

    Dressing: Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad.

    Total Abdominal Hysterectomy (TAH)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Supine; Foley catheter placed · Avg time: 60–120 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Specimen: uterus ± tubes ± ovaries to pathology

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] O'Sullivan-O'Connor (OSO) retractor on the field; select appropriate blade sizes for the patient's body habitus - [ ] Heaney clamps × 6–8 on the Mayo stand and easily accessible in the clamp rack — you will pass these repeatedly - [ ] 0 Vicryl ties pre-cut × 8–10; on the field in a visible location (these are the ligature ties for each pedicle) - [ ] Suture scissors (not tissue scissors) immediately accessible for cutting after each tie - [ ] Long instruments available: long Metzenbaum, long Deaver, long DeBakeys — pelvic work is deep - [ ] Specimen: large basin on the back table; confirm pathology slip required - [ ] Foley catheter confirmed in by the circulator before draping

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    #10 blade on #3 handle Skin incision
    Mayo scissors, curved Fascial and general cutting
    Mayo scissors, straight General cutting
    Metzenbaum scissors Pelvic tissue dissection
    Metzenbaum scissors, long Deep pelvic dissection
    DeBakey tissue forceps Standard tissue handling
    DeBakey tissue forceps, long Deep pelvic tissue handling
    Adson forceps Skin and superficial tissue
    Russian tissue forceps General tissue grasping
    Heaney clamps × 8 Workhorse clamp for pedicle ligation — keep all 8 in a visible rack; passed in rapid succession
    Ochsner / Kocher clamps × 4 Tissue and fascial clamping
    Long Heaney or Masterson clamps × 4 Deep pelvic pedicles
    Right-angle clamps × 2 Vessel isolation and dissection
    Mayo-Hegar needle drivers × 2 Standard suturing
    Long Mayo-Hegar needle driver Deep pelvic suturing
    O'Sullivan-O'Connor (OSO) retractor + lateral blades + bladder blade + upper arm Self-retaining pelvic retractor — essential for pelvic exposure; confirm correct blade set
    Deaver retractors × 2 Hand-held retraction of pelvic structures
    Long Deaver retractors × 2 Deep pelvic hand-held retraction
    Army-Navy retractors × 2 Skin and subcutaneous retraction
    Richardson retractors × 2 Fascial retraction
    Suture scissors Dedicated scissors for cutting ties only — do not use for tissue
    Yankauer suction Standard suction
    Poole suction Irrigation and bulk fluid removal
    ESU pencil with long active extension Electrocautery including deep pelvic use
    Bipolar forceps Precise hemostasis
    Pre-cut 0 Vicryl ties × 10+ Pedicle ligation in rapid succession — lay out visibly before the case starts
    Large specimen basin Uterus ± adnexa to pathology

    Position: Supine; Foley catheter placed before prepping. Low lithotomy added if vaginal access needed.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; umbilicus to mid-thigh including perineum; betadine for vaginal prep.

    Draping: Four towels framing the lower abdominal incision → laparotomy sheet. Stirrups and perineal drape if lithotomy position.

    The Knife: #10 blade. Pfannenstiel (transverse) or midline incision (umbilicus to pubis) for large uteri, malignancy, or limited pelvic access.

    Retractors: O'Sullivan-O'Connor self-retaining retractor placed after peritoneal entry: lateral blades + inferior bladder blade + superior arm for bowel retraction. Deaver retractors hand-held for specific pelvic structures as needed.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Enter peritoneum; systematic pelvic exploration
    2. Round ligaments: clamped (Heaney), cut, ligated bilaterally (0 Vicryl tie)
    3. Broad ligament opened; ureter identified and protected before proceeding
    4. IP ligaments (ovaries removed): clamped, cut, ligated; OR utero-ovarian ligaments (ovaries preserved): clamped, cut, ligated
    5. Uterine vessels: bladder reflected downward; uterine vessels clamped at the cervix bilaterally (three-clamp technique: two clamps on patient's side, one on specimen); cut; suture-ligated with 0 Vicryl
    6. Cardinal and uterosacral ligaments: clamped, cut, ligated sequentially
    7. Vagina incised circumferentially around the cervix; uterus and cervix removed
    8. Vaginal cuff closure: running 0 Vicryl locking stitch or interrupted figure-of-eight

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Uterine clamp slippage: if a Heaney clamp slips off a pedicle, tell the surgeon immediately — do not assume they saw it; re-clamping protects against hemorrhage and ureteral injury - Ureter identification: the ureter must be confirmed before clamping the uterine vessels; if the surgeon is working in a scarred or distorted pelvis, slow down and confirm anatomy before passing any clamp - Running out of Heaney clamps: this case uses 6–8 Heaneys in rapid succession; track their location; never leave a clamp on a pedicle without telling the surgeon what is still on the field - Pedicle bleeding after ligature: if a tie slips, have a suture on a needle holder immediately available — 0 Vicryl on a CT-1 or SH needle - Vaginal cuff closure: once the uterus is removed, the surgeon works in the vagina from above; keep your field organized and ready to pass the 0 Vicryl for cuff closure without delay

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Vaginal cuff 0 Vicryl running locking or interrupted figure-of-eight Critical layer — hemostasis
    Peritoneum 3-0 Vicryl running May be left open
    Fascia 0 Vicryl or PDS running
    Subcutaneous 3-0 Vicryl
    Skin Staples or 4-0 Monocryl
    Drain JP in pelvis Rarely needed; used if significant dissection

    Dressing: Gauze 4×4 + ABD pad.

    Laparoscopic Hysterectomy (TLH)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Low lithotomy → steep Trendelenburg · Avg time: 75–120 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Energy device: LigaSure or EnSeal · Uterine manipulator: required

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Uterine manipulator (RUMI or Koh system) placed vaginally before the abdominal approach — confirm it's been placed before prepping - [ ] Laparoscopic energy device (LigaSure or EnSeal) — confirm correct instrument and generator are in the room - [ ] Laparoscope: 10 mm 0° or 30°; camera white-balanced; light cord confirmed - [ ] 5 mm trocars × 3 + 10–12 mm umbilical trocar - [ ] 0 Vicryl × 2 for port site fascial closure; 4-0 Monocryl for skin - [ ] Vaginal cuff closure: 0 Vicryl on a CT-1 needle; may be sutured laparoscopically or vaginally

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    #11 blade on #3 handle × 4 All port incisions
    10–12 mm umbilical trocar × 1 Camera port
    5 mm trocars × 3 Working ports
    10 mm 0° or 30° laparoscope + camera + light cord Confirm white balance and light before draping
    Laparoscopic energy device (LigaSure or EnSeal) Pedicle ligation — confirm correct generator is connected
    Laparoscopic graspers × 2 One toothed; tissue manipulation and retraction
    Laparoscopic scissors (hook or curved) Tissue cutting and colpotomy
    Laparoscopic Maryland dissector Blunt dissection and vessel exposure
    Laparoscopic suction/irrigation Field irrigation and fluid removal
    Laparoscopic needle drivers × 2 Vaginal cuff closure through scope
    Carter-Thomason fascial closure device Fascial closure at all ports ≥10 mm
    Uterine manipulator (RUMI or Koh system) Placed vaginally pre-op — confirm in place before draping
    Specimen retrieval bag (endobag) For adnexa if removed
    0 Vicryl on CT-1 needle Vaginal cuff closure
    ESU pencil Backup energy
    Yankauer suction Backup suction

    Position: Supine → modified low lithotomy (Allen stirrups) after abdominal access established; steep Trendelenburg (bowel displaces out of the pelvis by gravity).

    Prep: CHG/alcohol abdomen; betadine vaginal prep.

    Draping: Fenestrated laparoscopic sheet over the trocar sites; perineal drape with vaginal access incorporated.

    The Knife: #11 blade for all port incisions. Umbilical: 10–12 mm. Three additional 5 mm ports in lower quadrants and suprapubic.

    Retractors: Trocars + pneumoperitoneum + steep Trendelenburg. The uterine manipulator antireflexes and retroflexes the uterus from below to expose each vascular pedicle.

    The Procedure¶

    Mirrors TAH steps executed laparoscopically. Energy device (LigaSure/EnSeal) replaces the clamp-cut-tie technique for ligating pedicles. The vaginal cuff is cut with laparoscopic scissors; the uterus is delivered vaginally through the colpotomy.

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Energy device to the ureter: the ureter runs close to the uterine artery; if the energy device fires too close to the ureter without direct visualization, thermal injury causes a delayed ureteral leak — confirm the surgeon has visualized the ureter before each energy device activation at the parametrium - Uterine manipulator movement: if the manipulator is not moving correctly (the scrub tech or assistant is managing it from below), the surgeon cannot expose the correct pedicle — communicate; respond to verbal cues - Pneumoperitoneum loss: if a trocar is dislodged, CO₂ escapes rapidly; the surgeon must repuncture — have an extra trocar of the same size on the field - Port site fascial closure: ports ≥10 mm must have fascial closure to prevent port-site hernia; do not skip this step

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Vaginal cuff 0 Vicryl Laparoscopic or vaginal
    Port sites ≥10 mm fascia 0 Vicryl Required
    Skin (all ports) 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular

    Dressing: Small adhesive dressings over port sites.

    Dilation and Curettage (D&C)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Lithotomy · Avg time: 10–20 min · Team: surgeon only · No incision · Specimen: endometrial curettings to pathology

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Hegar or Pratt dilators: lay out in ascending order on the Mayo stand before the case starts - [ ] Weighted speculum: confirm size (standard vs. large); on the Mayo stand - [ ] Anterior Sims retractor on the Mayo stand - [ ] Single-tooth tenaculum (cervical): loaded in hand before passing - [ ] Uterine sound: confirm it's on the Mayo stand - [ ] Sharp curettes (small, medium, large) in order - [ ] Specimen cup labeled and ready to receive curettings immediately

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    Weighted (Auvard) vaginal speculum Provides constant posterior vaginal retraction via its own weight
    Anterior Sims retractor Anterior vaginal wall retraction
    Single-tooth tenaculum Grasps and stabilizes the anterior cervix
    Hegar dilators — full set (#1–14) Cervical dilation in ascending order — lay out in sequence before the case
    Pratt dilators — full set Alternative tapered dilator set
    Uterine sound Measures uterine cavity depth before dilation
    Sharp uterine curette, small Endometrial curettage
    Sharp uterine curette, medium Endometrial curettage
    Sharp uterine curette, large Endometrial curettage
    Serrated ring forceps / sponge forceps Removing larger tissue fragments
    Suction curettage cannula + syringe or vacuum system If suction curettage planned
    Betadine-soaked sponge stick Cervical prep
    Specimen cup Labeled and ready before the first curette pass

    Position: Lithotomy (Allen stirrups).

    Prep: Betadine vaginal prep.

    Draping: Lithotomy/perineal drape — splits around both legs; perineum and vagina exposed.

    The Knife: No incision. Access is through the natural orifice (vagina/cervix).

    Retractors: Weighted speculum (posterior vaginal wall retraction — its own weight provides constant retraction) + anterior Sims retractor (anterior vaginal wall) = complete cervical exposure.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Bimanual examination under anesthesia; determine uterine size and position
    2. Grasp the cervix with a single-tooth tenaculum on the anterior lip
    3. Sound the uterus; note depth (normal 6–8 cm)
    4. Dilate the cervix: pass Hegar (or Pratt) dilators in ascending sequence from small to desired size
    5. Pass sharp curette into the uterine cavity; scrape the endometrial lining systematically; collect curettings
    6. Suction curettage (if indicated): vacuum aspiration cannula passed; suction applied; contents removed

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Uterine perforation: if the sound or curette passes further than the noted uterine depth, the uterus may have been perforated — call it out; the surgeon will assess with laparoscopy - Cervical laceration from tenaculum: if the tenaculum tears through the cervix, bleeding occurs at the site; silver nitrate or a figure-of-eight suture (0 Vicryl) is needed — have it ready - Dilators passed out of order: always hand the next size up in sequence; if a size is skipped, the cervix can tear rather than dilate - Incomplete specimen: if the pathology specimen is very small or absent, the surgeon will want to confirm the cavity was fully curetted; have an additional curette of a different size ready

    The Closure¶

    No formal wound closure. Remove the tenaculum; confirm cervical hemostasis; remove retractors. No sutures. No dressing.

    Robotic-Assisted Total Laparoscopic Hysterectomy (da Vinci TLH)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Low lithotomy → steep Trendelenburg · Avg time: 90–150 min · Team: console surgeon + bedside scrub tech + circulator + perineal assistant · Robot: da Vinci

    What changes at the back table: You are stationed at the patient cart. The console surgeon controls all robotic arm movements remotely and cannot directly pass anything to themselves. Every suture — including the vaginal cuff closure — comes through the assistant port from your hands. A separate perineal assistant manages the uterine manipulator from below throughout the entire abdominal phase; the console surgeon directs manipulator positioning verbally.

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Uterine manipulator (RUMI or Koh system) placed vaginally before any abdominal approach — confirm it is in place and the perineal assistant is positioned before prepping; the case cannot begin without it - [ ] Robot arm drapes × 4: assist the circulator before docking - [ ] Ports: 12 mm camera port (umbilical); 2–3 × 8 mm robotic arm ports; 1 × 8–12 mm assistant port for the bedside scrub tech - [ ] Robotic instruments: ProGrasp forceps + monopolar curved scissors + bipolar Maryland forceps + large needle driver — confirm all seated - [ ] Energy device for pedicle ligation: da Vinci Vessel Sealer Extend or bipolar Maryland — confirm per preference card; thermal spread from the Vessel Sealer is 2–4 mm - [ ] Vaginal cuff closure suture: 0 Vicryl on CT-1 needle × 2; load on a laparoscopic needle holder; have immediately available when the uterus is delivered — passed through the assistant port; surgeon closes the cuff robotically - [ ] Specimen delivery: uterus exits vaginally through the colpotomy; no endobag required for the uterus (have one available if adnexa are removed) - [ ] Endobag for adnexa: have one unsealed on the back table if oophorectomy is planned

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    EndoWrist ProGrasp forceps Tissue grasping and retraction via robotic arm
    EndoWrist monopolar curved scissors Dissection and cutting via robotic arm
    EndoWrist bipolar Maryland forceps Hemostasis via robotic arm
    EndoWrist large needle driver Suturing via robotic arm
    da Vinci Vessel Sealer Extend Pedicle ligation — thermal spread 2–4 mm; confirm per preference card
    Laparoscopic needle holder × 2 Passing 0 Vicryl through assistant port for vaginal cuff closure
    Laparoscopic grasper (5 mm) Assistant port retraction
    Laparoscopic suction/irrigation cannula For assistant port
    Endobag For adnexa if removed
    Robotic arm drapes × 4 Applied before docking
    12 mm trocar × 1 Camera port
    8 mm robotic trocars × 2–3 Robotic arm ports
    8–12 mm assistant port trocar × 1 Bedside scrub tech working port

    Position: Low lithotomy in Allen stirrups → steep Trendelenburg (30–45°) once ports are placed; gravity displaces the bowel cephalad and exposes the pelvic structures.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol abdomen; betadine vaginal prep; perineal area prepped for the uterine manipulator.

    Draping: Laparoscopic fenestrated sheet + perineal drape with vaginal access incorporated; robotic arm drapes applied before docking.

    The Procedure¶

    Robotic da Vinci TLH mirrors the conventional TLH sequence (see Section 17.3) executed through robotic arms:

    1. Round ligaments divided bilaterally with the energy device
    2. Ureter identification: the surgeon identifies the ureter on each side before any clamp or energy device approaches the parametrium; do not pass instruments during this step
    3. IP ligaments (ovaries removed) or utero-ovarian ligaments (ovaries preserved): divided with energy device
    4. Uterine vessels: bladder reflected downward; energy device divides the uterine artery pedicles bilaterally — confirm the surgeon has visualized the ureter before each activation near the uterine vessels; delayed ureteral thermal injury from a missed ureter presents 5–10 days post-op
    5. Cardinal and uterosacral ligaments divided sequentially
    6. Vagina incised at the colpotomy cup of the uterine manipulator; uterus delivered vaginally by the perineal assistant
    7. Vaginal cuff closure: 0 Vicryl on CT-1 passed through the assistant port; console surgeon sutures the cuff laparoscopically; have the suture loaded and in hand before the uterus is out

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Uterine manipulator response: the console surgeon controls the arms but not the manipulator; the perineal assistant must respond promptly to exposure requests; if the correct pedicle is not exposed, the surgeon will wait — do not pass the energy device until anatomy is exposed - Ureter thermal proximity: the Vessel Sealer and bipolar both spread heat 2–4 mm; a ureter not visualized before each activation is a ureter at risk; if the surgeon pauses near the parametrium, they are looking for the ureter — wait for them to call the instrument - Vaginal cuff suture timing: have the 0 Vicryl on CT-1 loaded and in your hand before the uterus is delivered; the surgeon will call for it immediately after the colpotomy is complete - CO₂ loss through the colpotomy: when the vagina is entered, pneumoperitoneum can escape around the manipulator; the perineal assistant should hold the manipulator firmly during the colpotomy step - Ovarian specimen extraction: if the ovaries are removed, place them in an endobag before extraction; tumor seeding of the port site is a risk if specimen is delivered loose

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Vaginal cuff 0 Vicryl CT-1 running Passed through assistant port; sutured robotically
    Port sites ≥10 mm fascia 0 Vicryl Required
    Skin (all ports) 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular

    Dressing: Small adhesive dressings over port sites.

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "In pelvic surgery, your Approach determines whether the ureter is safe. Your retractors determine whether the surgeon can see it."

    • In a C-section, the Doyen retractor is your most important tool. If it slips, the bladder enters the field. Hold it steady and do not let your grip fatigue.
    • In hysterectomy, keep your Heaney clamp supply visible and accessible. You will pass them in rapid sequence. Running out of clamps mid-case is not an option.
    • For a D&C, pre-arrange dilators in ascending order on the Mayo stand before the speculum goes in. You will not have time to sort them once the tenaculum is on the cervix.
    • The ureter in pelvic surgery is always at risk. If the surgeon stops and asks for better exposure near the parametrium, they are looking for the ureter. Do not rush them.
