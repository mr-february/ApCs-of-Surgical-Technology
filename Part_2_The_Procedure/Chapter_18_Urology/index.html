    Chapter 18: Urology¶

    Anatomy You Need at the Table¶

    Kidneys: Retroperitoneal; renal pelvis → ureter → bladder. Three narrowings where stones lodge: ureteropelvic junction (UPJ), pelvic brim, ureterovesical junction (UVJ).

    Bladder: Muscular; trigone (posterior base) contains the two ureteral orifices and the internal urethral orifice.

    Prostate: Surrounds the prostatic urethra. Peripheral zone (most cancers), transitional zone (BPH), central zone. The verumontanum is the distal safety landmark during TURP — do not resect past it; the external sphincter lies just beyond.

    Male urethra: Prostatic → membranous (external sphincter — critical to preserve for continence) → penile/bulbar.

    Why it matters at the back table: Urologic cases run heavily on scopes, irrigation, and energy devices through natural orifices. Your job is knowing which scope goes on which sheath, keeping irrigation running, and tracking fluid balance.

    Cystoscopy with Ureteral Stent Placement¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Lithotomy · Avg time: 15–30 min · Team: surgeon only or + 1 · Fluoroscopy: required for stent placement · No incision

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Cystoscope sheath (16–22 Fr) + bridge + telescope (30° standard; 70° for lateral walls) — confirm all components are together and functional - [ ] C-arm sterile drape ready; fluoroscopy required for stent wire placement - [ ] Guidewires: hydrophilic guidewire (Glidewire) × 1; stiff guidewire × 1 backup - [ ] Double-J ureteral stent: confirm size (length and diameter) ordered; confirm it's on the field unopened - [ ] Stent pusher (inserter) compatible with the stent system - [ ] Irrigation: 1L sterile saline bags × 2 primed and running through the cystoscope irrigation tubing - [ ] Lubricating jelly: sterile; for urethral introduction - [ ] Cystoscopy drape with integrated collection pouch — fluid runs constantly; the pouch catches the runoff

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    Cystoscope sheath (16–22 Fr) + obturator Urethral entry; obturator removed once in bladder
    Cystoscope bridge (working channel bridge) Attaches telescope to sheath; provides the working channel
    30° telescope Standard bladder visualization
    70° telescope Lateral wall and ureteral orifice visualization
    Irrigation tubing + 1 L sterile saline bags × 2 Spiked and primed; bladder distension and visualization medium
    Hydrophilic guidewire (Glidewire, 0.035") Primary wire for ureteral access
    Stiff guidewire (0.035") Backup wire if primary meets resistance
    Double-J ureteral stent Confirm length and diameter ordered; do not open until confirmed
    Stent pusher System-specific — confirm compatibility with stent before opening
    Foley catheter May be left post-procedure
    Cystoscopy drape with integrated collection pouch Fluid runoff collection
    Sterile lubricating jelly Urethral introduction
    C-arm sterile drape Fluoroscopic guidance during wire and stent placement

    Position: Lithotomy (Allen or candy-cane stirrups).

    Prep: Betadine; perineum, external genitalia, and inner thighs. Males: prep the glans and urethral meatus.

    Draping: Lithotomy/cystoscopy drape with collection pouch; fenestrated aperture over the urethral meatus.

    The Knife: No skin incision. The approach is through the natural orifice (urethra). The obturator tip of the cystoscope sheath is the entry instrument.

    Retractors: The cystoscope sheath holds the urethra open. Irrigation fluid distends the bladder and creates the working space.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Lubricate the urethra; introduce the cystoscope sheath with obturator
    2. Remove obturator; attach the bridge and telescope; connect irrigation; distend the bladder
    3. Systematic inspection: all walls, trigone, both ureteral orifices, dome
    4. Stent placement: under fluoroscopy, pass a guidewire through the working channel up the ipsilateral ureter to the renal pelvis; pass the double-J stent over the guidewire with the pusher; withdraw the guidewire; proximal coil in the renal pelvis, distal coil in the bladder

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Guidewire resistance: if the guidewire will not advance past a narrowing (UPJ, stone, stricture), do not force it — additional guidewire types (stiffer, angled) may be needed; have a second guidewire on the field - Stent mismatch: the pusher must match the stent system; a generic pusher on a specific stent will not seat correctly — confirm before opening - Irrigation bottle running dry: a cystoscope without irrigation deflates the bladder and collapses the visual field; maintain at least one backup bag primed and ready to switch without stopping - Fluoroscopy exposure: when the C-arm fires, step clear; do not hold instruments in the beam

    The Closure¶

    No wound closure. Remove all instruments; drain the bladder if needed. Foley catheter may be left in place post-procedure at the surgeon's discretion.

    Transurethral Resection of the Prostate (TURP)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Lithotomy · Avg time: 45–90 min · Team: surgeon only · No incision · Irrigation fluid: glycine 1.5% (monopolar) or saline (bipolar) — confirm with surgeon

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Resectoscope: confirm monopolar vs. bipolar system; gather the matching sheath (22–26 Fr), working element (cutting loop), and telescope - [ ] Irrigation fluid: glycine 1.5% (monopolar) or normal saline (bipolar) — do NOT mix these up; confirm with surgeon before spiking bags - [ ] Ellik evacuator on the field for evacuating tissue chips; use with saline (not glycine) - [ ] Three-way Foley catheter (22–24 Fr) with 30 mL balloon — needed at the end; have it ready - [ ] Track irrigation in vs. irrigation out throughout the case; document

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    Resectoscope sheath (22–26 Fr) Confirm monopolar vs. bipolar system with surgeon before opening
    Resectoscope working element (cutting loop electrode) The active resection instrument
    30° telescope for the resectoscope Visualization through the resectoscope
    Resectoscope irrigation tubing Connects irrigation fluid to the sheath
    Glycine 1.5% irrigation fluid Monopolar system only — do NOT use with bipolar
    Normal saline irrigation fluid Bipolar system only — confirm type before spiking bags
    Ellik evacuator Removes prostatic chips — use with saline only, never glycine
    Ball electrode Hemostasis; confirm it fits the working element
    Three-way Foley catheter (22–24 Fr, 30 mL balloon) Placed at case end for continuous bladder irrigation (CBI)
    CBI tubing + normal saline bags Set up at end of case for post-resection bladder irrigation
    Extra working element with fresh cutting loop Backup if primary loop breaks mid-case
    Fluid measurement / tracking log Circulator documents in vs. out throughout the entire case

    Position: Lithotomy (Allen stirrups; legs slightly lower than standard — reduces prostatic venous pressure and bleeding).

    Prep: Betadine; perineum and genitalia.

    Draping: Cystoscopy drape with collection pouch.

    The Knife: No skin incision. The resectoscope sheath passes through the urethra. The cutting element is the loop electrode (uses electrical current in cut mode to shave prostatic tissue).

    Retractors: The resectoscope sheath (22–26 Fr) holds the urethra open. Irrigation distends the bladder and carries resected tissue chips away from the field.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Insert resectoscope sheath; attach working element (cutting loop), telescope, and irrigation
    2. Inspect the bladder; identify the verumontanum — the distal safety landmark; mark its position visually; resection must not extend past this point
    3. Systematically resect obstructing prostatic tissue in chips; work from the bladder neck toward the verumontanum; coagulate bleeding vessels with the ball electrode
    4. Evacuate prostatic chips periodically using the Ellik evacuator
    5. Final hemostasis; confirm no active bleeding before withdrawing the scope

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - TURP syndrome: absorption of hypotonic glycine irrigation into the circulation causes hyponatremia, hypertension, bradycardia, and visual changes in the patient; if the circulator or anesthesia reports that the fluid balance is deeply negative (>1 L absorbed), alert the team — this is an emergency - Resection past the verumontanum: crossing this landmark damages the external sphincter → permanent incontinence; if the surgeon is approaching the verumontanum, confirm their awareness - Loop electrode failure: if the loop breaks or the current stops cutting, have a backup working element on the field; know how to switch the loop - Bleeding obscuring the field: aggressive prostatic bleeding drops visibility suddenly; have the ball electrode ready for immediate coagulation

    The Closure¶

    No wound closure. A three-way Foley catheter (22–24 Fr, 30 mL balloon) is placed into the bladder; balloon positioned at the bladder neck; continuous bladder irrigation (CBI) initiated to prevent clot formation. Brief catheter traction may tamponade the prostatic fossa.

    Laparoscopic Radical Nephrectomy¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Lateral decubitus, operative side up · Avg time: 90–150 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Implants: none · Specimen: kidney to pathology

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Laparoscopic tower: camera, light source, insufflator — all on and functioning before draping - [ ] Endo-GIA with vascular loads (white/gray) × 2–3 for renal artery and vein; confirm with surgeon - [ ] Medium and large titanium clips + clip applier for smaller vessels - [ ] Specimen extraction bag (endobag) — large enough for the kidney - [ ] Extraction port incision planning: surgeon will extend one port site; have #10 blade and longer instruments ready for that step - [ ] Kidney rest: confirm it is elevated and locked before prepping - [ ] 0 Vicryl for fascial closure at extraction site; 4-0 Monocryl for skin

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    #11 blade on #3 handle × 4 All port incisions
    #10 blade on #3 handle Extraction port incision extension
    10–12 mm camera trocar × 1 Camera port
    5 mm trocars × 2–3 Working ports
    10 mm 0° or 30° laparoscope + camera + light cord Confirm white balance and light before draping
    Laparoscopic graspers × 2 Tissue manipulation and retraction
    Laparoscopic Maryland dissector Hilar dissection and vessel exposure
    Laparoscopic scissors Tissue cutting and ureter division
    Laparoscopic L-hook electrocautery or Harmonic/LigaSure Energy dissection throughout
    Laparoscopic clip applier with medium and large titanium clips Smaller vessel control
    Endo-GIA stapler + vascular loads (white/gray) × 2–3 Renal artery and vein division — confirm load type before every fire
    Large specimen endobag Must be large enough for the kidney; do not force extraction
    Laparoscopic fan retractor Colon medial retraction to expose the retroperitoneum
    Laparoscopic suction/irrigation Field irrigation and fluid removal
    0 Vicryl Fascial closure at extraction site
    4-0 Monocryl Skin closure at all port sites
    Yankauer suction Backup suction

    Position: Lateral decubitus, operative side up; beanbag positioner. Kidney rest elevated under the flank; table slightly flexed at the waist to open the flank space. Confirm stability before draping.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; entire flank circumferentially from lower rib margin to iliac crest, midline anteriorly to the spine posteriorly.

    Draping: Split sheet under the patient → operative flank exposed → Ioban incise drape may seal drape edges.

    The Knife: #11 blade for all port incisions. 10–12 mm camera port at the umbilicus or lateral; two or three 5 mm working ports in the flank; hand-assist port (Pfannenstiel or lower quadrant) if hand-assisted technique.

    Retractors: Pneumoperitoneum provides the working space. Laparoscopic fan retractor or grasper retracts the colon medially. Hand-assist port provides direct tactile retraction (hand-assisted cases).

    The Procedure¶

    1. Access the peritoneal cavity; place trocars under direct vision
    2. Reflect the colon medially along the white line of Toldt; expose the retroperitoneum and kidney
    3. Identify the renal hilum: dissect and divide the renal artery first (Endo-GIA vascular load or clips + divided); then the renal vein
    4. Free the kidney from Gerota's fascia; preserve the adrenal gland if not involved by tumor
    5. Divide the ureter distally with clips; specimen completely freed
    6. Place specimen in endobag; extend one port incision to extract the kidney

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Artery before vein, always: if the renal vein is divided before the artery, the kidney engorges with blood and bleeds dramatically during the remainder of the dissection — this is an unrecoverable mistake during the case; confirm the sequence with the surgeon before handing any stapler or clip near the hilum - Endo-GIA load selection: vascular loads only at the hilum; confirm the load type with the surgeon before every fire - Inferior vena cava proximity: the right renal vein is very short; on the right side, the IVC is immediately medial; if the stapler misfires or the vein tears, this bleeds catastrophically — have a second vascular load immediately at hand during right-side nephrectomy - Endobag integrity: if the bag tears during extraction, tumor cells can seed the port site; handle the specimen carefully; do not force extraction

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Extraction site fascia 0 Vicryl interrupted or running Close securely
    Port sites ≥10 mm fascia 0 Vicryl Carter-Thomason or direct suture
    Skin (all sites) 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular

    Dressing: Small adhesive dressings.

    Robotic-Assisted Radical Prostatectomy (RARP)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, steep Trendelenburg · Avg time: 2.5–4 hrs · Team: console surgeon + bedside scrub tech + circulator · Robot: da Vinci (all robotic arm instruments passed through the assistant port by the scrub tech)

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Robot drapes: sterile robotic arm drapes × 3–4; assist the circulator draping each arm before docking - [ ] Robotic instruments: ProGrasp forceps, monopolar scissors, needle drivers × 2 — confirm all in the room and seated correctly in their cannulas - [ ] 3-0 Monocryl on a UR-6 needle × 2 for the Van Velthoven vesicourethral anastomosis; load BEFORE the case reaches that step - [ ] 2-0 Vicryl on a large CT needle for the DVC suture ligature — this is passed through the assistant port; have it on a loaded needle holder in advance - [ ] Specimen extraction bag (endobag) passed through the assistant port - [ ] JP drain (10 mm Blake or round): suture (2-0 nylon) for securing; placed through an assistant port site at the end - [ ] Foley catheter (18 or 20 Fr) on the back table — placed through the urethra before tying the anastomosis

    Instruments¶

    Instrument Notes
    EndoWrist ProGrasp forceps Tissue grasping and retraction via robotic arm
    EndoWrist monopolar curved scissors Dissection — cold scissors only during nerve-sparing steps
    EndoWrist bipolar Maryland forceps Hemostasis via robotic arm
    EndoWrist large needle drivers × 2 Vesicourethral anastomosis suturing
    Laparoscopic needle holder × 2 Passing sutures through assistant port — DVC 2-0 Vicryl and anastomosis 3-0 Monocryl
    Laparoscopic clip applier (Hem-o-lok clips) Vessel clipping through assistant port
    Laparoscopic suction/irrigation For assistant port
    Specimen endobag Prostate extraction through assistant port
    Robotic arm drapes × 4 Applied before docking
    12 mm trocar × 1 Camera port
    8 mm robotic trocars × 3–4 Robotic arm ports
    10–12 mm assistant port trocar × 1 Bedside scrub tech working port
    Foley catheter (18 or 20 Fr) On back table visible throughout — placed before final anastomosis throw
    JP drain (10 mm Blake drain) Placed near anastomosis at case end
    2-0 nylon JP drain securing suture
    3-0 Monocryl on UR-6 × 2 Van Velthoven anastomosis — pre-load 20 minutes before needed

    Position: Supine; steep Trendelenburg (30–45°). Arms tucked bilaterally. Padded shoulder supports to prevent sliding cephalad. The robot is docked over the patient's lower body after port placement.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; umbilicus to mid-thigh, full width; include the perineum.

    Draping: Laparoscopic lower abdominal draping; sterile robotic arm drapes (applied before docking).

    The Knife: #11 blade for all port incisions. 12 mm camera port just above the umbilicus; three or four 8 mm robotic arm ports in a fan configuration across the lower abdomen; one 10–12 mm assistant port for the bedside scrub tech.

    Retractors: Pneumoperitoneum + steep Trendelenburg displace bowel cephalad. Robotic ProGrasp forceps provide intraoperative retraction. Bedside scrub tech provides additional retraction through the assistant port with a laparoscopic grasper.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Ports placed under direct vision; robot docked to the ports
    2. Incise peritoneum to enter the space of Retzius; bladder falls posteriorly
    3. Divide the endopelvic fascia; ligate the dorsal venous complex (DVC) with 2-0 Vicryl — passed through the assistant port by the scrub tech
    4. Dissect the bladder neck; identify and divide the urethra anteriorly
    5. Dissect the posterior prostate off the rectum; divide posterior urethra and seminal vesicles
    6. Nerve-sparing dissection (if planned): preserve neurovascular bundles laterally using cold scissors only — no energy
    7. Divide the urethra at the apex; remove specimen in an endobag through the assistant port
    8. Vesicourethral anastomosis: 3-0 Monocryl running suture (Van Velthoven — two sutures from each side); Foley catheter placed before the final throw is tied

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Instrument handoff timing: the console surgeon operates continuously; they will call for sutures, clips, and instruments without pausing — have the 3-0 Monocryl for the anastomosis fully loaded and ready 20 minutes before it will be needed; do not make the surgeon wait - DVC suture passing: the 2-0 Vicryl for the DVC ligature must pass through the assistant port on a loaded needle holder; practice the motion for passing through a 12 mm port before the case — the angle matters - Nerve-sparing designation: if the surgeon calls "nerve spare," cold scissors only on that side — no bipolar, no Bovie; hand only what is asked for; do not guess - Anastomosis suture twist: if the 3-0 Monocryl twists during the running anastomosis, the suture must be cut and reloaded — have a backup suture loaded and immediately available - Foley catheter timing: the catheter must be placed into the urethra before the last throw of the anastomosis is tied; if it is not on the field, the case stops — keep it on the back table visible throughout the case

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Pelvic peritoneum 3-0 Vicryl running Close over the anastomosis
    12 mm port site fascia 0 Vicryl Required
    All port site skin 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular
    JP drain 2-0 nylon Placed near the anastomosis; exits through an assistant port site

    Dressing: Small adhesive dressings. Foley catheter remains 5–7 days post-op.

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "In urology, the Approach is often through a natural orifice. The irrigation fluid is your medium, your light, and your retractor all at once — manage it carefully."

    • In TURP, track every liter in and out. A deeply negative fluid balance is a medical emergency, not a notation on the OR record.
    • In nephrectomy, the renal artery is always divided before the vein. This is not a preference — it is the sequence that prevents catastrophic blood loss.
    • In RARP, your job at the bedside is anticipation. The console surgeon cannot stop mid-dissection. Pre-load the anastomosis suture before the specimen is out of the field.
    • Cystoscopy runs on irrigation. An empty bladder collapses the visual field instantly. Keep a backup bag primed and ready to switch without pausing.
