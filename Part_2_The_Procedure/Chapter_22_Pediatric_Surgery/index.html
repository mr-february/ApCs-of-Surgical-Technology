    Chapter 22: Pediatric Surgery¶

    Why Pediatric Surgery Demands a Different Approach¶

    The ApC framework applies universally. But in pediatric surgery, every element must be recalibrated for a patient whose anatomy is smaller, whose physiology is more fragile, and whose tolerance for error is narrower than an adult's.

    Before any pediatric case begins, confirm: - Correct instrument scale (baby instruments, not adult sets) - Correct suture size (3-0 or finer for bowel; 6-0 for vascular structures) - Temperature management is active (warming blanket; warm irrigation; room temp elevated) - Blood volume awareness: ~80 mL/kg; a 4 kg neonate has ~320 mL total blood volume; a 50 mL loss is 15% of their entire volume

    Why it matters at the back table: You are not scrubbing a small adult. The same instrument pressure that holds tissue in an adult crushes it in a neonate. The same room temperature that is fine for an adult drops a neonate's core temp within minutes. Every decision is magnified.

    Pyloric Stenosis — Ramstedt Pyloromyotomy¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine · Avg time: 20–40 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Age: typically 2–8 weeks old · Blood loss: minimal; but any loss in a neonate is significant

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Confirm baby-scale instruments: small Dennis Browne or baby Richardson retractors; Fredet-Ramstedt pyloric spreader (or a fine hemostat as an equivalent) - [ ] #15 blade × 2 for the skin incision and the seromuscular incision; a fresh blade for the pyloromyotomy - [ ] Warming blanket activated under the patient before positioning; warm irrigation saline on the back table - [ ] Mucosal integrity test: anesthesiologist will inject air through the NGT at the end; saline in the wound; watch for bubbles - [ ] 3-0 and 4-0 Vicryl for fascial closure; 5-0 Monocryl for skin; Dermabond on the field as an alternative - [ ] Room temperature elevated before the patient arrives — neonates lose heat rapidly through a large surface-area-to-volume ratio

    Position: Supine; small shoulder roll; head in neutral.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; mid-chest to mid-thigh, full width. Do not apply cold prep solution directly on the infant's skin — minimize exposure time to reduce heat loss.

    Draping: Pediatric laparotomy drape (smaller than the adult equivalent) or small towels + miniaturized aperture sheet. Minimal drape weight — heavy drapes compress small bodies and restrict respiratory movement.

    The Knife: #15 blade. Open approach: small right upper quadrant transverse incision (2–3 cm) or circumumbilical incision at the superior edge of the umbilicus. Laparoscopic approach: umbilical 5 mm port + two 3 mm working ports; #11 blade for the 3 mm incisions.

    Retractors: Open: tiny Denis Browne retractors or small baby Richardson retractors. The pylorus is a small, olive-shaped thickened mass. Retraction must be gentle — crushing the pylorus causes mucosal injury and post-op obstruction. Use the minimum traction necessary to deliver the pylorus.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Deliver the pylorus into the wound with a Babcock clamp or gentle digital pressure from beneath
    2. Incise the serosa and pyloric muscle longitudinally on the avascular anterior surface with a #15 blade; extend from the antrum to the pyloric vein (marks the duodenopyloric junction — do not cross this)
    3. Spread the incised muscle fibers with the pyloric spreader (or fine hemostat) — circular muscle fibers spring apart; the mucosa should bulge into the myotomy without protruding through it
    4. Mucosal integrity test: anesthesiologist injects air through the NGT; surgeon inspects the wound filled with saline for bubbles (air bubbles = perforation)
    5. If mucosa intact: complete; return pylorus to abdomen

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Mucosal perforation: if the spreader tears through the mucosa, the air test will show bubbles; the perforation must be repaired with 4-0 absorbable suture and a Graham patch of omentum if possible; anesthesia must be notified immediately - Myotomy too short: if the incision does not extend far enough toward the duodenum, the stenosis is not fully released; the pyloric vein is the distal landmark — confirm with the surgeon the incision reaches it - Instrument weight on the pylorus: the pylorus in a neonate is tiny; a heavy instrument resting on it will perforate the mucosa without any force applied; keep instruments clear of the structure unless actively using them - Temperature drop during the case: monitor actively; if the baby's temp drops by 2°C, anesthesia may call the pace of the case; have warm irrigation ready

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Fascia / peritoneum 3-0 Vicryl interrupted
    Subcutaneous 4-0 Vicryl
    Skin 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular Or Dermabond + Steri-strips

    Dressing: Small adhesive bandage only. Bulky dressings in infants peel, shift, and are uncomfortable — keep it minimal.

    Pediatric Laparoscopic Appendectomy¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Supine, slight left tilt after trocar placement · Avg time: 20–40 min · Team: surgeon + 1 · Pneumoperitoneum pressure: 8–10 mmHg (lower than adult)

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Confirm instrument sizing: 3–5 mm instruments for small children; 5 mm ports maximum for most pediatric cases - [ ] Pneumoperitoneum pressure setting: confirm 8–10 mmHg with the surgeon — never use adult pressure settings (14–15 mmHg) in small children; high pressure compromises the diaphragm - [ ] Endoloops × 2–3 (2-0 chromic or Vicryl) for appendiceal base ligation; or a small-gauge endo-GIA if the base is thickened - [ ] Specimen bag: confirm a size appropriate for extraction through a 5 mm umbilical port - [ ] 3-0 Vicryl for 5 mm port fascial closure; 5-0 Monocryl for skin; Dermabond available - [ ] Irrigation warm saline: warm, not cold — temperature matters in every pediatric case

    Position: Supine; slight left lateral tilt after trocar placement (gravity displaces the cecum and appendix into view).

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; nipple line to mid-thigh, full width. Keep the infant warm throughout.

    Draping: Pediatric laparoscopic drape; or small towels + miniaturized lap sheet.

    The Knife: #11 or #15 blade. Umbilical 5 mm port (camera). Two 3–5 mm working ports in the RLQ and suprapubic region. Smaller children may be managed with all 3 mm instruments.

    Retractors: Pneumoperitoneum at 8–10 mmHg. Laparoscopic grasper retracts the cecum to expose the appendix. No other retractors needed.

    The Procedure¶

    Same technique as adult laparoscopic appendectomy (Chapter 13), scaled to the smaller anatomy. Appendiceal base ligated with endoloops or a small-gauge endo-GIA. Specimen extracted through the umbilical port in a bag.

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Pneumoperitoneum pressure: confirm the insufflator setting is pediatric-appropriate before creating the pneumoperitoneum; the pediatric diaphragm is easily splinted; high pressure → respiratory compromise in minutes - Port size creep: if the surgeon asks for a 10 mm port for a child under 15 kg, confirm this is intentional; 10 mm port fascial closure in a child requires 2-0 Vicryl and may require a separate stab incision with a fascial closure device - Specimen extraction bag fit: a bag designed for adults will not pass through a 5 mm port; confirm the bag size before opening it

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    5 mm port site fascia 3-0 Vicryl
    Skin 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular Or Dermabond

    Dressing: Small Tegaderm over each port.

    Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH) Repair¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: I (Clean) · Position: Supine, slight contralateral rotation · Avg time: 60–120 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Age: neonate (may be days old) · Left-sided defect is most common (Bochdalek)

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Neonatal instrument set: small Richardson retractors, small Deaver or malleable ribbon retractors — all baby-scaled - [ ] Suture for diaphragm repair: 2-0 or 3-0 Prolene or Ethibond interrupted for primary repair; confirm if Gore-Tex patch is needed (large defect); have both ready - [ ] Gore-Tex patch: confirm size and thickness if the defect is known to be large; keep it sterile until needed - [ ] Temperature management: warming blanket active; room temperature elevated; warm irrigation; overhead warming lamp if available - [ ] Monitor blood loss volume meticulously: 10 mL of blood in a 3 kg neonate is >4% of their total blood volume

    Position: Supine; slightly rotated toward the contralateral side. Operative arm extended. Most CDH repairs are left-sided.

    Prep: CHG/alcohol; axilla to mid-thigh on the operative side; include the entire abdomen.

    Draping: Four towels framing a subcostal incision → pediatric laparotomy sheet. Infant warming measures throughout.

    The Knife: #15 blade. Left subcostal incision (2–4 cm below and parallel to the costal margin); or transverse upper abdominal incision for large defects.

    Retractors: Small baby Richardson retractors for the abdominal wall. Small Deaver or malleable ribbon retractor for the liver and stomach. Gentle — neonatal organs are soft and easily contused.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Enter the abdomen; identify herniated contents in the chest (bowel, stomach, spleen, sometimes the left lobe of the liver)
    2. Gently reduce herniated organs back into the abdomen — no traction; neonatal organs tear easily
    3. Inspect the diaphragm defect: size, location, and quality of diaphragmatic tissue at the margins
    4. Primary repair (small-to-moderate defects): horizontal mattress sutures (2-0 or 3-0 Prolene or Ethibond); interrupted; close the defect without tension
    5. Patch repair (large defects): Gore-Tex patch sutured to the diaphragmatic margins
    6. Confirm repair is tension-free; irrigate; check hemostasis

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Temperature crash: neonatal CDH cases are long; the baby's core temperature must be checked every 15 minutes; a drop of 2°C is a physiological emergency that increases coagulopathy and cardiac instability; maintain all warming measures throughout - Lung hypoplasia: the ipsilateral lung is underdeveloped and will not fully expand at the time of repair; do not expect normal lung expansion on the operative side; the anesthesia team manages ventilation carefully to avoid high pressures - Organ fragility during reduction: the herniated bowel may be ischemic if it has been in the chest long; handle with extreme gentleness; Babcock clamps or very loose gauze contact only - Tension on the repair: if the diaphragm margins are too far apart for primary closure without tension, a patch is required; do not let the surgeon struggle with a primary repair that will dehisce — have the patch open and on the field

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Fascia / musculoaponeurotic 2-0 or 3-0 Vicryl interrupted
    Subcutaneous 3-0 Vicryl
    Skin 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular Or skin glue
    Chest drain (if placed) Small-bore catheter (10–14 Fr) Many surgeons do NOT place a chest tube; surgeon preference

    PSARP — Imperforate Anus (Posterior Sagittal Anorectoplasty)¶

    QUICK FACTS Wound Class: II (Clean-Contaminated) · Position: Prone (jackknife/Kraske) · Avg time: 60–120 min · Team: surgeon + 1 assistant · Nerve stimulator: required for sphincter mapping · Critical anatomy: external sphincter complex must be preserved

    The Approach¶

    BEFORE YOU SCRUB - [ ] Nerve stimulator (Pena stimulator): confirm it is in the room and functional before positioning the patient; test the response in a visible muscle before the surgical field is draped - [ ] Gelpi retractors: × 2 minimum for holding the posterior sagittal wound open - [ ] #15 blade for the posterior sagittal incision - [ ] 3-0 and 4-0 Vicryl for muscle and sphincter re-approximation - [ ] Betadine irrigation for the bowel/rectal field (contaminated bowel if the fistula is entered) - [ ] 5-0 Monocryl or 5-0 Vicryl for skin closure around the neo-anus - [ ] Bowel bag or towel to isolate the rectum once mobilized (contaminated organ)

    Position: Prone; jackknife (Kraske) position; hips elevated as the highest point; table broken. Tape may be placed across the buttocks and secured to the OR table sides to part them and improve midline exposure.

    Prep: Betadine; entire perineum, buttocks, and perianal region.

    Draping: Prone perineal drape; buttocks exposed within the aperture. Small clear adhesive aperture drape may be placed over the anus.

    The Knife: #15 blade. Midline posterior sagittal incision from the lower sacrum through the external sphincter muscle complex to the perineum.

    Retractors: Gelpi retractors (self-retaining) hold the posterior sagittal wound open. The nerve stimulator is the critical guide — low-current probe touched to the muscle surfaces; when on the sphincter, the anus visibly contracts; this maps where cuts are made and where they must not go.

    The Procedure¶

    1. Midline posterior sagittal incision; use the nerve stimulator to map the external sphincter complex before cutting any muscle
    2. Divide midline structures precisely — gluteus muscles, levator ani, puborectalis — always exactly in the midline (Pena technique: each structure split midline to preserve bilateral symmetry)
    3. Identify the rectum (or rectal fistula); dissect free; mobilize sufficient length to reach the perineum without tension
    4. Divide the fistula to the urethra, bladder, or vagina (malformation type determines location)
    5. Anoplasty: place the mobilized rectum in the center of the sphincter muscle complex; fix with interrupted sutures anchoring the rectal wall to the sphincter; create a neo-anus of the correct caliber

    ⚠ WATCH FOR - Nerve stimulator guidance before every cut: the surgeon must use the stimulator before cutting any muscle in the midline; do not hand the scalpel until the surgeon has confirmed the anatomy; a cut placed outside the midline destroys sphincter function - Fistula entry to the genitourinary tract: if the rectum opens into the urethra or bladder, urine and bowel contents are in the field; isolate the bowel with a towel or bowel bag; maintain strict sterile technique - Length of rectal mobilization: if the rectum is too short to reach the perineum without tension, the anoplasty sutures will tear; alert the surgeon if the bowel appears to be under tension during the anoplasty suture placement - Neo-anus caliber: if the opening is too tight, the sutures are being placed too close to the center; if too loose, the repair will not function; the surgeon will calibrate with a Hegar dilator

    The Closure¶

    Layer Suture Notes
    Levator ani / puborectalis / external sphincter 3-0 Vicryl interrupted Precisely re-approximated in the midline
    Subcutaneous 3-0 or 4-0 Vicryl
    Skin 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular or 5-0 Vicryl interrupted Around the neo-anus

    Dressing: Very light gauze + non-adherent. Any dressing in the perineum must not obstruct the neo-anus.

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "In pediatrics, the Approach is not just surgical — it's thermal, metabolic, and physiological. A cold baby on a poorly warmed table, with adult instruments, sutured with adult suture sizes, will fail in ways that have nothing to do with the surgeon's technique."

    • In pyloromyotomy, the mucosa is the boundary between success and perforation. The spreader applies force against muscle fibers. If you feel resistance increase suddenly and the surgeon stops, that is the mucosa pushing back — not an invitation to apply more force.
    • For PSARP, the nerve stimulator is your Approach guide. Never cut in the midline without confirming sphincter location first. Confirm its function before draping.
    • Temperature in neonatal cases is not a background detail. Check it actively. A 2°C drop in a 3 kg neonate is a physiological emergency.
    • Count blood loss in mL per sponge. A sponge soaked in an adult case is minor. In a neonate, a soaked 4×4 may represent 10% of the patient's blood volume.
