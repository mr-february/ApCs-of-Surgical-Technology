# Chapter 22: Pediatric Surgery

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Apply the ApC framework to common pediatric surgical procedures.
2. Explain how the Approach differs for pediatric patients — from padding to temperature to instrument scale.
3. Describe the specific draping, incision, and retractors for Ramstedt pyloromyotomy, appendectomy, and CDH repair.
4. Identify the physiological differences that alter every element of the ApC framework in neonates and children.

---

## 22.1 Why Pediatric Surgery Demands a Different Approach

The three-part ApC structure applies universally — but in pediatric surgery, every element of the Approach must be recalibrated for a patient whose anatomy is smaller, whose physiology is more fragile, and whose tolerance for error is narrower than an adult's.

**Before any pediatric case begins, the scrub tech must verify:**
- Correct instrument scale (baby instruments, not standard adult sets)
- Correct suture size (3-0 or finer for bowel; 6-0 for vascular structures)
- Temperature management is set up (warming blanket active; warm irrigation prepared; room temperature elevated)
- Blood volume calculated: ~80 mL/kg; a 4 kg neonate has ~320 mL total blood; 50 mL blood loss = 15% of their volume

## 22.2 Pyloric Stenosis — Ramstedt Pyloromyotomy

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; small shoulder roll under the shoulders; head in neutral.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep from mid-chest to mid-thigh, full width. Smaller prep area than an adult — do not use cold prep solution directly on an infant's skin; allow it to warm slightly or prep quickly to minimize heat loss.

**Draping:**
- A **pediatric laparotomy drape** (smaller than adult equivalent) or custom draping with four small towels + a small aperture sheet
- Minimal drape weight — heavy drapes compress small bodies and restrict respiratory movement
- Warm the infant with a **warming blanket** placed before the drapes; remove when the field is established

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard; smaller glove sizes are typical.

**The Knife:** #15 blade on a #3 handle. The incision is **small and precise**:
- **Open approach:** small right upper quadrant transverse incision (2–3 cm), or circumumbilical incision at the superior edge of the umbilicus
- **Laparoscopic approach:** umbilical 5 mm port + two 3 mm working ports (right and left upper quadrant); #11 blade for the 3 mm incisions; or a single 5 mm umbilical incision for a single-incision technique

**Retractors:**
- **Open:** tiny Denis Browne retractors or small baby Richardson retractors hold the wound edges; an assistant holds steady retraction throughout the case
- The pylorus is a small, thickened olive-shaped mass — retraction must be gentle; crushing the pylorus causes mucosal injury and post-op obstruction

---

### The Procedure

1. Deliver the pylorus into the wound with a babcock clamp or gentle digital pressure from beneath
2. Incise the serosa and pyloric muscle longitudinally (along the avascular anterior surface) with a #15 blade; extend from the antrum to just proximal to the pyloric vein (marks the duodenopyloric junction)
3. Spread the incised muscle fibers with a pyloric spreader (a small hemostat or dedicated Fredet-Ramstedt spreader) — the circular muscle fibers spring apart; the mucosa should bulge into the myotomy without bulging through it
4. **Test mucosal integrity:** the anesthesiologist injects air into the NGT; the surgeon inspects for air bubbles in saline-filled wound (perforation = bubbles)
5. If mucosa is intact: complete

---

### The Closure

**Fascial/peritoneal layer:** 3-0 Vicryl interrupted.

**Subcutaneous:** 4-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular; or skin glue (Dermabond) + Steri-strips.

**Dressing:** Small adhesive bandage; no bulky dressings (infant will move against any taping).

---

## 22.3 Pediatric Laparoscopic Appendectomy

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; slight left lateral tilt after trocar placement (gravity displaces the cecum and appendix into view).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep from nipple line to mid-thigh, full width. Keep infant warm.

**Draping:** Pediatric laparoscopic drape; or small towels + miniaturized lap sheet.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard (smaller sizes).

**The Knife:** #11 blade or #15 blade:
- Umbilical 5 mm port (primary camera)
- Two 3–5 mm working ports in the RLQ and suprapubic region
- Smaller children may be done with all 3 mm instruments (micro-laparoscopy)

**Retractors:** Pneumoperitoneum at 8–10 mmHg (lower pressure than adult, as pediatric diaphragm is more easily compromised by high intraabdominal pressure). Laparoscopic grasper retracts the cecum to expose the appendix.

---

### The Procedure

Same technique as adult (see Chapter 13), scaled to smaller anatomy. The appendiceal base may be ligated with endoloops or a small-gauge endo-GIA. The specimen is extracted through the umbilical port.

---

### The Closure

**5 mm port site fascia:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular; or Dermabond.

**Dressing:** Small Tegaderm over each port.

---

## 22.4 Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH) Repair

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; slightly rotated toward the contralateral side; arm on the operative side extended. Most CDH repairs are **left-sided** (Bochdalek defect — the left posterolateral diaphragm failed to close).

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; prep from axilla to mid-thigh on the operative side; include the entire abdomen.

**Draping:**
- Four towels framing a **subcostal incision** on the affected side (or a midline incision for large defects)
- Pediatric laparotomy sheet
- Infant warming measures throughout

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard (appropriate small sizes for neonatal instruments).

**The Knife:** #15 blade on a #3 handle; **left subcostal incision** (2–4 cm below and parallel to the costal margin); or transverse upper abdominal incision.

**Retractors:**
- Small baby Richardson retractors retract the abdominal wall
- A **Deaver retractor** (small size) or malleable ribbon retractor retracts the liver and stomach out of the field as the diaphragm is exposed
- The diaphragm defect itself is the operative target — once the herniated organs are reduced back into the abdomen, the defect margins become the working field

---

### The Procedure

1. Enter the abdomen; identify herniated abdominal contents in the chest (bowel, stomach, spleen, liver lobe depending on defect size)
2. Gently reduce the herniated organs back into the abdomen (no traction — neonatal organs are fragile)
3. Inspect the diaphragm defect: size, location, and quality of diaphragmatic tissue on each margin
4. **Primary repair** (small-to-moderate defects): horizontal mattress sutures (2-0 or 3-0 Prolene or Ethibond) close the defect; interrupted sutures preferred for strength
5. **Patch repair** (large defects, absent diaphragm posterolaterally): a **Gore-Tex patch** or autologous muscle flap is sutured to the diaphragmatic margins
6. Confirm repair is tension-free; irrigate; check hemostasis

---

### The Closure

**Fascial / musculoaponeurotic layer:** 2-0 or 3-0 Vicryl interrupted.

**Subcutaneous:** 3-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular; or skin glue.

**Chest drain:** Many surgeons do NOT place a chest drain (lung expansion occurs spontaneously with time and positive pressure ventilation); if placed, it is a small-bore catheter (10–14 Fr) connected to water seal drainage at negative pressure.

## 22.5 Posterior Sagittal Anorectoplasty (PSARP) — Imperforate Anus

### The Approach

**Position:** **Prone** (jackknife/Kraske position); hips elevated as the highest point; table broken; buttocks elevated; tape may be placed across the buttocks and secured to the OR table sides to part them and improve midline exposure.

**Prep:** Betadine; prep the entire perineum, buttocks, and perianal region.

**Draping:**
- Prone perineal drape; the buttocks are exposed within the aperture
- A **small clear adhesive aperture drape** may be placed over the anus

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #15 blade on a #3 handle; **midline posterior sagittal incision** from the lower sacrum through the external sphincter muscle complex to the perineum (or to the perineal body anteriorly).

**Retractors:**
- **Gelpi retractors** (self-retaining; placed to hold the posterior sagittal wound open)
- A **nerve stimulator (Pena stimulator):** low-current electrical probe that is touched to the muscle surfaces — when on the sphincter muscle, the anus contracts visibly, confirming correct anatomy; the surgeon maps the sphincter complex before cutting through any muscle
- The nerve stimulator is the critical "retractor equivalent" — it guides where cuts are made

---

### The Procedure

1. Midline posterior sagittal incision; identify and map the external sphincter complex with the nerve stimulator
2. Divide midline structures precisely: gluteus muscles, levator ani, puborectalis — each structure split exactly in the midline (Pena technique: always in the midline to preserve both sides)
3. Identify the rectum (or rectal fistula); dissect the rectum from surrounding structures; mobilize sufficient length
4. Divide the fistula to the urethra, bladder, or vagina (depending on the malformation type)
5. **Anoplasty:** place the mobilized rectum in the center of the sphincter muscle complex; fix with multiple interrupted sutures anchoring the rectal wall to the sphincter; create a neo-anus of appropriate caliber

---

### The Closure

**Levator ani, puborectalis, external sphincter:** 3-0 Vicryl interrupted; precisely re-approximated in the midline.

**Subcutaneous:** 3-0 or 4-0 Vicryl.

**Skin:** 5-0 Monocryl subcuticular or 5-0 Vicryl interrupted.

**Dressing:** Very light gauze + non-adherent; any dressing in the perineum must not obstruct the neo-anus.

---

## 22.6 Nissen Fundoplication (Laparoscopic)

### The Approach

**Position:** Supine; slight reverse Trendelenburg; both arms tucked.

**Prep:** CHG/alcohol; abdomen from nipples to pubis.

**Draping:** Pediatric laparoscopic draping.

**Gown and Gloves:** Standard.

**The Knife:** #11 blade; umbilical camera port (5 mm); three to four additional 3–5 mm working ports in the upper abdomen.

**Retractors:** Pneumoperitoneum at 8–10 mmHg; a liver retractor placed through one port to elevate the left lobe of the liver and expose the esophageal hiatus.

---

### The Procedure

1. Dissect the esophageal hiatus; identify and preserve the anterior and posterior vagus nerves
2. Dissect the short gastric vessels (divide with energy device)
3. Mobilize the gastric fundus; bring it posterior to the esophagus
4. Wrap the fundus 360° around the lower esophagus (Nissen) or 270° (Toupet, posterior partial wrap)
5. Suture the wrap: 2-0 Ethibond interrupted sutures; 3 sutures fix the wrap to itself and to the esophageal muscular wall
6. Crural repair: if hiatal hernia present, close the crura with 2-0 Ethibond behind the esophagus

---

### The Closure

**5 mm ports ≥5 mm:** 3-0 Vicryl fascial closure.

**Skin:** 5-0 Monocryl; or skin glue.

**Dressing:** Small Tegaderm over each port.

## Clinical Pearls

> "In pediatrics, the Approach is not just surgical — it's thermal, metabolic, and psychological. A cold baby on a poorly warmed table, with adult instruments, sutured with adult suture sizes, will fail in ways that have nothing to do with the surgeon's technique."

- In a pyloromyotomy, the mucosa is the boundary between success and perforation. Pass instruments carefully; the pylorus is held, not grasped.
- For PSARP, the nerve stimulator is your Approach guide. Never cut in the midline without confirming where the sphincter is first.
- Temperature during a neonatal CDH repair: check it every 15 minutes. A temperature drop of 2°C in a neonate is a physiological emergency.
