## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Microorganism** | A living organism too small to be seen with the naked eye |
| **Pathogen** | A microorganism capable of causing disease |
| **Bacteria** | Single-celled prokaryotic organisms; the most common cause of SSIs |
| **Spore** | A resistant, dormant bacterial form capable of surviving harsh conditions (e.g., *C. difficile*, *B. anthracis*) |
| **Biofilm** | A structured community of microorganisms adhering to surfaces, protected by a polysaccharide matrix |
| **Surgical Site Infection (SSI)** | An infection occurring within 30–90 days of surgery at the site of the incision or deep tissue |
| **Endogenous infection** | Infection from the patient's own normal flora |
| **Exogenous infection** | Infection from an outside source (personnel, instruments, environment) |
| **Standard Precautions** | Infection control practices applied to all patients regardless of diagnosis |
| **Transmission-Based Precautions** | Additional precautions applied based on the known or suspected route of pathogen transmission |
| **Asepsis** | The absence of pathogenic microorganisms |
| **Aseptic technique** | Practices designed to prevent contamination of the sterile field |

## 3.1 Microbiology Basics: A Surgical Perspective

Microbiology is the study of organisms too small to be seen without magnification. For surgical technologists, microbiology is not an abstract science — it is the direct foundation for every aseptic practice performed in the OR. Understanding *why* we do what we do begins with understanding what we are fighting.

### Categories of Microorganisms

**Bacteria** are single-celled organisms and the primary causative agents of surgical site infections. They are classified in multiple ways:

*By Gram stain:*
- **Gram-positive**: thick peptidoglycan cell wall retains crystal violet stain (purple); e.g., *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus pyogenes*
- **Gram-negative**: thin peptidoglycan wall with outer lipopolysaccharide layer; pink stain; e.g., *Escherichia coli*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Klebsiella*

*By morphology:*
- **Cocci**: spherical (e.g., *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus*)
- **Bacilli**: rod-shaped (e.g., *Clostridium*, *E. coli*)
- **Spirochetes**: spiral-shaped (e.g., *Treponema pallidum*)

*By oxygen requirement:*
- **Aerobic**: require oxygen to survive
- **Anaerobic**: grow in the absence of oxygen (important in bowel cases, deep tissue infections)
- **Facultative anaerobes**: can survive with or without oxygen

**Viruses** are not living cells — they are genetic material (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein coat. Viruses cannot be treated with antibiotics. Relevant examples include HIV, Hepatitis B (HBV), and Hepatitis C (HCV) — all bloodborne pathogens with direct implications for OR personnel safety.

**Fungi** include yeasts and molds. Candida is a common opportunistic fungal pathogen in immunocompromised surgical patients. Fungal infections post-surgery are often hospital-acquired and serious.

**Prions** are misfolded proteins associated with diseases like Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD). Prions are extraordinary resistant to standard sterilization methods, including autoclaving. Suspected CJD cases require special instrument handling protocols.

## 3.2 Surgical Site Infections (SSIs)

A **surgical site infection (SSI)** is an infection that occurs at or near a surgical incision within 30 days of the procedure (or within 90 days if an implant is involved). SSIs are one of the most common and costly healthcare-associated infections (HAIs).

### Classification of SSIs
| Type | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Superficial incisional** | Involves only skin and subcutaneous tissue |
| **Deep incisional** | Involves fascial and muscle layers |
| **Organ/space** | Involves any anatomy opened or manipulated during surgery (e.g., peritonitis, mediastinitis) |

### Common SSI Pathogens
| Organism | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| *Staphylococcus aureus* | Most common SSI pathogen; MRSA is a serious concern |
| *Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus* | Common in implant-related SSIs |
| *Enterococcus* | GI/GU procedures |
| *E. coli* | Abdominal/colorectal procedures |
| *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* | Resistant; associated with contaminated environments and immunocompromise |

### SSI Risk Factors

*Patient factors:*
- Diabetes mellitus (impaired immune response, poor tissue healing)
- Obesity (poor vascularity in adipose tissue)
- Smoking
- Immunosuppression
- Malnutrition
- Prolonged preoperative hospital stay

*Surgical factors:*
- Lengthy procedures (>2 hours)
- Contaminated or dirty wound classification
- Poor technique or excessive tissue trauma
- Inadequate hemostasis
- Foreign material left in wound

### SSI Prevention Bundle
Evidence-based practices shown to reduce SSI rates:
- Prophylactic antibiotic administration within **60 minutes before incision** (vancomycin requires 120 minutes)
- Antibiotic redosing for prolonged cases
- Hair removal with clippers (never razors — razors cause microabrasions that increase infection risk)
- Normothermia maintenance (hypothermia impairs immune function)
- Glucose control in diabetic and non-diabetic patients
- Chlorhexidine gluconate (CHG) patient skin prep
- Sterile technique

## 3.3 Chain of Infection

For an infection to occur, six conditions must exist in sequence. This is called the **chain of infection**. Breaking any link in the chain prevents infection.

```
Infectious Agent → Reservoir → Portal of Exit → Mode of Transmission → Portal of Entry → Susceptible Host
```

| Link | Example in the OR |
|------|-----------------|
| **Infectious agent** | *S. aureus* on a personnel member's skin |
| **Reservoir** | Nasal colonization of OR staff |
| **Portal of exit** | Shedding of skin cells, sneezing |
| **Mode of transmission** | Airborne droplets contaminating the sterile field |
| **Portal of entry** | Open surgical wound |
| **Susceptible host** | Immunocompromised patient |

Every aseptic practice in the OR targets one or more links in this chain.

## 3.4 Modes of Transmission

Understanding how pathogens travel helps explain why OR practices exist.

### Contact Transmission
- **Direct contact**: physical transfer between source and host (e.g., contaminated hand touching a wound)
- **Indirect contact**: transfer via a contaminated intermediate object (fomite) such as an instrument, drape, or countertop

Contact transmission is the most common route for SSIs and HAIs. Hand hygiene and aseptic technique directly interrupt it.

### Droplet Transmission
Large respiratory droplets (>5 microns) travel short distances (<3 feet) and fall quickly. Examples: influenza, *Streptococcus*. Surgical masks protect against droplet transmission — both from personnel to patient and patient to personnel.

### Airborne Transmission
Small particles (≤5 microns) remain suspended in air for extended periods and can travel long distances. Examples: tuberculosis (*Mycobacterium tuberculosis*), measles, varicella. N95 respirators (not standard surgical masks) are required for airborne precautions.

### Vector-Borne Transmission
Transmission via insects or animals (e.g., mosquitoes, ticks). Not a primary concern in the OR setting.

## 3.5 Standard Precautions

**Standard precautions** are the minimum infection control practices applied to all patients, regardless of their known or suspected infectious status. The principle is that all blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions (except sweat), non-intact skin, and mucous membranes are treated as potentially infectious.

### Components of Standard Precautions

**Hand Hygiene**
The single most effective infection control intervention. In the OR:
- Use **alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR)** when hands are not visibly soiled
- Use **soap and water** when hands are visibly soiled or after exposure to *C. difficile* spores (alcohol does not kill spores)
- Perform hand hygiene before and after every patient contact, after glove removal, after touching environmental surfaces

**Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)**
| PPE | When Used |
|-----|-----------|
| **Gloves** | When touching blood, body fluids, or mucous membranes |
| **Gown** | When splashing or soiling of clothing is anticipated |
| **Mask** | When working in restricted zone with open sterile field; when patient has respiratory illness |
| **Eye protection** | When splash or spray is possible |
| **Face shield** | Covers entire face; used when extensive splash risk is present |

**Safe Injection Practices**
- Never recap needles with two hands; use a scoop technique or safety device
- Use single-use vials whenever possible
- Label all medications on the sterile field immediately upon receipt

**Sharps Safety**
Needlestick injuries represent one of the most significant occupational hazards for surgical personnel. Prevention strategies:
- Never pass sharps hand-to-hand; use a neutral zone (kidney basin) on the field
- Announce sharps before passing ("scalpel coming")
- Use blunt-tip suture needles when indicated
- Count all sharps meticulously

## 3.6 Transmission-Based Precautions

When a patient's known or suspected pathogen requires precautions beyond standard, **transmission-based precautions** are implemented. In the OR, these are implemented in addition to — not instead of — standard precautions.

| Precaution Type | Route | PPE Required | OR Implications |
|-----------------|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Contact** | Direct/indirect contact | Gown + gloves | Last case of the day if possible; dedicated equipment; terminal clean |
| **Droplet** | Respiratory droplets | Surgical mask | Standard OR mask is sufficient |
| **Airborne** | Airborne particles | N95 respirator | Negative pressure room if available; fit-tested N95 required |

### MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*)
MRSA is a contact precaution pathogen with significant surgical implications. MRSA colonization is common (approximately 1–2% of the population) and can result in serious SSIs. Preoperative MRSA screening (nasal swabs) and decolonization with mupirocin ointment and CHG bathing is used at many institutions before elective surgery.

## 3.7 Surgical Hand Antisepsis

Before gowning and gloving, the scrub technologist performs **surgical hand antisepsis** — a more thorough process than routine hand hygiene, designed to reduce transient flora and suppress the growth of resident flora for the duration of glove wear.

### Traditional Surgical Scrub
Uses an antiseptic soap (chlorhexidine gluconate or povidone-iodine) and a scrub brush or pick:
1. Remove jewelry, inspect hands and nails (no cuts, no nail polish, no artificial nails)
2. Pre-wash hands and forearms with water
3. Clean under nails with nail pick
4. Apply antiseptic agent; use timed scrub (3–5 minutes per institution policy) OR counted stroke method
5. Scrub each surface of each finger, hand, and forearm to 2 inches above the elbow
6. Rinse hands and forearms, fingers up, keeping hands above elbows
7. Dry with sterile towel, using a patting motion from fingertips to elbows

### Alcohol-Based Surgical Hand Rub (ABHR)
An increasingly common alternative, validated by studies to be as effective as traditional scrub:
1. Pre-wash with soap and water on the first scrub of the day; dry thoroughly
2. Clean under fingernails
3. Apply ABHR per manufacturer instructions, covering all surfaces of hands and forearms
4. Allow to dry completely before gowning and gloving (do not wipe dry)
5. Repeat application per product instructions

### Key Principles
- Hands must remain higher than elbows during and after scrubbing to prevent runoff contamination
- Never re-contaminate scrubbed hands by touching unscrubbed surfaces
- If contamination occurs after scrubbing, rescrub

## Clinical Pearls

> "The organism doesn't know it's not supposed to be there. You have to make sure it never gets the chance."

- The most common SSI pathogen (*S. aureus*) lives on the skin of approximately 30% of the healthy population. Your prep technique, your sterile field, and your technique matter.
- Contact precaution cases should ideally be scheduled last if possible, allowing a terminal cleaning of the room afterward.
- When in doubt about the infectious status of a patient, apply standard precautions fully. You cannot tell by looking who is colonized with MRSA or who has an undiagnosed bloodborne pathogen.
