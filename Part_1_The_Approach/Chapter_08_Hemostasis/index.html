    Chapter 8: Hemostasis¶

    8.1 Physiologic Hemostasis¶

    Before examining surgical hemostasis, it is useful to understand the body's own mechanisms. When a vessel is injured:

    1. Vascular spasm: the injured vessel constricts immediately, reducing blood flow
    2. Platelet plug formation: platelets adhere to the damaged vessel wall and aggregate, forming a temporary plug
    3. Coagulation cascade: a series of clotting factor reactions (intrinsic and extrinsic pathways) that culminate in the conversion of fibrinogen to fibrin, forming a stable clot

    Surgical hemostasis supports and supplements these mechanisms when bleeding exceeds the body's capacity or speed to respond.

    8.2 Mechanical Hemostasis¶

    Mechanical methods physically control bleeding by compression, ligation, or occlusion.

    Direct Pressure¶

    The simplest hemostatic technique: apply a sponge (laparotomy pad, raytec) with pressure to the bleeding site. Effective for diffuse oozing, venous bleeding, and as a temporizing measure while a more definitive technique is prepared.

    The scrub tech maintains a ready supply of damp sponges and anticipates requests for pressure application.

    Ligatures and Ties¶

    A tie is a length of suture used to ligate (encircle and close off) a vessel or tissue pedicle. The surgeon clamps the vessel with a hemostat, the scrub tech passes a free tie (suture without a needle), and the surgeon ties it around the clamped tissue before releasing the clamp.

    Types of ties: - Free tie: suture passed on the field and tied by the surgeon - Tie on a passer (stick tie): suture loaded onto a needle holder or clamp and passed to the surgeon for suture ligation of a specific vessel; used when a free tie might slip

    Passing a free tie: Hold the suture between both hands, pull it taut, and present it to the surgeon's dominant hand over the clamped vessel. Avoid letting the suture tail trail in the wound.

    Hemostatic Clips¶

    Metallic or absorbable polymer clips applied with a clip applier to mechanically occlude vessels without heat or suture. Rapid and precise. Titanium clips remain permanently; polymer clips (e.g., Hem-o-lok) absorb over months.

    Bone Wax¶

    A mixture of beeswax and isopropyl palmitate applied directly to cut bone surfaces to mechanically tamponade bleeding from the Haversian canals. Used in craniotomies, sternotomies, and orthopedic cases. Bone wax is a foreign body and should be used sparingly; it inhibits bone healing and may increase infection risk.

    On the sterile field: bone wax is soft and may be rolled into a small ball for easy application. Passed on a periosteal elevator or directly.

    Tourniquet¶

    A pneumatic cuff applied to an extremity to create a bloodless operative field. Used in orthopedic and vascular extremity cases. - Applied over cast padding above the operative site - Inflated to a pressure above systolic BP (typically 50–75 mmHg above) - Maximum safe inflation time is typically 90–120 minutes for upper extremities, up to 2 hours for lower extremities (institution and surgeon dependent) - Deflation is documented; reperfusion injury and tourniquet paralysis are potential complications

    8.3 Thermal Hemostasis: Electrosurgery¶

    Electrosurgical units (ESUs) use high-frequency alternating electrical current (300 kHz–3 MHz — well above the frequency range that causes cardiac arrhythmia) to thermally coagulate or vaporize tissue.

    Monopolar Electrosurgery¶

    In monopolar electrosurgery, current flows: Generator → Active electrode (pencil tip) → Patient tissue → Dispersive electrode (grounding pad) → Generator

    The current is concentrated at the active electrode tip (creating heat) and dispersed over the large surface area of the grounding pad (preventing burns there).

    Modes: - Cut mode: continuous waveform; heats cells rapidly, causing vaporization — smooth cutting - Coag mode: pulsed waveform; heats more slowly, causing desiccation/coagulation without vaporization — more char, less cutting - Blend mode: alternating cut and coag waveforms; simultaneous cutting and coagulation

    Dispersive electrode (grounding pad): - Must be placed on a fleshy, well-vascularized, hair-free area - Placed as close to the operative site as practical - Must have complete, uniform skin contact — any gap concentrates current and causes a burn - Never place over bony prominences, scar tissue, metal implants, or ECG electrodes

    ESU safety: - Active pencil tip must be placed in a safety holster when not in use - Never activate the ESU when not in contact with or near tissue — arcing can cause fires - Smoke generated by ESU (surgical plume) contains toxic byproducts — use smoke evacuation systems - Be cautious near oxygen-enriched environments (see fire safety, Chapter 2)

    On the sterile field: The scrub tech receives the ESU pencil and cord, secures the cord to the drape, and places the pencil in the holster on the field. Extra tips may be opened during the case. Clean the tip as needed with a scratch pad (not a blade).

    Bipolar Electrosurgery¶

    In bipolar electrosurgery, current flows only between the two tines of the bipolar forceps. There is no dispersive electrode needed, and current does not travel through the patient's body beyond the immediate tissue in the forceps.

    Advantages over monopolar: - More precise; less collateral thermal damage - Safe for use near critical structures (nerves, vessels, brain) - No dispersive electrode required

    Disadvantages: - No cutting capability; only coagulation - Less effective on large vessels

    Common uses: neurosurgery, microsurgery, ophthalmic surgery, cases involving nerves or delicate structures

    Argon Beam Coagulator (ABC)¶

    An ESU variant in which a stream of argon gas is used to deliver the coagulation current. The ionized argon conducts the current to the tissue surface, improving hemostasis while reducing char and tissue depth.

    Useful for liver, spleen, and other highly vascular solid organs where diffuse surface bleeding is the problem.

    8.4 Chemical and Topical Hemostatic Agents¶

    When mechanical and thermal methods are insufficient or inappropriate, topical hemostatic agents provide additional coagulation support.

    Agent Type Mechanism Examples Notes
    Gelatin sponge (Gelfoam) Absorbable gelatin Provides scaffold for clot formation Gelfoam Absorbed in 4–6 weeks; may be soaked in thrombin
    Oxidized cellulose (Surgicel) Absorbable cellulose Lowers local pH; mechanical and biochemical hemostasis Surgicel, Surgicel Nu-Knit Absorbed in 7–14 days
    Collagen sponge/pad (Helistat, Instat) Absorbable collagen Activates platelet aggregation Helistat Effective for diffuse oozing
    Thrombin (topical) Enzyme Converts fibrinogen to fibrin Thrombin-JMI, Evithrom Applied alone or combined with gelatin sponge (FloSeal, Surgiflo)
    Fibrin sealant (Tisseel, Evicel) Two-component glue Fibrin clot formation mimicking final coagulation step Tisseel Two syringes mix at tip; used for anastomoses, surface bleeding
    Bone wax Mechanical Physical tamponade of bone canals — See Section 8.2
    Epinephrine (local) Vasoconstrictive Arteriolar constriction In local anesthetics Added to local anesthetic for control of tissue bleeding

    Handling hemostatic agents on the sterile field: - Know what is open on your field and where it is - Label all medications (including thrombin) immediately when received from the circulator - Thrombin must never be injected intravenously — it causes systemic coagulation and is potentially fatal. Keep thrombin clearly separated from injectable medications. - Prepare Gelfoam soaked in thrombin in a small basin, not on the open instrument field

    8.5 Laser Surgery¶

    LASER stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. Lasers produce a coherent, monochromatic, highly focused beam of light that can cut, vaporize, coagulate, or ablate tissue depending on wavelength and power settings.

    Common Surgical Lasers¶

    Laser Wavelength Color Target Common Uses
    CO₂ laser 10,600 nm Infrared Water (soft tissue) ENT, gynecology, dermatology, microsurgery
    Nd:YAG laser 1,064 nm Near-infrared Pigmented tissue GI, urology, ophthalmology
    Argon laser 488–514 nm Visible blue-green Hemoglobin/melanin Ophthalmology (retinal), dermatology
    KTP laser 532 nm Green Hemoglobin ENT, urology
    Holmium:YAG (Ho:YAG) 2,100 nm Near-infrared Water Urology (lithotripsy), arthroscopy, spine
    Diode laser 800–980 nm Near-infrared Various ENT, ophthalmology
    Excimer laser 193–351 nm Ultraviolet Tissue surface Ophthalmology (LASIK)

    Laser Hazards¶

    Hazard Risk Control
    Eye injury Laser energy can cause retinal burns or corneal damage, even from reflected beams Wavelength-appropriate protective eyewear required for all personnel and patient; signs posted outside OR
    Skin/tissue burn Misdirected beam can burn skin, drapes, or deep tissue Cover non-target tissue with moist towels; use laser-specific drapes
    Fire Oxygen-enriched environment + laser beam Wet sponges around operative site; use laser-safe ETTs in airway cases; minimize FiO₂
    Laser plume Vaporized tissue particles contain carcinogens and viable virus particles Laser plume evacuators required; all personnel wear laser-specific N95 masks
    Electrical hazard High-voltage equipment Follow manufacturer guidelines; trained personnel only

    The scrub technologist must: - Don appropriate wavelength-specific eye protection before laser activation - Ensure the patient's eyes are protected - Have moist towels/sponges prepared to surround the operative site - Know the laser safety officer and the facility laser safety program - Never look directly at the beam

    8.6 The Scrub Technologist's Role in Hemostasis¶

    The scrub tech anticipates hemorrhage and manages the response at the level of the sterile field.

    Proactive: - Keep suture ties ready during dissection near major vessels - Have hemostatic clips loaded in the applier - Keep a supply of appropriate sponges (damp laparotomy pads, raytec) accessible - Know which topical hemostatic agents are on the field and their locations

    Reactive: - When unexpected bleeding occurs, respond without being told: place a sponge, hand a sucker, pass a clamp - In major hemorrhage, maintain awareness of sponge counts despite the urgency — a massive bleed is the scenario where retention is most likely - Communicate any shortage of critical supplies to the circulator immediately

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "In a bleed, your hands should be moving before the surgeon finishes the sentence."

    • ESU pencil tip builds char over time. Clean it proactively with the scratch pad — a dirty tip requires more power, which causes more collateral damage.
    • Never leave the ESU pencil resting on a drape unholstered. The device can be inadvertently activated, and it is a fire starter.
    • Thrombin is topical only. If it lands on an instrument that then goes back into the wound, it is not harmful — but injecting it is deadly. Know what is in each labeled basin.
