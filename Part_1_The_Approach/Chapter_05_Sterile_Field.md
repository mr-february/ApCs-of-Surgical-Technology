# Chapter 5: The Sterile Field

---

## 5.1 What Is the Sterile Field?

The sterile field is the designated area of the operating room that has been established as free of microorganisms. It includes:
- The draped patient
- The draped back table
- The draped Mayo stand
- All sterile instruments, supplies, and equipment within the field
- The gowns and gloves of scrubbed personnel from the chest to table level and from cuffs to elbows

The sterile field exists to protect the patient from exogenous sources of infection during surgery — it is the physical barrier between the patient's open tissues and the non-sterile world.

---

## 5.2 Principles of Aseptic Technique

The following principles govern behavior in and around the sterile field. They are not optional guidelines — they are the rules by which every action in the OR is judged. Every member of the surgical team is responsible for upholding them.

### Principle 1: Only Sterile Items May Be Placed on the Sterile Field
Items must be confirmed sterile before being placed on or transferred to the sterile field. Package integrity must be inspected. Any item of questionable sterility must be removed.

### Principle 2: Sterile Persons Touch Only Sterile Items
A scrubbed and gowned surgical technologist or surgeon may only contact sterile surfaces. Reaching across the non-sterile field, touching the back of the gown, or contacting the circulator's hands breaks this rule.

### Principle 3: Unsterile Persons Avoid the Sterile Field
Non-scrubbed personnel must not reach over or lean across the sterile field. The circulating nurse passes items to the field from a distance — never standing over or touching sterile surfaces.

### Principle 4: The Sterile Field Is Continuously Monitored
Once established, the sterile field must be observed and not left unattended. If a sterile field is unattended and its sterility cannot be confirmed, it is considered contaminated.

### Principle 5: Sterile Drapes Define the Field
Only the top surface of a sterile drape is considered sterile. Anything that falls below table level is considered contaminated. Items that drop below the table edge must not be retrieved and returned to the field.

### Principle 6: The Edges of Sterile Packaging Are Not Sterile
When opening sterile packages, the outer edge (the peel flap, the fold) is considered the boundary between sterile and non-sterile. Contents are placed onto the field without allowing the package to touch the field, or by allowing the contents to drop without contact with non-sterile surfaces.

### Principle 7: Moisture Is the Enemy of Sterility (Strike-Through)
A sterile barrier wet with any liquid allows microorganisms to wick through from non-sterile surfaces — this is called **strike-through contamination**. Wet sterile drapes, wet packaging materials, or wet gown surfaces over non-sterile areas are considered contaminated.

### Principle 8: Movement Around the Sterile Field Is Controlled
Scrubbed personnel face the sterile field and move past each other front-to-front or back-to-back. Non-scrubbed personnel pass behind scrubbed personnel. Turning your back to the sterile field is a contamination risk.

### Principle 9: Talking, Coughing, and Sneezing Near the Field Are Risks
Respiratory droplets can contaminate the sterile field. Minimizing unnecessary conversation, using proper masking, and directing airflow away from the field are important habits.

### Principle 10: When in Doubt — Contaminate It
If any question exists about whether an item, surface, or person has been contaminated, it must be treated as contaminated. There is no "probably fine" in aseptic technique.

## 5.3 Establishing the Sterile Field

### Timing
The sterile field should be established as close to the time of patient skin incision as practical. Long delays between setup and incision increase the time available for contamination. Most facilities allow sterile fields to stand for a case day if continuously monitored and uncovered; institution policy governs maximum allowable times.

### Back Table Setup
The scrub technologist dons sterile gown and gloves, then sets up the back table in a systematic, organized manner:

1. Open the back table pack and drape the table with a sterile barrier
2. Open instrument sets onto the table (by the circulator or by pulling from peel packs)
3. Organize instruments by category: cutting/dissecting, grasping/clamping, retraction, suction, specialty items
4. Count sponges, sharps, and instruments with the circulator (initial count)
5. Set up suture, electrosurgery equipment, and specialty supplies
6. Load the Mayo stand with instruments for the beginning of the case

### Opening Sterile Supplies
The circulating nurse opens sterile supplies and transfers them to the sterile field using one of two methods:

**Method 1 — Drop technique (peel pouches)**
The circulator peels the package open and "presents" the opening toward the scrub tech, who lifts the item out without touching the package edges. Or, the item is allowed to drop directly onto the field from above.

**Method 2 — Flip technique (wrapped packages)**
The circulator unfolds the outer wrap, holding the corners away from the sterile interior, and either presents or drops the inner-wrapped item to the sterile field.

**Critical rules:**
- The outer 1-inch border of any wrapping is considered non-sterile
- The circulator never reaches across or contacts the sterile field
- Items are never dropped from excessive height (risk of bouncing or uncontrolled landing)
- Pouring sterile liquids into containers on the sterile field requires the circulator to pour without touching the sterile container; pour from a close height to prevent splash

## 5.4 Gowning and Gloving

Donning a sterile gown and gloves is one of the fundamental skills of surgical technology. It must be performed correctly, without contamination, every time.

### Gowning Procedure
1. Complete surgical hand antisepsis (scrub or ABHR)
2. Enter the OR with hands above the waist, not touching any surface
3. Approach the sterile back table; the gown is folded inside-out and presented by the circulator or pre-placed on the back table
4. Grasp the gown at the inside neckline; step back from the table
5. Allow the gown to unfold without touching the outside surfaces
6. Slide both arms into the sleeves simultaneously, keeping hands inside the gown cuffs (for closed gloving)
7. The circulator ties the back of the gown

### Closed Gloving Technique (Preferred)
Hands remain inside the gown sleeves throughout the gloving process — the gown cuff is never breached.

1. Using the sleeve-covered right hand, pick up the left glove from the inner glove wrapper
2. Place the glove palm-down on the left sleeve cuff, fingers pointing toward the elbow
3. Using sleeve-covered fingers, fold the glove cuff over the gown cuff
4. Push the left hand through the gown cuff and into the glove simultaneously
5. Repeat with the right hand, using the gloved left hand to assist

### Open Gloving Technique
Used when gloving without a sterile gown (e.g., changing a glove during a case) or when adding gloves at certain junctures.

1. Pick up the first glove by the cuff with the opposite hand
2. Slide the dominant hand into the glove without touching the outside
3. With the gloved hand, slide fingers under the cuff of the second glove
4. Slide the second hand into the second glove
5. Adjust both gloves for fit without contaminating the glove surfaces (touch only the sterile outer surface)

### Assisted Gloving
An already-gowned and gloved team member can assist another in gloving:
1. The assisting tech holds the glove open, thumb tucked away
2. The recipient slides their hand into the glove
3. The assisting tech rolls the glove cuff up over the gown sleeve

### Removing Contaminated Gloves (Gown and Glove Removal)
At the end of a case:
1. Remove gown first (circulator pulls from behind), allowing gown to roll inward
2. Remove gloves by grasping the outside of one glove near the wrist, peeling it off inside out
3. Slide finger of ungloved hand under the remaining glove cuff (touching only the inside)
4. Peel off the second glove inside out, enclosing the first

## 5.5 Maintaining the Sterile Field During the Case

Once the case begins, the scrub technologist's primary role is to maintain the sterile field and pass instruments efficiently. Key ongoing responsibilities:

**Instrument management:**
- Keep Mayo stand organized and current to the step of the procedure
- Remove used instruments from the field promptly to reduce clutter
- Anticipate the surgeon's next move and pre-load suture or set up the next instrument before being asked

**Handling sharps:**
- Maintain a neutral zone (e.g., kidney basin or emesis basin) for passing scalpels and needles
- Never pass sharps hand-to-hand
- Announce sharps: "Scalpel" or "Needle back"
- Keep used needles on a magnetic needle mat or in a needle box; count at intervals

**Suction and cord management:**
- Secure suction tubing, electrosurgery cords, and light cables to the drapes using clamps
- Ensure cords are not draped over sterile surfaces in ways that risk contamination
- Maintain suction tip in a clean location when not in active use

**Sponge discipline:**
- Sponges on the field must always be accounted for
- Tally sponge counts in groups of five (standard packaging)
- Bloody sponges should be collected in a defined area and counted before discarding the field count

## 5.6 Recognizing and Responding to Contamination

Contamination events will occur in every surgical career. The mark of a professional is not whether contamination happens — it's whether it is recognized, announced, and corrected.

### Common Contamination Events

| Event | Correct Response |
|-------|----------------|
| Scrubbed person touches non-sterile surface | Announce contamination; change gown and/or gloves |
| Sterile item falls below table level | Do not retrieve; discard; open replacement |
| Package found wet or torn | Consider non-sterile; remove from field; replace |
| Hole found in glove | Change glove immediately; inform surgeon |
| Unknown person touches sterile drape | Consider contaminated; redress field |
| Circulator leans over field | Call out; assess extent; treat as contaminated |

### Announcing Contamination
When contamination occurs, the person who observes it must speak up — immediately and clearly:
> *"The field is contaminated — the back table was brushed. I need to redress."*

This requires **surgical conscience**: the willingness to prioritize patient safety over the convenience of the moment, even when it slows the case, even when you are the one who caused the contamination.

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "Your sterile field is only as good as the weakest moment in which you failed to speak up."

- When setting up the back table, organize instruments the same way every time. Consistency becomes muscle memory, and muscle memory frees your mind to anticipate the case.
- If you are unsure whether you touched something non-sterile, change your glove. The patient cannot speak for themselves — you must speak for them.
- Maintain visual contact with your sterile field whenever possible. An unmonitored sterile field is a question mark.
