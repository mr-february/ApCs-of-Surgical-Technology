    Chapter 11: Skin Preparation and Draping¶

    11.1 Purpose of Skin Preparation¶

    Even with intact skin, the human body harbors millions of bacteria. Skin prep cannot sterilize the skin — it reduces the total bacterial count to a level unlikely to cause infection in the context of the surgical wound. The goal is to decrease the risk of endogenous SSIs (those caused by the patient's own flora).

    Skin prep: - Reduces transient flora (surface-level contamination) - Suppresses resident flora (deeper skin flora) - Creates an antiseptic barrier that persists into the procedure

    Skin prep is typically performed by the circulating nurse or surgical technologist (circulating), in coordination with the scrub technologist who will later drape.

    11.2 Hair Removal¶

    If hair must be removed from the surgical site, use electric clippers with a single-use or disinfectable head — never a razor.

    Rationale: Razors cause microscopic skin abrasions that become colonized with bacteria and increase SSI risk. Studies consistently show razor shaving results in higher SSI rates than clipping.

    Timing: Hair removal should be performed as close to surgery as practical, ideally immediately before prep in the holding area or OR.

    Policy: Many facilities have abandoned routine hair removal entirely; it is only performed if hair physically interferes with the surgical procedure.

    11.3 Skin Prep Agents¶

    Chlorhexidine Gluconate (CHG)¶

    • Mechanism: Disrupts bacterial cell membrane integrity; bactericidal
    • Spectrum: Broad-spectrum gram-positive and gram-negative, some fungi
    • Onset: Rapid
    • Persistence: Excellent — continues to work after application; residual activity persists even after rinsing with water
    • Available forms: Aqueous (water-based) or alcohol-based (CHG + isopropyl alcohol — more effective)
    • Precautions: Do not use in ears (ototoxic), eyes, or on mucous membranes; do not use in premature infants

    CHG + 70% isopropyl alcohol (e.g., ChloraPrep) is the current evidence-based standard for most surgical skin antisepsis.

    Povidone-Iodine (Betadine)¶

    • Mechanism: Free iodine denatures proteins and nucleic acids
    • Spectrum: Broad-spectrum (bacteria, fungi, viruses, spores)
    • Persistence: Limited — reduced when exposed to blood, serum, or other organics
    • Available forms: Aqueous scrub, aqueous paint, alcohol-based
    • Precautions: May cause skin sensitivity; avoid eyes; drying takes time — do not wipe off

    Povidone-iodine is preferred for mucous membrane prep (vaginal, anorectal, oral) and for patients with CHG allergy.

    Isopropyl Alcohol¶

    • Fast onset, excellent broad-spectrum activity
    • Short persistence (evaporates rapidly)
    • Highly flammable — must dry completely before draping or using electrosurgery
    • Used primarily as a component of combination products (CHG/alcohol)

    Iodine in Alcohol (Io-Scrub, Duraprep)¶

    Combination of iodine povacrylex and isopropyl alcohol. Strong, rapid antiseptic activity. Must dry completely before draping (≥3 minutes). Do not pool in skin folds.

    11.4 Skin Prep Technique¶

    General Principles¶

    1. Start at the incision site and work outward in a circular (concentric) motion
    2. Never return to the center after moving outward — the periphery is dirtier than the center
    3. Prep the full area that may be needed for the incision or for field extension
    4. Use sufficient coverage: extend beyond the proposed incision to allow for variation and wound extension
    5. Allow the prep to dry completely before draping — wet prep under drapes increases strike-through risk and fire risk if alcohol-based
    6. Remove excess pooling solution from the skin to prevent chemical burns

    Special Technique Situations¶

    Contaminated area (stoma, open wound, anus): - Prep the contaminated area last, using a separate prep sponge - Work from clean area inward toward the contaminated site — never from contaminated to clean

    Umbilicus: - Prep the umbilicus separately with a separate applicator; it harbors higher concentrations of bacteria - Use a swab or small applicator to reach into the umbilical recess

    Genital/perineal prep: - Use povidone-iodine (aqueous) for vaginal, scrotal, or perianal prep — not CHG on mucous membranes - For female vaginal cases: prep vaginal vault with long-handled prep sponges; prep cervix if applicable

    Orthopedic extremity prep: - May require circumferential (360°) prep around the limb - The extremity may need to be elevated by an assistant holding the foot/ankle above the table while the prep is applied

    Face and head: - Protect the eyes (tape closed or use a protective shield) - Use iodine-based solutions around eyes and ears, not CHG - Be cautious near hairline — prep agents should not soak into hair unnecessarily

    11.5 Principles of Surgical Draping¶

    Draping establishes the sterile field around the patient. Properly applied drapes create a continuous sterile surface from the operative site to the back table, over which instruments, sutures, and supplies can be passed and managed.

    Draping Principles¶

    1. Only the top surface of drapes is sterile — once placed, drapes are not repositioned (cephalad or caudal); repositioning would contaminate the top surface by touching the undrape table or patient
    2. Drapes define the limits of the sterile field — instruments and hands must remain above the drape surface
    3. Draping proceeds from the incision site outward — the clean area is covered first; less-clean areas covered later
    4. Sterile persons drape toward themselves — they hold the drape with their hands protected in the folded edge, and unfold it away from themselves and toward the incision
    5. If a drape becomes contaminated, it must be replaced — not simply covered with another drape
    6. Drapes are secured with towel clamps, sterile staples, or adhesive incise drapes after placement

    Draping Sequence (General Abdominal, Supine)¶

    1. Four sterile draping towels: placed to outline the incision site on all four sides; secured with towel clamps or staples
    2. Laparotomy drape (lap sheet): a large fenestrated drape with a pre-cut opening; placed over the patient so the opening aligns with the incision; the remainder of the drape covers the patient and table
    3. Mayo stand drape: a long sterile sleeve drape placed over the Mayo stand
    4. Back table drape: already placed by the scrub tech during setup

    11.6 Drape Types¶

    Drape Description Use
    Draping towels (towel drapes) Small sterile towels used to outline incision site All procedures
    Laparotomy (lap) sheet Large, fenestrated; covers patient and table Abdominal procedures
    Transverse (split) sheet Split sheet that wraps around an extremity Extremity procedures
    U-drape U-shaped; used above and below a limb Arthroscopy, hand
    Eye drape Small, fenestrated; adhesive border Ophthalmic procedures
    Perineal drape Designed for lithotomy; leg portions extend over stirrups OB/GYN, urologic
    Thyroid drape Modified head sheet; opening over the neck Thyroid/ENT
    Mayo stand sleeve Tubular sterile sleeve slipped over the Mayo stand All procedures
    Microscope drape Sterile sleeve applied over the surgical microscope Neuro, ophthalmology, ENT
    C-arm drape Sterile covering for fluoroscopy C-arm arm Orthopedic, vascular

    Antimicrobial Incise Drapes (Ioban, 3M)¶

    Adhesive sterile plastic film applied directly over the prepped, dry skin. The incision is made through the drape.

    Purpose: Prevents skin flora from migrating laterally into the wound during the procedure; may decrease SSI rates in certain cases.

    Application: Must be applied to completely dry skin — wet skin prevents adhesion. Roll the film onto the skin without trapping air bubbles.

    Not all surgeons use incise drapes — follow the preference card.

    11.7 The Scrub Technologist and Draping¶

    During draping, the scrub technologist may: - Pass sterile drapes to the surgical team - Apply the Mayo stand sleeve - Place the back table drape (already done during setup) - Pass towel clamps for securing drapes - Apply electrosurgery cord, suction tubing, and light cable holders to the drapes after the patient is draped

    Once draping is complete: - Connect suction tubing to the wall suction (circulator connects at the wall end) - Secure ESU cord to the drape - Plug in and test the ESU with the circulator - Position the Mayo stand over the patient - Ensure all counts are complete and documented before the surgeon begins

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "Prep it right the first time. You don't get a second chance before the incision."

    • Check your prep solution before opening the case. A CHG-allergic patient needs a different agent. Check the allergy list.
    • Alcohol-based prep agents need time to dry. Three minutes minimum — resist the surgeon's impatience when patient safety is at stake.
    • When passing drapes, hold them up and out — away from your scrub suit — before placing them. A drape that brushes your non-sterile torso is contaminated.
