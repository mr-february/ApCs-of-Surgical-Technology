# Chapter 1: What Is Surgical Technology?

---

## 1.1 Defining the Profession

Surgical technology is the allied health profession that focuses on providing surgical care to patients in the perioperative setting. The surgical technologist works under the supervision of a registered nurse (RN) and the direction of a licensed surgeon to ensure that the operating room environment is safe, that equipment functions properly, and that the operative procedure is conducted under conditions that maximize safety and efficiency for the patient.

The practice of surgical technology spans three phases of the surgical experience: preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative. Within each phase, the surgical technologist performs specific, defined responsibilities — from setting up the sterile back table before the first patient enters the room, to maintaining the sterile field during the most complex procedures, to assisting with specimen handling and wound closure as the case concludes.

Surgical technologists are not surgeons, and they are not nurses — they are a specialized hybrid, trained in the technical and aseptic demands of the operating room with a depth of procedural knowledge that few other healthcare roles require.

---

## 1.2 History of the Profession

The modern surgical technologist did not emerge from a classroom. The profession was born in necessity.

During **World War II**, the United States military faced an acute shortage of trained surgical personnel. Medics and corpsmen were trained to assist surgeons in battlefield and field hospital settings, performing tasks that had previously been reserved for nurses. Their effectiveness — under extreme conditions, with minimal resources — demonstrated that focused technical training could produce highly capable OR assistants.

After the war, veterans with surgical training entered civilian hospitals. Hospital administrators, already wrestling with nursing shortages, recognized the value of having dedicated OR technical staff. Throughout the 1950s and 1960s, ad hoc programs began to appear at hospitals across the country, training "operating room technicians" in the skills of the scrub role.

In **1969**, a group of OR technicians and their nursing supporters formed the **Association of Surgical Technologists (AST)** to establish professional standards, develop educational curricula, and advocate for the profession. The organization created a code of ethics, practice standards, and — critically — a pathway toward credentialing.

The **National Board of Surgical Technology and Surgical Assisting (NBSTSA)** was established to administer a national certification examination, culminating in the **Certified Surgical Technologist (CST)** credential. Today, graduation from a CAAHEP-accredited program and passage of the CST exam are the gold standard of entry into the profession.

## 1.3 Educational Pathway

### Program Accreditation
Surgical technology programs must be accredited by **CAAHEP** (Commission on Accreditation of Allied Health Education Programs), through its reviewing arm **ARC/STSA** (Accreditation Review Council on Education in Surgical Technology and Surgical Assisting). Accreditation ensures that programs meet established competency standards and that their graduates are eligible to sit for the CST exam.

Programs are offered at:
- **Community colleges** (most common — typically Associates of Applied Science degree)
- **Technical/vocational schools** (certificate programs — typically 12–24 months)
- **Universities** (Bachelor of Science programs available at select institutions)

### Curriculum Content
Accredited programs must include:
- Basic sciences (anatomy, physiology, microbiology, pharmacology)
- Surgical technology core (aseptic technique, instrumentation, sterilization)
- Surgical specialties (organized by body system)
- Clinical practicum (hands-on hours in actual operating rooms)

The **AST** recommends a minimum of 120 **surgical procedures** performed in the scrub role during clinical education, across a range of specialties.

### The CST Examination
The NBSTSA CST examination is a computer-based, multiple-choice exam covering:
- Perioperative care (preoperative, intraoperative, postoperative)
- Ancillary duties (sterilization, equipment, pharmacology)
- Fundamental knowledge (anatomy, physiology, microbiology)
- Specialty-specific content

Certification must be maintained through **continuing education (CE)** — 60 CE credits are required every four years to maintain CST status.

## 1.4 The Surgical Team

The surgical technologist works within a multidisciplinary team. Understanding each member's role — and how the surgical technologist interfaces with each — is essential to functioning effectively in the OR.

| Team Member | Role |
|-------------|------|
| **Surgeon** | Performs the operative procedure; directs the scrub tech regarding instrument and supply needs |
| **Surgical Technologist (scrub)** | Maintains the sterile field; passes instruments; manages the back table and Mayo stand |
| **Circulating Nurse (RN)** | Manages the nonsterile OR environment; documents, retrieves supplies, communicates |
| **Anesthesiologist / CRNA** | Manages the patient's anesthetic state and airway |
| **Surgical First Assistant** | Provides direct surgical assistance; may be CST-SFA, PA, RNFA, or resident |
| **Radiologic Technologist** | Provides intraoperative imaging when needed (fluoroscopy, C-arm) |
| **Perfusionist** | Manages cardiopulmonary bypass during cardiac procedures |
| **Pathologist** | Evaluates and interprets surgical specimens |

### Team Dynamics
Effective surgical teams are built on **closed-loop communication** — messages are sent, received, and confirmed. Time-outs, count confirmations, and specimen identification all rely on this principle. The surgical technologist who speaks up when something is wrong — even when it is uncomfortable — is performing one of the most important functions in patient safety.

## 1.5 Legal and Ethical Considerations

### Scope of Practice
The scope of practice for surgical technologists varies by state. Some states have enacted specific statutes governing what STs may and may not do; others defer to institutional policy. It is the responsibility of every surgical technologist to know the laws of the state in which they practice.

Generally, surgical technologists in the scrub role may:
- Set up and maintain the sterile field
- Pass instruments and supplies to the surgeon
- Hold retractors, cut sutures (when instructed), and assist with counts
- Handle and label specimens (in coordination with the circulator)

Surgical technologists do not independently prescribe, diagnose, or perform unsupervised invasive procedures.

### Professional Ethics
The AST **Code of Ethics** outlines the professional obligations of surgical technologists, including:
- Placing the patient's welfare above all else
- Maintaining the highest standards of aseptic technique
- Maintaining patient confidentiality (HIPAA)
- Refusing to participate in procedures they believe are unethical or unsafe
- Continuing professional development

### HIPAA and Confidentiality
All patient information encountered in the OR — diagnosis, procedure, personal details — is protected under the **Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)**. Surgical technologists must never discuss patient information outside of clinical necessity, and must not document, photograph, or share patient identifiers in any unauthorized manner.

### Negligence and Liability
A surgical technologist can be held legally liable for negligent acts — actions that fall below the standard of care and result in patient harm. Common examples include:
- Miscounting instruments, sponges, or sharps
- Breaking sterile technique without disclosure
- Administering the wrong medication to the sterile field
- Failing to report a contamination event

**Reporting errors and near-misses is a professional and ethical obligation**, not an optional act.

---

## 1.6 Professionalism in the OR

The operating room is a high-stakes, high-stress environment. Professionalism — defined as the consistent application of skills, ethics, and conduct standards expected of a trained professional — is non-negotiable.

Professionalism includes:
- **Punctuality**: OR cases begin on schedule; your absence affects the entire team and the patient
- **Appearance**: surgical attire, PPE, and personal hygiene standards exist for patient protection
- **Communication**: clear, concise, respectful communication saves lives
- **Emotional regulation**: the OR is not a place for ego, panic, or interpersonal conflict
- **Lifelong learning**: medicine evolves; the CST who stopped learning five years ago is already behind

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "The surgeon sees the anatomy. You see the whole room. Someone has to."

- Know the procedure before you scrub in. Read the surgeon's preference card, review the anatomy, know which instruments come next. Anticipation is the mark of an expert scrub tech.
- When in doubt about sterility — contaminate it. Never rationalize keeping a compromised item in the field because you're not sure it was actually contaminated.
- A strong "surgical conscience" is worth more than any single technical skill.
