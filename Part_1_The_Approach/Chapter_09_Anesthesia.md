# Chapter 9: Anesthesia

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Identify and describe the four types of anesthesia used in surgical procedures.
2. Describe the phases of general anesthesia.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Anesthesia** | A state of controlled, temporary loss of sensation or consciousness for medical purposes |
| **Anesthesiologist** | A physician (MD/DO) specializing in anesthesia care |
| **CRNA** | Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetist; advanced practice nurse specializing in anesthesia |
| **Induction** | The phase of general anesthesia when consciousness is lost |
| **Maintenance** | The sustained anesthetic state during the operative procedure |

## 9.1 Types of Anesthesia

Anesthesia is not a single intervention — it is a spectrum of techniques calibrated to the patient, the procedure, and the goals of the operative team.

### General Anesthesia
The patient is rendered completely unconscious, unaware, and non-responsive to surgical stimulation. All protective airway reflexes are abolished, requiring assisted ventilation.

General anesthesia provides the "four A's":
- **Analgesia**: freedom from pain
- **Amnesia**: no memory of the procedure
- **Areflexia**: absence of protective reflexes
- **Akinesia**: muscular relaxation and immobility

Achieved through a combination of IV induction agents, inhalation agents, neuromuscular blocking agents, and analgesics.

### Regional Anesthesia
A region of the body is desensitized by blocking specific nerves. The patient may remain awake, sedated, or have monitored anesthesia care added.

Types:
- **Spinal (subarachnoid block)**: local anesthetic injected into the subarachnoid space (L3–L4 or L4–L5); rapid, dense block; used for lower extremity, perineal, and lower abdominal surgery
- **Epidural**: local anesthetic injected into the epidural space; slower onset; allows continuous infusion via catheter; used in OB, post-op pain management, extended cases
- **Peripheral nerve block**: targeted injection around a specific nerve or plexus (e.g., brachial plexus, femoral nerve, popliteal block); used for upper and lower extremity surgery; excellent post-op analgesia

### Monitored Anesthesia Care (MAC)
The patient receives IV sedation (typically midazolam, fentanyl, propofol) while the surgeon provides local anesthetic to the operative site. The patient is sedated but not fully unconscious and may be arousable.

Used for: minor procedures, endoscopy, ophthalmology, biopsies, superficial surgery

The scrub tech should be aware that MAC patients can hear and feel discomfort — communicate carefully.

### Local Anesthesia Only
A local anesthetic agent is injected by the surgeon directly into the operative site without any systemic sedation. The patient is fully awake.

Used for: very minor procedures (skin biopsy, lipoma excision), ophthalmology (cataract under topical), dental procedures

## 9.2 Common Anesthetic Agents

### Induction Agents (IV)
| Agent | Class | Key Characteristics | Notes |
|-------|-------|---------------------|-------|
| **Propofol (Diprivan)** | Alkylphenol | Rapid onset/offset, antiemetic | "Milk of amnesia"; white emulsion |
| **Ketamine** | NMDA antagonist | Dissociative; analgesic; preserves airway reflexes | Used in pediatrics, trauma; may cause emergence delirium |
| **Etomidate** | Imidazole | Minimal cardiovascular depression | Preferred for hemodynamically unstable patients; brief adrenal suppression |
| **Methohexital** | Barbiturate | Rarely used today | Occasionally for ECT |

### Inhalation (Volatile) Agents
Administered as gases via the anesthesia circuit; responsible for maintenance of general anesthesia.

| Agent | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Sevoflurane** | Most common; non-pungent; good for inhalation induction |
| **Desflurane** | Rapid onset/offset; pungent; airway irritant |
| **Isoflurane** | Older; cost-effective; pungent |
| **Nitrous oxide (N₂O)** | Adjunct; weak analgesic; limited when high O₂ required |

**MH trigger alert:** All volatile agents and succinylcholine are triggers for malignant hyperthermia in susceptible individuals (see Section 9.5).

### Neuromuscular Blocking Agents (NMBAs)
Produce skeletal muscle relaxation for intubation and surgical exposure.

| Agent | Type | Duration | Notes |
|-------|------|---------|-------|
| **Succinylcholine (Sux, Anectine)** | Depolarizing | Ultra-short (~8 min) | Fastest onset; MH trigger; use restricted in burn/crush/neuromuscular disease |
| **Rocuronium** | Non-depolarizing | Intermediate | Reversible with Sugammadex |
| **Vecuronium** | Non-depolarizing | Intermediate | Reversible with neostigmine/glycopyrrolate |
| **Cisatracurium** | Non-depolarizing | Intermediate | Hoffman elimination; useful in organ failure |
| **Pancuronium** | Non-depolarizing | Long | Tachycardia side effect |

**Reversal agents:**
- **Neostigmine + glycopyrrolate**: reverses non-depolarizing NMBAs
- **Sugammadex (Bridion)**: selective reversal of rocuronium and vecuronium; faster and more complete

### Local Anesthetic Agents
| Agent | Duration | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| **Lidocaine (Xylocaine)** | 1–2 hours | Most common; available in various concentrations; with or without epinephrine |
| **Bupivacaine (Marcaine)** | 4–8 hours | Long-acting; cardiac toxicity risk at high doses |
| **Ropivacaine (Naropin)** | 4–8 hours | Less cardiotoxic than bupivacaine |
| **Mepivacaine (Carbocaine)** | 2–3 hours | Intermediate duration |
| **Tetracaine** | Long | Spinal anesthesia |
| **Cocaine** | Short | ENT only; vasoconstrictor in the airway; controlled substance |

**Epinephrine addition:** When added to local anesthetic, epinephrine causes vasoconstriction at the injection site, prolonging the block duration and reducing systemic absorption. **Never use epinephrine-containing local anesthetic in areas without collateral circulation: digits, penis, nose, ear flaps.**

**On the sterile field:** All medications received on the sterile field must be labeled immediately with the drug name and concentration. Use pre-labeled medication cups; confirm with the circulator before labeling.

## 9.3 Phases of General Anesthesia

### Phase 1: Preoperative Preparation
- IV access established
- Monitors applied (ECG, SpO₂, NIBP, EtCO₂, temperature)
- Pre-oxygenation: 100% O₂ via facemask for 3–5 minutes to denitrogenate lungs
- Pre-medication given if indicated (anxiolytic, antisialagogue, antiemetic)

### Phase 2: Induction
- IV induction agent administered (rapid loss of consciousness within 30–60 seconds)
- Succinylcholine or rocuronium given for intubation
- Laryngoscopy and endotracheal intubation (or LMA placement)
- Confirmation of placement: bilateral breath sounds, chest rise, EtCO₂ waveform
- The scrub tech should be silent and still during induction — this is the most critical moment for the anesthesia provider

### Phase 3: Maintenance
- Volatile agent +/- nitrous oxide delivered via ventilator
- IV analgesics (fentanyl, remifentanil), adjunct agents as needed
- Surgical procedure is performed during this phase
- Monitors track depth of anesthesia and physiologic stability

### Phase 4: Emergence
- Volatile agents discontinued; patient begins to wake
- Neuromuscular blockade reversed
- Patient must be able to breathe spontaneously, follow commands, and maintain airway before extubation
- The scrub tech should be aware that emergence can be a period of patient agitation — keep instruments away from the patient's reach; do not lean on the patient

### Phase 5: Recovery (PACU)
- Patient transferred to post-anesthesia care unit
- Monitored until stable, awake, and meeting discharge criteria

## 9.4 Airway Management Devices

### Endotracheal Tube (ETT)
A cuffed tube inserted through the mouth (orotracheal) or nose (nasotracheal) into the trachea, past the vocal cords. Provides definitive airway protection.

- Sizes: typically 7.0–8.0 mm for adult males, 6.5–7.5 mm for adult females
- Cuff inflated to 20–30 cmH₂O to create a seal
- Position confirmed by EtCO₂ and chest auscultation
- Secured with tape or a commercial tube holder

### Laryngeal Mask Airway (LMA)
A supraglottic device (sits above the glottis, not in the trachea). Easier to insert than an ETT; does not protect against aspiration. Used for shorter procedures, patients with difficult airways, or when intubation is not required.

Types: standard, intubating (Fastrach), proseal (with gastric drain channel)

### Oral/Nasopharyngeal Airways
Used to maintain airway patency in sedated patients or during mask ventilation; not definitive airways.

### Surgical Airways
In emergency airway situations:
- **Cricothyrotomy**: emergency incision through the cricothyroid membrane; rapid but temporary
- **Tracheostomy**: surgical airway through the trachea (planned procedure)

## 9.5 Malignant Hyperthermia (MH)

Malignant hyperthermia is a **pharmacogenetic crisis** — a rare but potentially fatal hypermetabolic reaction to certain anesthetic agents in genetically susceptible individuals. It is a true operating room emergency.

### Triggering Agents
- **All volatile anesthetic agents** (sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane, halothane)
- **Succinylcholine** (the only neuromuscular blocking agent that triggers MH)

Non-triggering anesthetics (propofol, ketamine, opioids, non-depolarizing NMBAs) are used in MH-susceptible patients.

### Pathophysiology
In susceptible individuals, a mutation in the **ryanodine receptor (RYR1)** causes an uncontrolled release of calcium from muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum in response to triggers. Uncontrolled muscle metabolism ensues:
- Massive CO₂ production
- Hyperthermia (temperature may rise >1°C every 5 minutes)
- Muscle rigidity
- Rhabdomyolysis
- Acidosis
- Hyperkalemia → cardiac arrhythmia → cardiac arrest

### Signs and Symptoms (in approximate order of appearance)
1. Unexplained increase in end-tidal CO₂ (earliest and most sensitive sign)
2. Tachycardia
3. Hyperthermia (may be late sign)
4. Masseter spasm (jaw rigidity after succinylcholine)
5. Generalized muscle rigidity
6. Dark urine (myoglobinuria from muscle breakdown)
7. Metabolic and respiratory acidosis

### Treatment (MH Emergency Protocol)
The Malignant Hyperthermia Association of the United States (MHAUS) maintains an emergency hotline and protocol:
**MHAUS Hotline: 1-800-644-9737**

1. **Call for help** — this is an all-hands emergency
2. **Discontinue triggering agents immediately**
3. **Switch to non-triggering anesthetic** (total IV anesthesia)
4. **Hyperventilate with 100% O₂** at maximum flow
5. **Administer Dantrolene sodium IV**: 2.5 mg/kg IV bolus; repeat every 5 minutes until symptoms resolve (maximum ~10 mg/kg, often more required)
6. **Treat hyperthermia**: ice packs, cold IV fluids, gastric lavage with iced saline
7. **Treat acidosis**: sodium bicarbonate
8. **Treat hyperkalemia**: calcium chloride, insulin/glucose, sodium bicarbonate
9. **Continue monitoring**: ICU admission; MH can recrudesce
10. **Dantrolene IV for 24–48 hours** post-event

### The Scrub Tech's Role During an MH Crisis
- Do not abandon the sterile field unless directed — wounds may need to be closed rapidly
- Assist with closing if directed
- Remain calm and follow instructions from the anesthesia team
- Prepare ice-cold saline if asked
- Be prepared for rapid case termination

### Dantrolene Preparation
Dantrolene is provided as a lyophilized powder (20 mg per vial) requiring 60 mL of sterile water for reconstitution. Each vial requires vigorous mixing. In an MH crisis, **many vials** must be reconstituted simultaneously — all available personnel will be recruited to help.

## 9.6 Medications on the Sterile Field: Safety Principles

1. **Label all medications immediately** upon transfer to the sterile field
2. **Confirm verbally** with the circulator: "I'm labeling this as lidocaine 1% with epinephrine"
3. Use **pre-printed labels** or a permanent marker on medication cups/syringes
4. **Never have two unlabeled medication containers** open simultaneously
5. If a medication's identity is questioned, discard it — never guess
6. Thrombin must be labeled and kept separate from injectable medications

## Clinical Pearls

> "During induction and emergence, the anesthesia provider needs silence and space. Give both without being asked."

- If a surgeon is asking for epinephrine in a local anesthetic for a digit procedure, speak up — epinephrine is contraindicated in end-arterial tissues. Patient safety is everyone's responsibility.
- Rising EtCO₂ during a case is not normal. If you notice it on the monitor, inform the team.
- Know where the MH cart is before every case. Every OR should have one stocked and immediately accessible.
