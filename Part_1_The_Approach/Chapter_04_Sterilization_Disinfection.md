# Chapter 4: Sterilization and Disinfection

---

## 4.1 Defining the Hierarchy

The terms sterilization, disinfection, and cleaning are often used loosely, but they represent very distinct levels of microbial control with different indications, processes, and outcomes.

### Sterilization
Sterilization destroys **all microorganisms**, including the most resistant bacterial and fungal spores. An item is either sterile or it is not — there is no partial sterilization. Items that enter sterile body cavities or the bloodstream must be sterile.

### Disinfection
Disinfection destroys most pathogenic organisms but **does not reliably kill all spores**. Disinfection is classified by level:
- **High-level disinfection (HLD)**: kills all organisms except high numbers of spores; used for semi-critical items
- **Intermediate-level disinfection**: kills mycobacteria, most viruses, bacteria, some fungi; not sporicidal
- **Low-level disinfection**: kills most bacteria and some viruses; used for non-critical surfaces

### Cleaning (Sanitization)
Cleaning removes visible debris, organic material, and reduces the microbial load. Cleaning **must always precede** sterilization or disinfection — organic material (blood, protein) can shield microorganisms from sterilants and disinfectants. A dirty instrument cannot be reliably sterilized.

## 4.2 The Spaulding Classification System

In 1972, Earle Spaulding developed a rational framework for determining the level of reprocessing required for medical devices and equipment. The system classifies items based on the risk of infection if the item is contaminated.

| Category | Definition | Risk | Required Processing | Examples |
|----------|-----------|------|-------------------|---------|
| **Critical** | Enters sterile tissue or the vascular system | Highest | **Sterilization** | Scalpels, scissors, bone instruments, IV catheters, implants |
| **Semi-critical** | Contacts intact mucous membranes or non-intact skin | Intermediate | **High-Level Disinfection** (sterilization preferred) | Laryngoscope blades, flexible endoscopes, ET tubes |
| **Non-critical** | Contacts intact skin only | Low | **Low- to intermediate-level disinfection** | Blood pressure cuffs, ECG leads, bed rails, stethoscopes |

The Spaulding system remains the foundation of U.S. instrument reprocessing guidelines from the CDC and professional organizations.

## 4.3 Instrument Reprocessing Workflow

All surgical instruments follow a defined reprocessing pathway from contamination to sterile storage. This process is managed by **sterile processing (central processing) technicians**, but surgical technologists must understand it to ensure proper instrument care at point of use.

### Step 1: Point-of-Use Treatment
At the end of the case:
- Remove gross soil from instruments using sterile water or saline (do not use normal saline for prolonged soaking — it is corrosive)
- Keep instruments moist to prevent biofilm formation (use a moist towel or enzymatic foam spray)
- Transport instruments in a closed, leak-proof container to sterile processing

### Step 2: Decontamination
In sterile processing:
- Instruments are manually or mechanically cleaned
- **Automated washer-disinfectors** clean and thermally disinfect instruments using high-temperature water
- Ultrasonic cleaners use high-frequency sound waves to remove debris from complex surfaces and lumens
- Manual scrubbing under water (never above water, to prevent aerosolization of contaminants)

### Step 3: Inspection and Assembly
After cleaning:
- Instruments are inspected for functionality, cracks, corrosion, and debris
- Instruments are lubricated as needed ("milk")
- Sets are assembled per standardized instrument count sheets

### Step 4: Packaging
Instruments are wrapped or pouched for sterilization:
- **Woven/non-woven wraps**: wrapped in layers using a sequential wrap technique
- **Peel pouches**: single instruments or small sets; transparent for identification
- **Rigid containers**: reusable systems with filter-equipped lids; common for complex instrument sets

All packaging must be labeled with: contents, lot/load number, sterilizer number, expiration or process date, and technician identifier.

### Step 5: Sterilization
See Section 4.4.

### Step 6: Storage and Distribution
Sterile items are stored in a clean, dry, closed area away from traffic and moisture. Items are retrieved and delivered to ORs based on case schedules.

## 4.4 Methods of Sterilization

### Steam Sterilization (Autoclaving)
The most common, cost-effective, and well-validated method of sterilization. Uses **saturated steam under pressure** to kill microorganisms by denaturating proteins.

**Parameters for standard gravity cycle:**
- Temperature: 250°F (121°C)
- Pressure: 15 psi
- Time: 15–30 minutes

**Parameters for pre-vacuum (dynamic air removal) cycle:**
- Temperature: 270°F (132°C)
- Time: 4–10 minutes (faster due to superior steam penetration)

**Advantages:** fast, inexpensive, non-toxic residue, highly reliable
**Disadvantages:** not compatible with heat-sensitive instruments (some plastics, electronics, delicate optics)

**Immediate Use Steam Sterilization (IUSS)** — formerly called "flash sterilization":
- Used for unwrapped instruments needed urgently (e.g., a dropped instrument)
- IUSS items are delivered immediately to the field — they cannot be stored
- Should not be used routinely as a substitute for proper sterilization
- Must be documented and justified

### Ethylene Oxide (EtO) Gas
Low-temperature sterilization using ethylene oxide gas. Penetrates packaging and sterilizes at room temperature or slightly above.

**Parameters:** 55°C–60°C, 30–50% relative humidity, several hours (plus a lengthy aeration period to off-gas toxic residue)

**Advantages:** compatible with heat-sensitive items, excellent penetration
**Disadvantages:** carcinogenic gas; long cycle times; items require aeration before use (up to 8–12 hours); environmental and personnel safety concerns

**Indications:** flexible endoscopes (some types), cameras, power equipment, and other heat-sensitive items.

### Hydrogen Peroxide Gas Plasma (Sterrad)
Low-temperature sterilization using vaporized hydrogen peroxide energized into a plasma state.

**Cycle time:** 24–75 minutes
**Temperature:** 45°C–55°C

**Advantages:** no toxic residuals (breaks down to water and oxygen), fast turnaround, safe for most heat-sensitive items
**Disadvantages:** not compatible with cellulose (paper, linen), liquids, or items with very long lumens; more expensive per cycle

**Indications:** cameras, powered instruments, flexible scopes (select types), electronic equipment

### Peracetic Acid (Steris System)
Liquid chemical sterilization using peracetic acid (diluted acetic acid with hydrogen peroxide). Used for immediate use sterilization of heat-sensitive items submerged in the liquid.

**Cycle time:** ~30 minutes
**Temperature:** 50°C–56°C

**Note:** Items processed by peracetic acid cannot be stored; they must be used immediately. Items must be processed without packaging.

### Dry Heat
Uses hot air (160°C–180°C) for extended periods. Used for items that cannot be exposed to moisture (e.g., anhydrous oils, powders, glassware).

**Disadvantages:** very long cycle times, damages many plastics; limited use in modern surgical settings

## 4.5 Sterilization Monitoring

No sterilization process is infallible. Monitoring systems verify that sterilization parameters were met.

### Mechanical/Physical Indicators
Time, temperature, and pressure recordings from the sterilizer itself (printouts, digital logs). Reviewed with each cycle to confirm parameters were achieved.

### Chemical Indicators (CIs)
Chemical indicators change color or configuration when exposed to sterilization parameters. They are placed inside and outside every package.

| Class | Description | Examples |
|-------|------------|---------|
| Class 1 | Process indicators | Autoclave tape (stripes appear when exposed to steam) |
| Class 2 | Specific test indicators | Bowie-Dick test (pre-vacuum steam) |
| Class 3 | Single-parameter | React to one parameter only |
| Class 4 | Multi-parameter | React to multiple parameters |
| Class 5 | Integrating indicators | Respond to all critical parameters; most reliable CIs |
| Class 6 | Emulating indicators | Verified for specific cycles; very high performance |

**Important:** A chemical indicator confirms exposure to sterilization conditions — it does NOT confirm sterility. It is a process indicator, not a sterility guarantee.

### Biological Indicators (BIs)
The gold standard of sterilization monitoring. Contains live, resistant spores:
- **Steam sterilization**: *Geobacillus stearothermophilus* spores
- **EtO sterilization**: *Bacillus atrophaeus* spores

BIs are incubated after cycling. If spores survive, the sterilization cycle failed and all items in that load must be recalled.

**Frequency of BI use:**
- At least **weekly** for each sterilizer
- With every load containing implants
- After any sterilizer malfunction or repair

### Load Recall
If a BI returns positive (indicating sterilization failure):
- The sterilizer is taken out of service
- All items from potentially affected loads are recalled
- Items recalled must be reprocessed before use
- The event is documented and investigated

## 4.6 Event-Related vs. Time-Related Sterility

Historically, sterile packages were given expiration dates based on time (e.g., "good for 30 days"). Current evidence supports **event-related sterility** — the concept that a properly packaged, intact sterile item remains sterile indefinitely, assuming:
- The package is intact (no tears, moisture, holes)
- It was stored in appropriate conditions (clean, dry, closed area)
- The package was not improperly handled

Under this system, expiration dates are still applied as an internal audit mechanism, but package integrity inspection is the primary determinant of sterility on any given day.

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "Clean before you sterilize. A dirty instrument wrapped in a sterilization pouch is still a dirty instrument."

- Always inspect packaging before opening. Wet packages, torn packaging, or damaged containers must be considered non-sterile and returned for reprocessing.
- Never use a chemical indicator result alone to release items from quarantine after a suspected sterilization failure — wait for the BI result.
- If an IUSS item is processed for an emergency, document the reason. Routine IUSS bypasses important quality controls and should be a last resort.
