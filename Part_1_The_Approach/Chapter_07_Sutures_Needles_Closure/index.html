    Chapter 7: Sutures, Needles, and Wound Closure Devices¶

    7.1 Suture Classification¶

    Sutures are classified along three axes: absorbability, origin, and structure.

    By Absorbability¶

    Absorbable sutures are digested by body enzymes or hydrolysis over days to months. They are used in tissue that will heal to the point of not requiring permanent support, or in locations where suture removal is not practical.

    Non-absorbable sutures resist enzymatic digestion and remain indefinitely unless physically removed. They are used for permanent tissue approximation, vessel ligation, or in tissues that heal slowly.

    By Origin¶

    Natural (biological) sutures are derived from organic sources: - Plain gut: derived from sheep submucosa or beef serosa; rapidly absorbed (7–10 days) - Chromic gut: plain gut treated with chromium salts to delay absorption (10–21 days); less reactive than plain gut

    Natural sutures trigger more inflammatory response than synthetic materials and have variable absorption rates.

    Synthetic sutures are manufactured from polymers. They are more predictable, have lower tissue reactivity, and offer better tensile strength retention: - Polyglycolic acid, polyglactin 910, poliglecaprone, polydioxanone, nylon, polypropylene, polyester, etc.

    By Structure¶

    Monofilament: A single strand. Advantages: less capillarity (reduces bacterial wicking), smooth passage through tissue. Disadvantages: higher memory (stiff, springy), less knot security — requires more throws.

    Multifilament (braided or twisted): Multiple strands woven together. Advantages: easier handling, excellent knot security, pliable. Disadvantages: higher capillarity, slightly more tissue reaction.

    7.2 Common Suture Materials¶

    Absorbable Sutures¶

    Suture Type Absorption Common Uses
    Plain Gut Natural mono/multi 7–10 days Mucosa, ligation
    Chromic Gut Natural mono 10–21 days GI, OB/GYN, mucosa
    Polyglycolic acid (Dexon) Synthetic braided 60–90 days Fascial closure, GI
    Polyglactin 910 (Vicryl) Synthetic braided 56–70 days Fascia, subcutaneous, GI
    Vicryl Rapide Synthetic braided 10–14 days Skin, mucosa
    Poliglecaprone (Monocryl) Synthetic mono 91–119 days Subcuticular skin closure
    Polydioxanone (PDS) Synthetic mono 180+ days Fascial closure, pediatric cardiac
    Glycomer 631 (Biosyn) Synthetic mono 90–110 days General soft tissue

    Non-Absorbable Sutures¶

    Suture Type Characteristics Common Uses
    Silk Natural braided Excellent handling, high capillarity Vessel ties, cardiovascular (decreasing in favor of synthetics)
    Nylon (Ethilon, Dermalon) Synthetic mono High memory, strong Skin closure, ophthalmology, neurosurgery
    Polypropylene (Prolene, Surgilene) Synthetic mono Very smooth, minimal tissue reaction Vascular anastomosis, hernia, skin
    Polyester (Mersilene, Ethibond) Synthetic braided High tensile strength Cardiac, valve repair, orthopedic
    Polybutester (Novafil) Synthetic mono Slight elasticity Skin closure
    Stainless steel wire Metal mono or multi Maximum tensile strength, non-absorbable Sternal closure, orthopedic

    7.3 Suture Sizing¶

    Suture size refers to the diameter of the strand. The sizing system is counter-intuitive: - Increasing numbers (e.g., #0, #1, #2): larger diameter, heavier suture - Increasing zeros (e.g., 2-0, 3-0, 4-0, 5-0): smaller diameter, finer suture - #1 is larger than 2-0 (2-0 is two zeros; larger number of zeros = finer)

    Size Relative Diameter Common Use
    #5 Very heavy Sternal wire
    #2 Heavy Tendon repair, fascial retention
    #1 Heavy Mass closure of abdomen
    #0 Standard Fascial closure
    2-0 Moderate Deep fascial layer
    3-0 Medium Subcutaneous, muscle
    4-0 Fine Skin, vascular
    5-0 Very fine Vascular anastomosis, microsurgery
    6-0 to 9-0 Extremely fine Ophthalmology, microsurgery

    7.4 Reading a Suture Package¶

    Every suture package contains critical information the scrub technologist must be able to read quickly:

    1. Suture material name (generic and brand)
    2. Size (e.g., 3-0, 2-0)
    3. Length (e.g., 18 inches, 27 inches)
    4. Needle type and size (e.g., SH — small half-circle taper; FS-2 — free swaged cutting)
    5. Absorbable or non-absorbable
    6. Lot number and expiration date
    7. Number of sutures in packet (often a pack of 12 with individual foil packets inside)

    On the Sterile Field¶

    • Open suture package with the opening pointing toward your sterile field; allow the inner foil packet to drop onto the field
    • Open the inner packet and remove the suture carefully, preserving the needle
    • Load the needle into the needle holder at the upper 2/3 (between the tip and midpoint), perpendicular to the jaws
    • Present the needle holder to the surgeon with the needle pointing toward the tissue; control the free tail

    7.5 Surgical Needles¶

    A needle's design directly affects its performance. Understanding needle anatomy allows the scrub tech to select and load correctly.

    Needle Anatomy¶

    • Eye (swaged or closed): where suture is attached; swaged (eyeless) needles are standard
    • Body: the structural section; may be round, half-round, or flat
    • Point: the tip that penetrates tissue

    Needle Curvature¶

    Curvature Degrees Use
    Straight 0° Skin closure with fingers
    1/4 circle 90° Ophthalmic, superficial
    3/8 circle 135° Most general tissue; most common
    1/2 circle 180° Deep, confined spaces
    5/8 circle 225° Very deep; GI, GU
    J-needle J-shape Laparoscopic port closure

    Needle Point Types¶

    Point Type Description Use
    Taper (round) Tapers to a point; no cutting edge Fascia, bowel, vessel
    Cutting Two or three sharp edges; triangular cross-section Skin, dense tissue
    Reverse cutting Cutting edge on outer curve; reduces tissue tear-through Skin, fibrous tissue (preferred over conventional cutting)
    Tapercut Taper body with small cutting tip Fascia, calcified tissue
    Blunt Rounded tip; does not cut Liver, kidney, blunt dissection
    Spatula Flat body with cutting edges Ophthalmic; superficial layers

    7.6 Wound Closure Devices¶

    Suture is not the only method of wound closure. The scrub tech must be familiar with all available options.

    Surgical Staples¶

    Skin staplers: Disposable devices that fire individual stainless steel staples into skin edges, holding them in approximation. Faster than suture for long straight incisions. Common in abdominal, orthopedic, and spinal procedures. - Pass the stapler to the surgeon with the firing end toward the tissue - Skin staples are removed with a dedicated staple remover (not a hemostat)

    Internal staplers: Used for bowel anastomosis, lung resection, and vessel division. - Linear stapler (GIA — Gastrointestinal Anastomosis): fires two parallel rows of staples and simultaneously cuts between them; used for bowel division and lung stapling - Circular stapler (EEA — End-to-End Anastomosis): creates a circular staple line for end-to-end bowel anastomosis - Linear non-cutting (TA — Thoracoabdominal): fires two rows of staples without cutting; used to close a stump

    Loading units: Many staplers use replaceable cartridges (loads). Cartridges are color-coded by staple height (tissue thickness). The scrub tech must identify the correct load per surgeon instruction.

    Vascular and Hemostatic Clips¶

    • Ligaclips (titanium or absorbable polymer clips): Applied with a clip applier to ligate vessels; permanent or absorbable versions available
    • Hemoclips: Small metal clips for vessel control; remain in patient permanently

    Skin Closure Strips (Steri-Strips)¶

    Adhesive-backed paper or nylon strips applied across a wound for superficial reinforcement. Used alone for small wounds or after subcuticular closure to take tension off the suture line.

    Tissue Adhesive (Dermabond, Histoacryl)¶

    A cyanoacrylate-based liquid adhesive applied topically to skin edges held in approximation. Polymerizes on contact and creates a flexible, waterproof bond. - Used for small, low-tension wounds in cooperative patients (and in pediatrics to avoid suture removal) - Not suitable for high-tension or infected wounds - On the sterile field: handle with care — adhesive sets immediately on gloves

    Retention Sutures (Stay Sutures)¶

    Heavy, non-absorbable sutures placed through all layers of the abdominal wall to distribute tension in high-risk closures (obesity, malnourishment, multiple re-operations). Protective bolsters are placed under the suture bridges to prevent skin cutting.

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "The right suture for the right tissue — it's not one size fits all."

    • When loading suture on a needle holder, load it at 2/3 down the needle body — not at the tip (where it can break), not at the swage (where it weakens the suture attachment).
    • Monofilament sutures (Prolene, nylon, PDS) have memory. Pre-stretch them slightly by pulling through a moist gauze to reduce springback.
    • When passing suture to the surgeon, keep the tail organized — let it fall to one side and don't let it tangle with other supplies on the field.
    • Know the difference between the Vicryl the surgeon uses for fascia (#1), subcutaneous (3-0), and skin (4-0). All three may be open at once; keeping them distinct matters.
