    Chapter 25: Specimen Handling and Documentation¶

    25.1 Types of Surgical Specimens¶

    Tissue Specimens¶

    The most common type. Tissue removed during surgery is submitted for histopathological examination to: - Confirm or establish a diagnosis (e.g., cancer, infection) - Verify surgical margins (is all the tumor removed?) - Guide further treatment - Establish staging

    Tissue integrity depends on timely and appropriate handling. Autolysis (self-digestion by cellular enzymes) begins immediately after removal. Fixation stops this process.

    Fluid Specimens¶

    • Peritoneal fluid, pleural fluid, joint fluid: sent for cell count, glucose, protein, LDH, culture
    • Bile, abscess contents: sent for culture and sensitivity
    • Lavage fluid: from peritoneal washings in suspected cancer; cytology

    Microbiology (Culture) Specimens¶

    • Tissue from infected sites
    • Swabs from wounds
    • Fluid from joints, abscesses, pleural/peritoneal spaces
    • Do not fix culture specimens — fixatives kill organisms; culture requires viable tissue
    • Transport in culture media (aerobic + anaerobic) or in a sterile container

    Foreign Bodies¶

    Retained hardware, bullets, needles, or other foreign material may be removed and submitted. Chain-of-custody requirements are especially important for medico-legal specimens (bullets, fragments from traumatic injuries). Some may require law enforcement involvement.

    Implant-Associated Specimens¶

    Removed joint prostheses, explanted devices, infected hardware.

    25.2 Specimen Labeling and Chain of Custody¶

    Every specimen must be accurately identified from the moment of removal to the moment it is received in the laboratory.

    Minimum Labeling Requirements¶

    • Patient name (first and last)
    • Patient date of birth or MRN
    • Date of collection
    • Specimen type (what tissue/fluid it is)
    • Source/anatomical location (right breast, left lobe thyroid, appendix, etc.)
    • Surgeon name
    • Special instructions (e.g., "oriented specimen — clip at superior pole," "fresh for frozen section")

    Labeling Process¶

    1. The surgeon removes the specimen; passes it to the scrub technologist
    2. The scrub tech accepts the specimen without touching the gloved/scrubbed hands — the specimen is placed in a basin or passed directly
    3. The scrub tech immediately passes the specimen to the circulator, verbally identifying it: "Right axillary sentinel lymph node, left and right clip for orientation"
    4. The circulator places it in a correctly labeled container with appropriate fixative or transport medium
    5. The circulator documents in the operative record and on the pathology requisition

    Orientation Markers¶

    For specimens that have a specific orientation relevant to margin assessment: - Marking sutures (e.g., "short suture = superior, long suture = lateral") - Metal clips placed by the scrub tech per surgeon instruction - Ink (applied by pathology at the bench) These allow pathology to assess which margins are positive and where in the specimen the margins occur.

    Chain of Custody¶

    • Specimens must not be left unattended between removal and laboratory receipt
    • Transfer must be documented: who transferred it, to whom, at what time
    • Medico-legal specimens (bullets, foreign bodies in trauma) require especially rigorous custody documentation, including law enforcement chain of custody forms

    25.3 Fixatives¶

    10% Neutral Buffered Formalin (NBF)¶

    • Standard fixative for routine surgical pathology
    • Volume: minimum 10:1 fixative-to-tissue ratio
    • Allow fixation time proportional to specimen thickness (at least 24 hours for most specimens before processing)
    • Do not fix specimens for culture or cytology — formalin kills organisms and cells

    Saline¶

    • Transport medium for specimens to be reviewed fresh (before fixation)
    • Used for frozen section specimens (they must arrive unfixed to be cut on the cryostat)
    • Some surgeons pass small fresh specimens in saline for immediate inspection, then transfer to formalin

    Dry (No Fixative)¶

    • Calculi (kidney stones): sent dry for stone analysis (mineralogy)
    • Some hardware/foreign bodies

    Special Media¶

    • Culture media (thioglycollate broth, aerobic/anaerobic swab containers): for microbiological culture
    • RPMI / M-199 medium: for flow cytometry (lymphoma workup), chromosomal studies
    • Glutaraldehyde: for electron microscopy

    25.4 Frozen Section¶

    A frozen section is an intraoperative pathological consultation in which: 1. Fresh (unfixed) tissue is sent directly to pathology 2. The tissue is embedded in OCT (optimal cutting temperature) compound, frozen on a cryostat 3. Sections are cut and stained (H&E or similar) 4. A pathologist evaluates the section under the microscope 5. A verbal result is communicated back to the OR — typically within 15–20 minutes

    When frozen sections are used: - Determine if margins are clear before closing (oncologic surgery) - Confirm tissue identity (e.g., parathyroid gland vs. lymph node vs. fat) - Assess sentinel lymph nodes for metastasis

    Limitations of frozen section: - Accuracy less than permanent section (artifacts of freezing; tissue distortion) - Some tissues freeze poorly (fatty tissue, calcified tissue) - Pathologist may defer difficult cases to permanent sections ("defer to perm")

    Scrub tech responsibilities: - Identify frozen section specimen to the circulator verbally and clearly - Keep fresh — do not allow tissue to dry; place on a moist telfa or sponge if not immediately transferred - Do not place in formalin — this destroys the ability to freeze the tissue - Document the time the specimen was sent and the time the result was received

    25.5 Implant Documentation and Tracking¶

    All implantable devices placed in patients must be documented per regulatory requirements (Safe Medical Devices Act of 1990; FDA Medical Device Tracking requirements).

    Required documentation for each implant: - Device name and description - Manufacturer - Catalog number - Lot/serial number - Size - Expiration date (if applicable) - Date implanted - Patient name and MRN

    This information is entered into the operative record, the implant log, and may be sent to a centralized device registry.

    Why it matters: If a device is recalled, the manufacturer and facility must be able to identify every patient in whom that device was implanted. Missing documentation means patients cannot be notified.

    The scrub tech's role: - Retain all implant packaging until the case is closed and documentation is complete - Read the lot number and size from each opened package to the circulator for documentation - Do not discard packaging until the circulator confirms documentation

    25.6 Surgical Counts Documentation¶

    Every count result — initial, closing, and final — is documented in the operative record by the circulating nurse. The scrub tech confirms accuracy.

    Documentation includes: - Count correct/incorrect - What was counted (sponges, needles, instruments) - Count performed by (scrub tech name, circulator name) - Any discrepancy and the action taken - Disposition if discrepancy was not resolved (X-ray obtained, surgeon notified, etc.)

    Clinical Pearls¶

    "Every specimen that leaves your field carries a patient's diagnosis with it. Handle it accordingly."

    • Never pass a specimen to the circulator without verbally confirming its identity. A mislabeled specimen can result in an incorrect cancer diagnosis — a harm that may be irreversible.
    • If you are unsure which fixative to use, ask before the case. Some specimens need to be in culture media within minutes; formalin cannot be undone.
    • Keep implant stickers/packaging until you hear "confirmed" from the circulator.
