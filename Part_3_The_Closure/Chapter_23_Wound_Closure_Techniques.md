# Chapter 23: Wound Closure Techniques

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Describe the layers of wound closure and the rationale for each.
2. Identify common suture techniques and their applications.
3. Describe retention sutures and their indications.
4. Explain the scrub technologist's role during wound closure.

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Approximation** | Bringing tissue edges together for healing |
| **Tension** | Force applied across a wound; excessive tension impairs healing |
| **Dead space** | Empty space within a wound where fluid can accumulate |
| **Eversion** | Turning skin edges slightly outward; promotes better healing |
| **Inversion** | Turning tissue edges inward; used in bowel anastomosis |
| **Interrupted suture** | Individual sutures each tied separately |
| **Continuous (running) suture** | Single suture strand placed in a series without cutting between throws |
| **Subcuticular (intradermal)** | Suture placed within the dermis, parallel to the skin surface |
| **Retention sutures** | Heavy through-and-through sutures distributing tension across a high-risk abdominal wound |

---

## 23.1 Principles of Wound Closure

The goal of closure is to create the conditions in which the body can heal:
- Approximate tissue layers without excessive tension
- Eliminate dead space where fluid can collect
- Provide enough strength to withstand mechanical forces during healing
- Minimize foreign material left in the wound
- Achieve cosmetically acceptable skin closure when possible

Closure proceeds in anatomical layers — the same layers that were divided during the approach, restored in reverse order.

---

## 23.2 Layer-by-Layer Closure

### Peritoneum
The peritoneum is not always closed — evidence supporting routine peritoneal closure in laparotomy is mixed. When closed, it is approximated with a continuous absorbable suture (e.g., 3-0 Vicryl). The scrub tech must have irrigation ready — the peritoneal cavity may be irrigated with warm saline before closure.

### Fascia
The strongest layer — fascial closure bears the mechanical load of the closed abdomen. Dehiscence here results in evisceration (a surgical emergency).
- Mass closure (en masse) of the linea alba: continuous or interrupted heavy absorbable suture (0 or #1 PDS or looped PDS)
- Suture bites should be at least 1 cm from the fascial edge and 1 cm apart
- Running closure should be approximately 4:1 (suture length: wound length ratio)

The scrub tech loads suture correctly, manages the needle, and may load multiple needles in advance for a running fascial closure.

### Subcutaneous Tissue
Closed with absorbable suture (3-0 Vicryl) to eliminate dead space and take tension off the skin. Not closed when the wound is contaminated (Class III/IV) — leaving this layer open allows drainage.

### Skin
Multiple closure options (see below). In contaminated wounds, skin may be left open with wet-to-dry dressings and closed 3–5 days later (delayed primary closure).

## 23.3 Suture Techniques

### Interrupted Sutures
Individual sutures; each tied separately.

**Simple interrupted**: single suture passes through both wound edges; tied; cut. If one suture fails, the others hold.

**Mattress sutures:**
- **Vertical mattress (Donati)**: deep and superficial bites in a U configuration; excellent eversion; used for tension and wound edges with poor blood supply
- **Horizontal mattress**: parallel bites; compresses tissue; used for tension, hemostasis, fascia

**Figure-of-eight**: two crossing bites tied together; strong; used for fascia

**Indications for interrupted skin:** Areas subject to tension, areas where suture removal must be staged, contaminated wounds (can remove alternate sutures to allow drainage)

### Continuous (Running) Sutures
A single suture strand placed in a series; faster than interrupted; distributes tension evenly.

**Simple running**: continuous simple suture; fast; one failure compromises the entire suture line
**Running locking (blanket stitch)**: suture loops back through the previous throw; hemostatic; used for fascial closure, bowel
**Running mattress**: continuous horizontal mattress; skin closure
**Subcuticular (intradermal)**: suture placed horizontally within the dermis; cosmetically superior; absorbable or removable via pull-through; no suture marks on skin

### Subcuticular Closure
The preferred skin closure when cosmesis is important:
1. Enter dermis at one end of the wound
2. Advance through the dermis in alternating bites on each side
3. Exit dermis at the far end and tie; or use locking knot
4. Use absorbable suture (Monocryl, Vicryl Rapide) for buried; non-absorbable (Prolene) for pull-through removal

### Purse-String Suture
A circular continuous suture around a circular opening (e.g., stoma site, gastrostomy site, colostomy). Tightened to close the opening.

## 23.4 Bowel Closure Techniques

In GI surgery, bowel anastomoses require specific suture techniques:

**Two-layer hand-sewn anastomosis:**
- **Inner layer (mucosal)**: full-thickness continuous absorbable suture (Connell or Cushing for inverting); brings mucosa-to-mucosa
- **Outer layer (seromuscular, Lembert suture)**: interrupted non-absorbable inverting sutures that bury the inner layer

**One-layer anastomosis:**
- Single layer of interrupted full-thickness sutures (Gambee stitch); preserves luminal diameter; widely used

**Stapled anastomosis:**
- GIA and/or EEA staplers (see Chapter 13); everts tissue at staple line

## 23.5 Retention Sutures

Retention sutures are heavy, through-and-through sutures placed to reinforce the fascial closure in high-risk abdominal wounds where dehiscence is likely.

**Indications:**
- Morbid obesity
- Malnourished or cachectic patients
- Multiple prior abdominal surgeries
- Immunosuppressed (steroids, chemotherapy)
- Intra-abdominal infection
- Postoperative ileus/distension expected

**Materials:** #2–#5 nylon or polypropylene; or heavy braided sutures

**Technique:**
- Placed at 2–3 cm intervals through all layers (skin, subcutaneous, fascia, occasionally peritoneum)
- **Bolsters** (short lengths of rubber tubing, plastic, or gauze rolls) placed under the suture bridges to distribute pressure and prevent suture cutting through the skin

Retention sutures are typically removed 2–3 weeks postoperatively; standard fascial sutures are permanent or absorbable with long retention.

---

## 23.6 The Scrub Technologist's Role at Closure

Closure phase is not less demanding than the procedure — it requires the same level of focus.

**At closure:**
- Perform final count (see Chapter 6) before the surgeon begins fascial closure
- Have sutures loaded and ready for each layer in sequence
- Keep track of needles through the closure — every needle passed must be accounted for
- Ensure irrigation is ready if the surgeon irrigates before closing peritoneum
- Keep the field clean — sponges removed from the wound before closure, not after

**Final check before skin:**
- Instruments, sponges, sharps counts all reconciled
- Drain sutured in place if present
- Implants documented
- Specimen handled and labeled

---

## Clinical Pearls

> "The closure tells the whole story. A hasty close undoes a perfect procedure."

- In long running fascial closures, manage the suture tail — don't let it tangle in the wound. Keep it draped cleanly across the field.
- When subcuticular sutures are placed, keep the suture moist if using absorbable — dry Monocryl becomes stiff and difficult to handle.
- If a count is off when closure begins, the surgeon must wait. Do not let a surgeon close on an unresolved count discrepancy.
