# Chapter 26: Postoperative Care Transitions

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the learner will be able to:

1. Describe the scrub technologist's responsibilities at the end of a surgical case.
2. Explain the safe patient transfer process from the OR table.
3. Identify common postoperative complications and their early signs.
4. Describe OR room turnover and terminal cleaning procedures.

---

## 26.1 End-of-Case Responsibilities

The case is not over when the last suture is placed. The scrub technologist has a defined set of responsibilities that complete the procedure and prepare for safe patient transfer.

### Before Patient Leaves the Table
1. **Final count confirmed**: all instruments, sponges, and sharps reconciled — documented by circulator
2. **Wound dressed**: dressing applied before transfer (minimizes contamination during transfer)
3. **Drains secured**: all drains sutured in place; not kinked; labeled
4. **Specimens passed**: all specimens in labeled containers with appropriate fixative; documentation complete
5. **Implant documentation**: all implant labels collected; documentation confirmed by circulator
6. **Electrosurgery tip holstered and accounted for**: pencil secured and counted
7. **Sharps secured**: all needles in needle box or magnetic needle pad; counted

### Breaking Down the Sterile Field
After the patient leaves the OR:
1. Do not break the sterile field until the patient is out of the room — contamination can still affect the patient if they return, and premature breakdown creates confusion about counts
2. Dispose of sharps in a sharps container (never in regular trash)
3. Dispose of sponges and used supplies in the appropriate waste containers
4. Collect instruments for transport back to sterile processing
5. All instruments should remain moist — use a pre-soak spray or wet towel; do not allow blood to dry onto instruments (makes decontamination more difficult and damages instruments)

## 26.2 Patient Transfer from the OR Table

Patient transfer is a high-risk moment — falls, line/tube dislodgement, and position-related injury can occur.

### Transfer Principles
- **Minimum four people** for transfer of an adult patient from OR table to transport stretcher
- Anesthesia provider maintains the airway and directs the transfer at the head
- Three personnel along the patient's side, positioned at shoulders, hips, and legs
- Use a **slide board or roller** to minimize lifting
- All IV lines, monitoring leads, urinary catheter, and drains are managed during the transfer (assign one person to manage lines)
- Move the patient on the count of "1–2–3" — unified, coordinated movement
- **Lock wheels** on both the OR table and the stretcher before transfer

### After Transfer
- Reconnect monitoring on the transport stretcher
- Ensure the patient is covered and warm — transport is cold
- Confirm the patient's airway is secured if still intubated
- Confirm urinary catheter drains freely; no kinks
- Confirm IV lines are intact

## 26.3 Report and Handoff to PACU

The **SBAR framework** (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) is the standard structure for patient handoffs:

| Component | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Situation** | Patient name, age, procedure performed, surgeon, anesthesia provider |
| **Background** | Relevant medical history, allergies, blood type, antibiotic given |
| **Assessment** | Estimated blood loss (EBL), fluid received, urine output, any intraoperative events or complications |
| **Recommendation** | Anticipated needs: pain management, drains and what to expect from them, wound dressing, postop orders, any concerns |

The scrub technologist is typically not the primary person giving handoff report — this is the circulating nurse and anesthesia provider. However, the scrub tech may be asked about intraoperative events, count results, or specimen status.

## 26.4 Common Postoperative Complications

While the scrub technologist's primary role ends when the patient leaves the OR, awareness of postoperative complications is part of comprehensive surgical education.

### Hemorrhage
- **Primary hemorrhage**: at the time of surgery (inadequate hemostasis)
- **Reactive hemorrhage**: within 24 hours as vasospasm resolves; blood pressure normalizes after anesthesia
- **Secondary hemorrhage**: 7–14 days postoperatively; slipping of a ligature; infection eroding a vessel

Signs: tachycardia, hypotension, decreased urine output, drain output >150 mL/hr, expanding hematoma

### Surgical Site Infection (SSI)
Signs: fever (usually >48 hours postop), erythema, warmth, swelling, purulent drainage from wound
Treatment: wound opening and drainage, antibiotics, wound care

### Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
- DVT: clot in deep veins (usually lower extremity); presents with calf pain, swelling, erythema
- PE: clot travels to pulmonary vasculature; presents with sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, cardiovascular collapse
- Prevention: sequential compression devices (SCDs), early ambulation, pharmacologic prophylaxis

### Pulmonary Complications
- **Atelectasis**: partial lung collapse; most common postop complication; prevented by incentive spirometry
- **Pneumonia**: especially in patients with prolonged intubation or poor respiratory effort
- **Aspiration**: more common with general anesthesia; gastric contents into the airway

### Ileus
Bowel dysfunction after abdominal surgery; bowel motility returns typically in 2–3 days for small bowel, 3–5 days for colon. Prolonged ileus (>5 days) may require NGT decompression.

### Urinary Retention
Common after pelvic surgery, spinal anesthesia, opioid use; managed with urinary catheterization.

### Anastomotic Leak
Failure of a bowel anastomosis; presents 3–7 days postop with fever, abdominal pain, tachycardia. Serious complication requiring reoperation.

### Wound Dehiscence
Fascial closure failure; may present with serosanguineous drainage ("salmon-colored drainage") before it is visually obvious. Risk of evisceration if full separation occurs. Evisceration = cover wound with moist saline gauze; emergent return to OR.

## 26.5 OR Turnover and Terminal Cleaning

### Between-Case Cleaning (Turnover)
After each case:
1. All soiled drapes, gowns, and materials are removed
2. All contaminated instruments collected and transported to sterile processing in closed containers
3. OR table cleaned with a hospital-grade disinfectant
4. All horizontal surfaces (lights, IV pole, anesthesia workstation) cleaned with disinfectant
5. Floor cleaned
6. Room restocked; new sterile supplies brought in for the next case

### Terminal Cleaning (End of Day)
At the end of the surgical day, each OR receives a terminal clean:
- All surfaces cleaned top-to-bottom with a hospital-grade disinfectant
- Walls spot-cleaned
- Floor scrubbed
- Waste removed
- All fixed equipment cleaned: OR table, lights, anesthesia machine, stands, monitors

### Isolation Cases
Contact precaution cases (MRSA, VRE, C. difficile):
- Terminal clean with appropriate disinfectant (C. difficile requires 10:1 bleach solution — sporicidal)
- Personal protective equipment for cleaning staff
- Room may be taken out of service until terminal clean is complete

## 26.6 The Complete Surgical Experience — Reflection

The surgical technologist who has mastered the ApC framework — Approach, Procedure, Closure — is not simply a person who hands instruments. They are:

- A patient advocate who maintains the sterile field even when no one is watching
- A team member who communicates clearly and professionally in moments of tension
- A technical expert who understands the anatomy, the equipment, the procedure, and the plan
- A safety officer who counts, labels, documents, and speaks up when something is wrong

Every patient on the OR table is someone's parent, partner, or child. They are unconscious, vulnerable, and entirely dependent on the team surrounding them. That team is only as strong as its least attentive member.

The ApC's are not just chapters in a textbook. They are the structure of professional practice and the framework of a career built in service of the most vulnerable moments in a human being's life.

Welcome to the profession.
