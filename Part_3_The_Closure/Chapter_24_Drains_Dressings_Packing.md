# Chapter 24: Drains, Dressings, and Packing

---

## 24.1 Surgical Drains

A surgical drain removes blood, serum, bile, lymph, or air from a wound cavity, preventing accumulation that would otherwise create dead space, promote infection, or impair healing.

### Types of Drains

**Closed-suction drains**
Maintain a vacuum, actively drawing fluid from the wound cavity.
| Drain | Description | Common Use |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Jackson-Pratt (JP)** | Flat, perforated silicone drain; connected to a compressible bulb reservoir | Breast surgery, soft tissue dissection, general surgery |
| **Hemovac** | Flat, perforated drain; connected to a spring-loaded collapsible cylinder reservoir | Orthopedic (joint arthroplasty), larger wounds |

To activate: empty the reservoir; compress it fully; cap it — vacuum created draws fluid.

**Passive drains**
Rely on gravity and pressure differential rather than active suction.
| Drain | Description | Use |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| **Penrose drain** | Soft, flat, latex or silicone tube; no suction; fluid wicks along exterior | Abscess cavities, contaminated wounds |
| **Sump drain** | Double-lumen; air enters one lumen while fluid drains through the other | Pancreatic, large abdominal cavities |
| **Malecot catheter** | Multi-flanged mushroom-shaped catheter | Nephrostomy, biliary drainage |

**Chest tubes**
Large-bore (20–40 Fr) tube placed in the pleural space to drain air, blood, or fluid. Connected to a **water-seal drainage system** (Pleur-evac):
- Chamber 1: water seal (one-way valve)
- Chamber 2: suction control
- Chamber 3: drainage collection
Continuously monitor for air leak (bubbling in water seal chamber).

### Drain Placement
Drains are placed by the surgeon; the scrub tech provides:
- Drain of appropriate type and size
- Clamp to pull drain through the skin exit site (counter-incision)
- Suture for securing the drain to the skin (typically 2-0 nylon, interrupted, around the drain)
- Drain collected in a sterile manner until the circulator can connect to the reservoir

**Securing the drain:** A single suture through the skin around the drain, tied snugly but not occlusive. The drain must not kink. Mark the drain exit site with a small amount of benzoin and adhesive dressing.

## 24.2 Wound Dressings

A wound dressing:
- Protects the wound from exogenous contamination
- Absorbs wound drainage
- Maintains an appropriate moisture environment
- Provides mechanical protection

### Basic Dressing Layers (Three-Layer Principle)
1. **Primary layer** (contact layer): directly contacts the wound surface
2. **Secondary layer** (absorptive layer): absorbs drainage
3. **Tertiary layer** (outer layer): holds the dressing in place; adds mechanical protection

### Dressing Types

**Gauze dressings**
Most basic and versatile.
- **Dry sterile gauze**: placed over closed wounds with minimal drainage; clean/clean-contaminated
- **Wet-to-dry**: saline-moistened gauze packed into open wounds; allows debridement as the dressing dries and is removed; standard for contaminated open wounds

**Non-adherent dressings**
- **Telfa (non-adherent pad)**: contact layer for wounds where sticking would be traumatic (donor sites, skin grafts)
- **Adaptic / Xeroform**: fine-mesh gauze impregnated with petrolatum or bismuth tribromophenate; non-adherent; used for donor sites, burns, skin grafts

**Film dressings**
- **Tegaderm / OpSite**: transparent, semipermeable adhesive film; waterproof; allows wound visualization without removal
- Used for superficial wounds, IV sites, covering closed surgical incisions

**Foam dressings**
Absorptive; used for moderately to heavily draining wounds.

**Hydrocolloid dressings**
- **DuoDERM**: absorbs drainage; creates gel over wound; promotes moist wound healing; not for infected wounds

**Alginate dressings**
Derived from seaweed; highly absorptive (absorbs up to 20× their weight); used for heavily draining wounds.

**Antimicrobial dressings**
- Silver-impregnated dressings (Mepilex Ag, Aquacel Ag): broad-spectrum antimicrobial; used for burns, chronically infected wounds

### Applying Dressings in the OR
The scrub tech applies the initial wound dressing — typically just before the patient is transferred off the OR table.
1. Confirm final count is complete
2. Wipe any skin prep residue from around the wound edges
3. Apply primary layer (non-adherent if needed)
4. Apply absorptive gauze layer
5. Secure with silk tape, paper tape, or foam tape

For abdominal wounds: 4×4 gauze + ABD (abdominal) pad + tape. For facial wounds: Telfa or non-adherent + gentle securing. For orthopedic: bulky wrap or splint per surgeon direction.

## 24.3 Wound Packing

Open contaminated wounds may be packed rather than closed primarily.

**Purpose:** Allow drainage to escape; prevent closure of infected or contaminated space before the wound can heal; promote granulation tissue formation.

**Materials:**
- Moist gauze (saline-moistened) or iodoform gauze (antiseptic-impregnated)
- Packed loosely — tight packing impedes healing and can cause pressure necrosis
- Changed daily or every 2–3 days per wound care orders

**Packing an open wound in the OR:**
1. Irrigate cavity copiously with warm saline
2. Loosely fill with moist gauze using ring forceps; do not pack tightly
3. Count packing material used (or document that the cavity was packed without formal count)
4. Cover with outer absorptive dressing

## 24.4 Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT / Wound VAC)

NPWT uses continuous or intermittent subatmospheric (negative) pressure applied to a wound to:
- Remove wound exudate
- Reduce wound edema
- Promote granulation tissue
- Mechanically contract the wound edges
- Improve blood flow to the wound

**Indications:** Open wounds, fasciotomies, split-thickness skin graft bolstering, sternal wounds, diabetic foot ulcers, complex traumatic wounds

**System (e.g., KCI ACTIVA, 3M PREVENA):**
1. **Foam (reticulated polyurethane or gauze-type)**: placed in the wound to fill the space
2. **Occlusive drape**: sealed over the foam and surrounding skin
3. **Suction tubing**: exits through a small hole in the drape; connected to the NPWT device
4. **Device**: applies vacuum at a set pressure (typically –125 mmHg continuous)

**On the sterile field:** The scrub tech provides the VAC foam, trimmed to fit the wound; the occlusive adhesive drape; and the tubing. The device itself is non-sterile and managed by the circulator.

## Clinical Pearls

> "The dressing protects everything that was accomplished in the OR. Apply it with the same care you brought to the case."

- When applying dressings over a drain site, ensure the drain is clearly visible through or at the edge of the dressing — nursing staff need to assess and empty it.
- Wound VAC foam must fill the wound completely — if it doesn't, it creates dead space. Cut and piece foam to fill all recesses.
- If a drain is accidentally removed during dressing application, do not attempt to reinsert it — notify the surgeon.
