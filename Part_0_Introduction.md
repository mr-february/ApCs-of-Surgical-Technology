# Introduction: The World of Surgical Technology

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this introduction, the learner will be able to:

1. Describe the historical evolution of surgical technology as a profession.
2. Define the role of the surgical technologist within the perioperative team.
3. Explain the ApC framework and how it applies to every surgical procedure.
4. Recognize the Approach, Procedure, and Closure within any case.

## The Beginning of a Career Built in the OR

Surgery is one of humanity's oldest interventions. Long before antisepsis, anesthesia, or the concept of sterile technique, healers performed operations using rudimentary tools and hard-won knowledge. What has remained constant throughout history is the need for a skilled, focused, and knowledgeable support system around the surgeon — someone who understands the anatomy, anticipates the next step, and ensures the environment remains as safe as possible for the patient.

That role belongs to the **Surgical Technologist**.

Surgical technology as a formal profession emerged in the mid-twentieth century, largely as a result of the demands of World War II, when the military trained personnel to assist surgeons in field hospitals under austere conditions. After the war, hospitals recognized the value of trained OR support staff and began developing formal programs. In 1969, the **Association of Surgical Technologists (AST)** was established, followed by the **National Board of Surgical Technology and Surgical Assisting (NBSTSA)**, which administers the Certified Surgical Technologist (CST) examination. Today, more than 100,000 surgical technologists work in hospitals, outpatient surgery centers, and specialty clinics across the United States.

## The ApC Framework: A Universal Language for Every Surgery

Every surgical procedure ever performed — from a simple skin biopsy to an 18-hour free flap reconstruction — obeys the same fundamental structure. Three phases. Every time. Without exception.

> **The Approach. The Procedure. The Closure.**

These are not abstract organizational categories. They are the physical, sequential reality of surgery. And as a surgical technologist, your ability to be useful at any step of any case depends on knowing exactly which phase you are in — and what is required of you at that moment.

---

### The Approach: Getting In

The Approach is everything that takes the surgical team from a sterile, undraped patient to a fully exposed, well-visualized operative field. It is how the surgery begins.

The Approach always includes the same elements, applied in the specific way that matches the case:

**1. Draping**
Every procedure begins with the creation of a sterile field around the operative site. The drapes define the boundaries of the surgeon's world. A laparotomy uses a fenestrated lap sheet and four draping towels to frame the abdominal incision. A cataract uses a small aperture eye drape. A shoulder arthroscopy uses a split sheet draped around the elevated arm. The drape is always different. The principle is always the same: isolate the operative field from contamination.

**2. Gown and Gloves**
The scrubbed team enters the sterile field gowned and gloved. This is true for every case, from the fastest minor procedure to the most complex reconstruction. The gown and gloves are the interface between the human beings performing the surgery and the sterile environment protecting the patient. When this interface is compromised — a glove tear, a gown contamination — it must be corrected immediately, regardless of what phase the surgery is in.

**3. The Knife: Initial Incision**
At some point in every approach, something cuts through tissue to gain entry. In open surgery, it is a scalpel — a specific blade on a specific handle, placed at a specific anatomical location. In laparoscopic surgery, it is the initial port incision and trocar placement. In endoscopic surgery, it is the introduction of the scope through a natural orifice. The knife (in its broadest sense) is the moment the approach transitions from preparation to action. The scrub tech must have the correct blade loaded, mounted safely, and ready to place the moment the surgeon calls for it.

**4. Retractors**
Once the incision is made, the goal is exposure — holding tissue out of the surgeon's line of sight so they can see and safely operate on the target structure. Retractors do this. Hand-held retractors are placed and held by the assistant (often the scrub tech in a two-person team). Self-retaining retractors hold themselves. Different procedures require entirely different retractor systems: Army-Navys for superficial skin, Balfours for the open abdomen, Finochietto spreaders for the chest, Weitlaners for spinal surgery. The scrub tech must have the right retractors ready, positioned, and — when holding them — must maintain steady, consistent exposure throughout the case.

---

### The Procedure: The Surgery Itself

Once the field is open and well-exposed, the operative work begins. This is the most procedure-specific phase — the steps differ entirely based on the surgery being performed. But even within the procedure, the scrub tech's core role remains constant: anticipate the next instrument, maintain the sterile field, manage the sponge and sharps counts, and support the surgeon's efficiency with well-timed, well-organized hand-offs.

Knowing the anatomy of the procedure — what the surgeon is looking at, what they are about to do, what could go wrong — is what separates a competent scrub tech from an expert one.

---

### The Closure: Closing the Field

When the operative work is complete, the team reverses the approach. Every layer that was opened is restored. The closure is not a formality — it is a critical determinant of the patient's outcome. An inadequate closure leads to dehiscence, hematoma, hernia, or infection. A thoughtful closure protects everything the surgeon accomplished.

The Closure includes:
- Wound closure layer by layer (peritoneum, fascia, subcutaneous, skin)
- Drain placement and securing
- Final count reconciliation (nothing is counted after the closure begins — the count clears the way for the closure)
- Wound dressing application
- Specimen handling and documentation
- Patient transfer from the OR table

## How This Book Is Organized

**Part I: The Approach** covers the universal principles that apply to every surgery — the OR environment, sterile technique, sterilization, instrumentation, sutures, hemostasis, anesthesia, positioning, and skin prep. These are the foundations. Without them, no procedure is possible.

**Part II: The Procedure** covers eleven surgical specialties. Each procedure within every chapter is structured identically — broken into its own Approach, Procedure, and Closure. You will see the same framework applied to a tonsillectomy, a total knee arthroplasty, a coronary bypass graft, and a cataract extraction. Different anatomy. Different instruments. Same structure.

**Part III: The Closure** covers the principles universal to all closures — wound closure technique, drains and dressings, specimen handling, and postoperative transitions.

---

## A Note on How to Read the Procedure Chapters

In Part II, every surgical procedure is presented under three subheadings:

> **The Approach** — Position, prep, specific draping, gown and gloves, the initial incision (which blade, which handle, where on the body), and the retractors used to gain and maintain exposure.

> **The Procedure** — The operative steps of the surgery, including instruments used, key anatomical landmarks, and scrub tech responsibilities at each step.

> **The Closure** — Layer-by-layer closure with specific suture selection, drain placement if applicable, dressing applied, and documentation requirements.

Read every procedure this way. When you scrub your first cholecystectomy, your first total knee, your first craniotomy — your mind should automatically move through The Approach, The Procedure, The Closure. Know what comes before the knife. Know what the knife does. Know what comes after the last stitch.

That structure is this book. It is also the structure of a surgical career.

## Surgical Conscience

Throughout this book, one concept will surface in every chapter: **surgical conscience**. It is the internalized commitment to aseptic technique and patient safety, maintained even when no one is watching, even when it is inconvenient, even when the surgeon is pressing for speed.

Surgical conscience is not taught — it is developed. This book will give you the knowledge. The conscience is yours to build.

Welcome to surgical technology.
